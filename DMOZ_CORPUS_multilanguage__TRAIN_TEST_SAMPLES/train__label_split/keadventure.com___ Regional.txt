               <div class="zen-item-body col-md-16 col-sm-16">
                <a class="zen-item-heading" href="/holidays/namibia-wildlife-botswana-etosha-okavango-safari">
                 The Great Southern African Lodge Safari
                </a>
                <span class="rating rating__right" style="width: 56px;">
                </span>
                <span class="ugc-created-by">
                 <div class="explorers-club silver-member" title="Silver Explorers Club Member">
                  <span class="icon-explorers-club">
                  </span>
                 </div>
                 roger					                					                  (newton ferrers)
                </span>
                <span class="album-item-text album-item-text__light date">
                 26 July 2018
                </span>
                <div class="zen-item-intro">
                 <p>
                  This is a "warts and all" review for the trip that started at the beginning of July, and would probably be relevant to June and August departures as well. Experiences and requirements at other times of the year
                  <span id="read-more-link-4067" style="display: inline">
                   …
                   <br/>
                   <a class="review-link" data-parent=".review-item-body" data-toggle="collapse" href="#read-more-text-4067" id="read-more-link-4067" onclick="javascript: jQuery('#read-more-text-4067').toggle(); jQuery(this).parent().toggle(); return false;">
                    Read more
                   </a>
                  </span>
                  <span class="collapse" id="read-more-text-4067">
                   may be different. This is not so much a holiday as a travel exerience. The Safari vehicles are well adapted for the trip, which is just as well considering that you will travel over 3000 miles in them, over 2000 of which will be on unsurfaced roads! Our guides, Tawanda and Jeff were great guys who worked their socks off to deliver the trip as described. For June/July/August departures remember that this is winter and there are more hours of darkness than daylight. Get used to getting up before dawn, preparing breakfast by the light of a headtorch, and preparing the evening meals the same way. Nights and early mornings were around 6 - 8 deg C, and it was not uncommon for daytime temperatures to remain in the upper teens. Note in the photos that most people are dressed in long trousers and fleeces. However, the upside of going at this time of year is that it is never oppressively hot, it's the dry season, the Okavango Delta is full of water, and there are no biting insects. In fact we rarely used any repellant, and neither of us had a single insect bite during the whole 20 day trip. The trip notes mention that some camp duties are required on this trip. This is not just for the 3 days camping in the delta, but throughout. If the trip notes mention a meal is provided then you will be preparing it, from the food provided, in teams of 3 guests with the two guides taking the lead. Once into the swing of things it's not onerous, and keeps costs down as well as providing excellent meals, and with 4 teams of 3 you are never preparing and clearing away more than one meal a day. Accommodation was excellent and exceeded our expectations throughout. Tented lodges were on raised wooden platforms and with a permanent roof covering to protect from the sun, and with ensuite shower rooms, mains electricity etc, and either large twin beds or doubles, always equipped with mosquito nets. Whilst we took sleeping bag liners, the standard of accommodation and bedding was such that we never considered using them. Accommodation was always clean and fresh and comfortable, and most had access to wifi somewhere on site although connectivity was sometimes marginal. Even the tents in the Okavango Delta had twin beds, duvets, full standing headroom and an ensuite chemical loo (for night use only). When packing, keep it light because you will be living out of your bag, and you will be doing the carrying! If trying to decide between an extra pair of shorts or an extra fleece and fleece hat, go for the extra fleece every time for June/July/August departures. Some guests brought down "puffer" jackets and had we brought ours we would have worn them some evenings and early mornings. Don't bother buying any special safari clothing because you will almost certainly already own something suitable. Most of the time you will be viewing game from a bright red safari vehicle. However, for the walking and canoeing game watching trips in the Okavango Delta you will need warm clothing in muted colours - no whites or reds. The optional trips at Livingstone and Swakopmund were expensive and we didn't bother. At Livingstone we spent a full day at Victoria Falls (hire waterproofs on site for $2, you will need them), and at Swakopmund we were ready for some "down time". The aquarium and museum are both excellent, and there are plenty of places to relax with a good book and a glass of something nice overlooking the sea and with excellent wifi. If you only do one optional trip, make it the low level flight over the Okavango Delta from Maun which is excellent value at around £100 for an hours flight. We had a young Botswanan lady pilot who took us down to below 50 feet along some of the waterways so that the giraffe were almost looking in the windows and it was one of the highlights of the entire trip and not to be missed. Travellers who have been on walking/trekking holidays may find this trip a bit sedentary. Walks are all short, none exceeding 4 miles, and by necessity there is a lot of time spent in the bus. Boots are not needed - good walking/approach/trail shoes are fine although "shortie" gaiters are useful to keep sand and grit out. Take a well stocked Kindle to read on the longer days because some of the scenery is "a bit samey". This is Africa and distances are large. The KE estimate of £350 spending money per person is possibly a bit optimistic, but do-able. We spent £400 each (plus £100 each for the Okavango flight) but ate and drank well, bought some modest gifts, and hopefully our tips for the 2 guides were reasonably generous because they were well earned. Take USD for your tips and emergencies, but for general expenditure and local currency there is a good network of ATMs which accept Visa debit/credit cards. Most lodges and restaurants accept visa/mastercard as well. Those that don't will accept USD. When you enter Zambia at Livingstone on arrival, don't forget that you need a multiple entry visa ($80) as you will need to re-enter Zambia on the final day to end up in Livingstone. Guests had booked flights on the final day leaving Livingstone at 13.00, and the guides had us at the airport by 10.30 so no panic on departure. The only disappointment was that there was only one opportunity to view game at Chobe Game Reserve on the penultimate day, and this was from a boat on the river. There is no land based game drive here. At Etosha we spent 3 nights and had 3 game drives. I would recommending reducing this to 2 nights and 2 game drives and add the extra day to Chobe to make time for a land based game drive there, and I have deducted one star for this reason. Overall though, this is a great trip, graded 4 by virtue of the long days, many spent on unsurfaced roads, but in a pair of countries over 4 times the size of the UK and a combined population of less than 5 million people, this is hardly surprising. You will see sights and experience things that you can see no other way and you will definitely come back with plenty to tell your grandchildren, so go for it! You'll not regret it.

                <img alt="Breathtaking Torres del Paine National Park" src="/media/image-cache/00413702-1238-4e0f-9ace-575c1f9d4a9a/250-210-1-2976-2108/1498037965-1495124921-1495124866-breathtaking-torres-del-paine-national-park.jpg" title="Breathtaking Torres del Paine National Park"/>
               </div>
               <div class="zen-item-body col-md-16 col-sm-16">
                <a class="zen-item-heading" href="/holiday/331#reviews-blogs">
                 Top tips for Classic Paine Circuit
                </a>
                <span class="ugc-created-by clearfix">
                 Christine
                </span>
                <div class="zen-item-intro">
                 <p>
                  The Classic Paine Circuit holiday was outstanding.  We were very lucky for 3 reasons, the guide Diego and support Jonas were excellent, the group worked extremely well and we all walked at a
                  <span id="read-more-link-3824" style="display: inline">
                   …
                   <br/>
                   <a class="review-link" data-parent=".review-item-body" data-toggle="collapse" href="#read-more-text-3824" id="read-more-link-3824" onclick="javascript: jQuery('#read-more-text-3824').toggle(); jQuery(this).parent().toggle(); return false;">
                    Read more
                   </a>
                  </span>
                  <span class="collapse" id="read-more-text-3824">
                   similar pace and the weather was kind to us.  I thought a few tips specific to this trip might be welcomed by anyone taking this trip in the future.  Baggage needed some extra planning as the domestic flight was only 15kg vs 23kg on the international and packing for the camping element of the trek was challenging as the baggage space was minimal.  But I assure you it was all fine.  These top tips assume you are familiar with KE’s pack list for the trip.   Walking gear - re-waterproof your jacket, trousers and rucksack cover and make sure your gear (including water gloves and boots) are waterproof.  The weather is varied often very windy, can be wet, sunny and friends had snow in the past. Make sure your rucksack cover is anchored to your bag. Gaiters were useful because it was muddy coming down from John Gardiner pass.  Make sure your cap or hat can be strapped down in case it’s windy. Walking Poles - useful for the river crossings and muddy downhills. One member of our party tripped on a rock and with her hands in the pole straps she couldn’t use her arms to break the fall and fell on her face which was pretty serious.  So make sure you never walk with your hands in the straps. Food &amp; Drink – excellent throughout.  No need to bring snacks.  Only need to bring 1 ltr bottle coz they give you one anyway and you can fill up regularly in the streams (guide will highlight any stream not suitable). Bars available in most refuges… beers and wines were about £8.50. At eco camp wine is included with the dinner and a pre-dinner drink and canapes provided during the briefings.  Cash &amp; Credit cards– you will need Argentinian and Chilean pesos for local expenses.  Most of us had US Dollars which we used for tips but they would have welcomed Chilean pesos.  The ATMs were temperamental so would suggest taking a couple of cards and trying the various ATMs if one doesn’t work. Baggage on the trek – as you will have read two bags are given to you at the Eco camp.  Bag 1 for the first 3 nights camping then on night 3 you get given bag 2.  These are pretty small (see photo below).  I chose to take my size 10 trainers for the evening, which took up 70% of the space.   In hindsight my boots stayed dry (despite several water crossings) and with all the campsites except offering showers and dining I could have managed with flip flops. One pair of spare bottoms for the evening is fine.  I left a clean outfit in El Calafate in the hotel and some clean clothes in the Eco lodge. Showers – I had a shower 4 nights out of 5 whilst camping.  Not always mega clean and had to queue but worth it.  Take some wet wipes for any nights you can’t get into the shower. Eco-camp tour at end – take it if you are offered.  Can be done in 20mins and very interesting.
