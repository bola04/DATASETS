              <div class="entry-header clearfix">
               <div class="entry-meta">
                <p class="edition-date">
                 Sunday, July 29th, 2018
                </p>
               </div>
               <div class="entry-title clearfix">
                <h3>
                 <a href="https://theintercept.com/2018/07/26/us-special-operations-africa-green-berets-navy-seals/" rel="bookmark" target="_blank" title="U.S. Secret Wars in Africa Rage On, Despite Talk of Downsizing">
                  U.S. Secret Wars in Africa Rage On, Despite Talk of Downsizing
                 </a>
                </h3>
               </div>
               <div class="entry-meta">
                <div class="byline">
                 <hgroup>
                  <p>
                   <span class="art-byline">
                    Nick Turse  -
                   </span>
                   The Interecept
                  </p>
                 </hgroup>
                </div>
               </div>
              </div>
              <!-- post content -->
              <div class="entry-content clearfix">
               <div class="editorial">
                <p>
                 Did you know that the United States is involved in at least eight wars on the Africa continent and that, as this article lays out, “more U.S. commandos are deployed to Africa than to any other region of the world except the Middle East.” What! You didn’t know that?
                </p>
                <p>
                 Don’t be surprised almost no one in America does. The media rarely mentions them. I wouldn’t even bet that Trump knows about them. But they affect the lives of millions of Africans and are very profitable for the military-intelligence-industrial complex.  Since the late 80s, one of America’s principal international activities has been destroying developing world societies to try and shape them into what we want. Unfortunately, it rarely ends well. Look at Iraq, Libya,  and Afghanistan.
                </p>
               </div>
               <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_40523" style="width:540px;">
                <img alt="" class=" wp-image-40523 jetpack-lazy-image" data-lazy-sizes="(max-width: 540px) 100vw, 540px" data-lazy-src="https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Nigerian-war.jpg?resize=540%2C270&amp;ssl=1" data-lazy-srcset="https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Nigerian-war.jpg?resize=300%2C150&amp;ssl=1 300w, https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Nigerian-war.jpg?resize=200%2C100&amp;ssl=1 200w, https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Nigerian-war.jpg?resize=768%2C384&amp;ssl=1 768w, https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Nigerian-war.jpg?resize=1024%2C512&amp;ssl=1 1024w, https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Nigerian-war.jpg?w=1440&amp;ssl=1 1440w" data-recalc-dims="1" height="270" src="http://www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/plugins/jetpack/modules/lazy-images/images/1x1.trans.gif" width="540"/>
                <noscript>
                 <img alt="" class=" wp-image-40523" data-recalc-dims="1" height="270" sizes="(max-width: 540px) 100vw, 540px" src="https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Nigerian-war.jpg?resize=540%2C270&amp;ssl=1" srcset="https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Nigerian-war.jpg?resize=300%2C150&amp;ssl=1 300w, https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Nigerian-war.jpg?resize=200%2C100&amp;ssl=1 200w, https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Nigerian-war.jpg?resize=768%2C384&amp;ssl=1 768w, https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Nigerian-war.jpg?resize=1024%2C512&amp;ssl=1 1024w, https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Nigerian-war.jpg?w=1440&amp;ssl=1 1440w" width="540"/>
                </noscript>
                <p class="wp-caption-text">
                 <em>
                  An American Special Forces soldier trains Nigerien troops during an exercise on the Air Base 201 compound, in Agadez, Niger, on April 14, 2018.
                 </em>
                 <br/>
                 <em>
                  Credit: The Intercept
                 </em>
                </p>
               </div>
               <p>
                Last October four U.S. soldiers – including two commandos – were killed in an ambush in Niger. Since then, talk of U.S. special operations in Africa has centered on missions being curtailed and troop levels cut.
               </p>
               <p>
                Press accounts have suggested that
                <a href="https://www.wsj.com/articles/u-s-is-reducing-presence-of-special-operations-forces-on-africas-front-lines-1523382635">
                 the number of special operators on the front lines has been reduced
                </a>
                , with the head of U.S. Special Operations forces in Africa directing his troops to
                <a href="https://www.thedailybeast.com/us-gen-reins-in-special-operations-forces-in-africa-after-niger-deaths-and-daily-beast-investigations">
                 take fewer risks
                </a>
                . At the same time, a “
                <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2018/06/04/world/africa/commandos-africa-pivot-major-powers.html">
                 sweeping Pentagon review
                </a>
                ” of special ops missions on the continent may result in drastic cuts in the number of commandos operating there. U.S. Africa Command has apparently been asked to consider the impact on counterterrorism operations of
                <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2018/06/04/world/africa/commandos-africa-pivot-major-powers.html">
                 cutting the number of Green Berets, Navy SEALs, and other commandos
                </a>
                by 25 percent over 18 months and 50 percent over three years.
               </p>
               <p>
                <a href="https://www.eurasiareview.com/21062018-the-risks-of-turning-americas-back-on-africa-analysis/">
                 Analysts
                </a>
                have already stepped forward to
                <a href="http://eng.chinamil.com.cn/view/2018-06/13/content_8061155.htm">
                 question
                </a>
                or
                <a href="https://www.voanews.com/a/us-weighs-deep-cuts-to-special-operations-forces-in-africa/4427284.html">
                 criticize
                </a>
                the proposed cuts. “Anybody that knows me knows that I would disagree with any downsizing in Africa,” Donald Bolduc, a former chief of U.S. commandos …

                  Hot Weather Spells Trouble For Nuclear Power Plants
                 </a>
                </h3>
               </div>
               <div class="entry-meta">
                <div class="byline">
                 <hgroup>
                  <p>
                   <span class="art-byline">
                    REBECCA HERSHER  -
                   </span>
                   npr
                  </p>
                 </hgroup>
                </div>
               </div>
              </div>
              <!-- post content -->
              <div class="entry-content clearfix">
               <div class="editorial">
                <p>
                 As if there were not already enough reasons to end nuclear energy power generation, we have this one brought on by climate change to consider. I recently saw on NOVA a propaganda package touting the new nuclear technologies. They were very careful not to say a word about the waste problem, nor did they say anything about this issue.
                </p>
               </div>
               <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_40529" style="width:455px;">
                <img alt="" class=" wp-image-40529 jetpack-lazy-image" data-lazy-sizes="(max-width: 455px) 100vw, 455px" data-lazy-src="https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Finland-nuclear-plant.jpg?resize=455%2C341&amp;ssl=1" data-lazy-srcset="https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Finland-nuclear-plant.jpg?resize=300%2C225&amp;ssl=1 300w, https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Finland-nuclear-plant.jpg?resize=200%2C150&amp;ssl=1 200w, https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Finland-nuclear-plant.jpg?resize=768%2C575&amp;ssl=1 768w, https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Finland-nuclear-plant.jpg?w=800&amp;ssl=1 800w" data-recalc-dims="1" height="341" src="http://www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/plugins/jetpack/modules/lazy-images/images/1x1.trans.gif" width="455"/>
                <noscript>
                 <img alt="" class=" wp-image-40529" data-recalc-dims="1" height="341" sizes="(max-width: 455px) 100vw, 455px" src="https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Finland-nuclear-plant.jpg?resize=455%2C341&amp;ssl=1" srcset="https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Finland-nuclear-plant.jpg?resize=300%2C225&amp;ssl=1 300w, https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Finland-nuclear-plant.jpg?resize=200%2C150&amp;ssl=1 200w, https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Finland-nuclear-plant.jpg?resize=768%2C575&amp;ssl=1 768w, https://i2.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Finland-nuclear-plant.jpg?w=800&amp;ssl=1 800w" width="455"/>
                </noscript>
                <p class="wp-caption-text">
                 <em>
                  The Loviisa nuclear power plant in Finland reduced its electricity output because of warmer seawater.
                 </em>
                 <br/>
                 Credit: Kimmo Mantyla/AP
                </p>
               </div>
               <p>
                Nuclear power plants in Europe have been forced to cut back electricity production because of warmer-than-usual seawater.
               </p>
               <p>
                Plants in Finland, Sweden and Germany have been affected by a
                <a href="http://www.cpc.ncep.noaa.gov/products/JAWF_Monitoring/Europe/temperature.shtml">
                 heat wave
                </a>
                that has broken records in Scandinavia and the British Isles and exacerbated
                <a href="https://www.npr.org/2018/07/24/631783839/a-terrible-day-greek-wildfires-kill-74-and-devastate-resort-village">
                 deadly wildfires
                </a>
                along the Mediterranean.
               </p>
               <p>
                Air temperatures have stubbornly lingered above 90 degrees in many parts of Sweden, Finland and Germany, and water temperatures are abnormally high — 75 degrees or higher in the usually temperate Baltic Sea.
               </p>
               <p>
                That’s bad news for nuclear power plants, which rely on seawater to cool reactors.
               </p>
               <p>
                Finland’s Loviisa power plant, located about 65 miles outside Helsinki, first slightly reduced its output on Wednesday. “The situation does not endanger people, [the] environment or the power plant,” its operator, the energy company Fortum, wrote in a
                <a href="https://www.fortum.com/media/2018/07/temporary-reduction-power-loviisa-power-plant-units-1-and-2-due-high-sea-water">
                 statement
                </a>
                .
               </p>
               <p>
                The seawater has not cooled since then, and the plant continued to reduce its output on both Thursday and Friday, confirmed the plant’s chief of operations, Timo Eurasto. “The weather forecast [means] it can continue at least a week. But hopefully not that long,” he said.
               </p>

                Federal air marshals have begun following ordinary US citizens not suspected of a crime or on any terrorist watch list and collecting extensive information about their movements and behavior under a new domestic surveillance program that is drawing criticism from within the agency.
               </p>
               <p class="story__graf">
                The previously undisclosed program, called “Quiet Skies,” specifically targets travelers who “are not under investigation by any agency and are not in the Terrorist Screening Data Base,” according to a Transportation Security Administration bulletin in March.
               </p>
               <p class="story__graf">
                The internal bulletin describes the program’s goal as thwarting threats to commercial aircraft “posed by unknown or partially known terrorists,” and gives the agency broad discretion over which air travelers to focus on and how closely they are tracked.
               </p>
               <p class="story__graf">
                But some air marshals, in interviews and internal communications shared with the Globe, say the program has them tasked with shadowing travelers who appear to pose no real threat — a businesswoman who happened to have traveled through a Mideast hot spot, in one case; a Southwest Airlines flight attendant, in another; a fellow federal law enforcement officer, in a third.
               </p>
               <p class="story__graf">
                It is a time-consuming and costly assignment, they say, which saps their ability to do more vital law …

                <h3>
                 <a href="https://www.axios.com/maternity-death-rate-child-birth-united-states-67d70b74-8549-4039-8443-62a0cb3517f9.html" rel="bookmark" target="_blank" title="U.S. tops list of most dangerous developed countries to give birth">
                  U.S. tops list of most dangerous developed countries to give birth
                 </a>
                </h3>
               </div>
               <div class="entry-meta">
                <div class="byline">
                 <hgroup>
                  <p>
                   <span class="art-byline">
                    Michael Sykes  -
                   </span>
                   AXIOS
                  </p>
                 </hgroup>

               </div>

              <!-- post content -->
              <div class="entry-content clearfix">
               <div class="editorial">
                <p>
                 I have just edited the galleys of a paper I am publishing in a week or so, “Why Doesn’t America Like Its Children?” It covers in greater length and more details, exactly the sort of thing covered in today’s lead article, whose publication I find very interesting because it tells me I’m not the only person looking at this outcome data in horror.
                </p>
                <p>
                 Compared the rest of the world’s developed nations having in a child in the U.S. is a dangerous business for both mother and baby. And the cost… The cost of delivering a child in the U.S. can be 10 times what it is Europe, and it results in 10s of thousands of families going bankrupt.  Here is a taste of the data, I will post my longer paper as soon as it is out.
                </p>
                <p>
                 What it all tells us once again is that we do not have a healthcare system in America we have an illness profit system, and we tolerate it year after year like submissive serfs.
                </p>
               </div>
               <div>
                <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_40508" style="width:499px;">
                 <img alt="" class=" wp-image-40508 jetpack-lazy-image" data-lazy-sizes="(max-width: 499px) 100vw, 499px" data-lazy-src="https://i1.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/maternity-hospital-room.jpg?resize=499%2C281&amp;ssl=1" data-lazy-srcset="https://i1.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/maternity-hospital-room.jpg?resize=300%2C169&amp;ssl=1 300w, https://i1.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/maternity-hospital-room.jpg?resize=200%2C113&amp;ssl=1 200w, https://i1.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/maternity-hospital-room.jpg?resize=768%2C432&amp;ssl=1 768w, https://i1.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/maternity-hospital-room.jpg?resize=1024%2C576&amp;ssl=1 1024w" data-recalc-dims="1" height="281" src="http://www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/plugins/jetpack/modules/lazy-images/images/1x1.trans.gif" width="499"/>

                  <img alt="" class=" wp-image-40508" data-recalc-dims="1" height="281" sizes="(max-width: 499px) 100vw, 499px" src="https://i1.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/maternity-hospital-room.jpg?resize=499%2C281&amp;ssl=1" srcset="https://i1.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/maternity-hospital-room.jpg?resize=300%2C169&amp;ssl=1 300w, https://i1.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/maternity-hospital-room.jpg?resize=200%2C113&amp;ssl=1 200w, https://i1.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/maternity-hospital-room.jpg?resize=768%2C432&amp;ssl=1 768w, https://i1.wp.com/www.schwartzreport.net/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/maternity-hospital-room.jpg?resize=1024%2C576&amp;ssl=1 1024w" width="499"/>

                   A hospital room in a maternity ward.

                   Credit: Shawn Patrick Ouellette/Portland Press Herald

                <p class="StoryBody__paragraph--2-Doz">
                 The United States is the most dangerous country in the developed world to give birth in, according to a new study by
                 <a class="StoryBody__link--10w8x" href="https://www.usatoday.com/in-depth/news/investigations/deadly-deliveries/2018/07/26/maternal-mortality-rates-preeclampsia-postpartum-hemorrhage-safety/546889002/">
                  USA Today
                 </a>
                 .
                </p>
                <p class="StoryBody__paragraph--2-Doz">
                 <strong>
                  By the numbers:
                 </strong>
                 Most women in the country give birth without incident, but nearly 50,000 are severely injured during birth annually and about 700 die. Much of it has to do with hospitals not using best practices during deliveries, the study says.
                </p>
               </div>
               <div aria-expanded="true" aria-hidden="false" class="" id="hidden-67d70b74-8549-4039-8443-62a0cb3517f9">
                <p class="StoryBody__paragraph--2-Doz">
                 <strong>
                  The details:
                 </strong>
                 Hypertension and hemorrhages are the two leading causes of childbirth death and injury and 90% of the deaths could be prevented. “USA TODAY repeatedly contacted 75 hospitals in 13 states to press for specific answers about whether they are following the
                 <a class="StoryBody__link--10w8x" href="https://safehealthcareforeverywoman.org/aim-program/">
                  [Alliance for Innovation on Maternal Health Programs]
                 </a>
                 Program’s recommended practices for hemorrhage and hypertension….Half wouldn’t answer the questions.”
                </p>
                <p class="StoryBody__paragraph--2-Doz">
                 <strong>
                  Be smart:
                 </strong>
                 This isn’t a new problem. Pro Publica reported similar statistics on
                 <a class="StoryBody__link--10w8x" href="https://www.propublica.org/article/die-in-childbirth-maternal-death-rate-health-care-system">
                  childbirth death and injury last year.
                 </a>
                </p>
                <p class="StoryBody__paragraph--2-Doz">
                 <strong>
                  The solution:
                 </strong>
                 More hospitals are advised to follow best practices and proper training protocols. Hospitals in California who follow AIM’s recommended practices have cut maternal death rates in half, but many are hesitant to adopt those measures.

                    Ilana Novick  -
                   </span>
                   truthdig
                  </p>
                 </hgroup>
                </div>
               </div>
              </div>
              <!-- post content -->
              <div class="entry-content clearfix">
               <div class="editorial">
                <p>
                 Yesterday I ran a story on voting and, today, here’s another one covering another aspect. The Republican Party aided by the Russians, yes, they are still at it, look at Senator Claire Mccaskill’s situation, are doing their best to rig the 2018 election, and they are doing it in front of our eyes. Meanwhile we are playing video games and watching sports; American democracy is in a state of extreme fragility.
                </p>

                 </a>
                </u>
                in 2013’s
                <u>
                 <a href="https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/12pdf/12-96_6k47.pdf">
                  Shelby County v. Holder
                 </a>
                </u>
                decision. He was referring to what he saw as a reduction in racial discrimination in the South and a rise in black residents’ access to voting since the passage of the 1965 Voting Rights Act. The election of the first black president and higher rates of black voting since 1965 were reason enough for him and four other justices to strike down Section 4 of the Voting Rights Act. It was a key provision that required Southern states with a history of voter discrimination to clear any changes to their voting laws with the federal government, under a process called preclearance.
               </p>
               <p>
                However, according to “
                <u>
                 <a href="https://www.brennancenter.org/sites/default/files/publications/Purges_Growing_Threat_2018.pdf">
                  Purges
                 </a>
                 : A Growing Threat to the Right to Vote
                </u>
                ,” a new report from the Brennan Center for Justice at New York University, the optimism shown in the Shelby County v. Holder decision hasn’t been borne out. This is especially true when it comes to voter purging, the process by which people are taken off the voter rolls.
               </p>
               <p>
                Voter purges are not inherently discriminatory. “When done correctly,” the report explains, “
                <u>
                 <a href="https://www.supremecourt.gov/opinions/12pdf/12-96_6k47.pdf">
                  purges ensure the
                 </a>
                </u>
