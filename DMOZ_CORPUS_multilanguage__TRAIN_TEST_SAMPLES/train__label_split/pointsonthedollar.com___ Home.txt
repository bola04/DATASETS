          <a href="http://www.pointsonthedollar.com/free-money-delivery-com-will-pay-8-week-use-platform-online-food-orders/" rel="bookmark">
           Free Money: Delivery.com Will Pay You $8 Each Week To Use Their Platform For Your Online Food Orders
          </a>
         </h2>
         <div class="post-info">
          <span class="date published time" title="2014-10-27T14:30:15+00:00">
           October 27, 2014
          </span>
          By
          <span class="author vcard">
           <span class="fn">
            <a href="http://www.pointsonthedollar.com/author/admin/" rel="author">
             David
            </a>
           </span>
          </span>
          <span class="post-comments">
           <a href="http://www.pointsonthedollar.com/free-money-delivery-com-will-pay-8-week-use-platform-online-food-orders/#respond">
            <span class="dsq-postid" data-dsqidentifier="1942 http://www.pointsonthedollar.com/?p=1942">
             Leave a Comment
            </span>
           </a>
          </span>
         </div>
         <div class="entry-content">
          <p class="p1">
           This one falls squarely into the category of not-going-to-make-you-rich-but-certainly-doesn’t-hurt: delivery.com is trying so hard to compete with Seamless/GrubHub that it’s apparently willing to give away $8 or more
           <i>
            each week
           </i>
           to lure customers to its online-ordering platform.
          </p>
          <p class="p1">
           <span class="s2">
            There’s no real magic, or even difficulty, here — just sign up for a delivery.com account, accept marketing emails (they only send one to two per week), and, like clockwork, each Saturday you’ll receive an email with a link for $8 off any order of $20 or more placed that weekend.
            <span class="Apple-converted-space">
            </span>
            I have no idea how long this marketing campaign will continue, but it has been going strong for nearly a year already: I started receiving the promotional emails back in December of 2013.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="p1">
           <a href="http://www.pointsonthedollar.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/delivery.com_.png">
            <img alt="delivery.com" class="aligncenter size-medium wp-image-1943" height="294" sizes="(max-width: 300px) 100vw, 300px" src="http://www.pointsonthedollar.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/delivery.com_-300x294.png" srcset="http://www.pointsonthedollar.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/delivery.com_-300x294.png 300w, http://www.pointsonthedollar.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/delivery.com_-1024x1006.png 1024w, http://www.pointsonthedollar.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/10/delivery.com_.png 1199w" width="300"/>
           </a>
          </p>
          <p class="p1">
           <span class="s2">
            Oh, and please excuse the shameless referral plug, but if you sign up through
            <a href="https://www.delivery.com/#!/share/593e4d" onclick="_gaq.push(['_trackEvent', 'outbound-article', 'https://www.delivery.com/#!/share/593e4d', 'this link']);">
             <span class="s1">
              this link
             </span>
            </a>
            , we’ll both get $7 once you place your first order.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="p1">
           <span class="s2">
            Unfortunately for
            <span class="s1">
             delivery.com
            </span>
            , the campaign hasn’t actually shifted my non-discounted orders away from Seamless, and it probably shouldn’t change your habits either.
            <span class="Apple-converted-space">
            </span>
            The primary reason that I stay loyal to Seamless is that Seamless transactions are coded as restaurant spend whereas
            <span class="s1">
             delivery.com
            </span>
            purchases are codes as “Misc. Personal Services,” so I’m able to take advantage of the restaurant/dining bonus category and earn extra rewards with cards that offer bonuses on these types of purchases.
            <span class="Apple-converted-space">
            </span>
            At the moment, I’m earning
            <a href="http://www.pointsonthedollar.com/td-easy-rewards-card-5-cash-back-dining-groceries-gas-cable-phone-utility-bills-6-months-plus-100/">
             <span class="s1">
              5% cash back on these purchases
             </span>
            </a>
            , but the Chase Sapphire Preferred card has been the mainstay of my Seamless account.
            <span class="Apple-converted-space">
            </span>
            The other, less significant reasons I remain a Seamless loyalist is that Seamless has a better interface and slightly more participating restaurants.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="p1">
           <span class="s2">
            Interestingly, delivery.com has its own rewards program, but it isn’t lucrative enough to compensate for the inability to capture extra credit card rewards for bonused purchases through Seamless.
            <span class="Apple-converted-space">
            </span>
            The delivery.com program is unnecessarily complicated, but suffice it to say that, at the very best, it yields about 1.47 cents of delivery.com credit per dollar spent (and, significantly, no points are earned on tax or tip); I can do better exploiting restaurant/dining bonus categories through Seamless.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p class="p1">
           <span class="s2">
            Bottom line?
            <span class="Apple-converted-space">
            </span>
            If you’re the type of person who occasionally spends more than $20 ordering food delivery on the weekends, you might as well
            <span class="s1">
             create a delivery.com account
            </span>
            and save $8 every now and then.
           </span>
          </p>
