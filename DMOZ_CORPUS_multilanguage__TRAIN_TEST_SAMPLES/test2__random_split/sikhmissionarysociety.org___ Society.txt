   <section class="sms-section article content1 sms-article-body" id="contentnanak9pub-article-body-2">
    <div class="container">
     <div class="media-container-row">
      <div class="sms-text col-12 col-md-8 sms-fonts-style display-7">
       <p>
        A one year study by S. Gurmukh Singh OBE has been
             published by the Sikh Missionary Society UK. The
             debt which humanity and India owes to Guru Tegh
             Bahadur has yet to be fully acknowledged.
       </p>
       <p>
        Guru Tegh Bahadur (1621-75 C.E.), the ninth Guru
             of the Sikhs, gave his life for the religious
             freedom of all. He saved the sub-continent of
             India from religious bigotry and thus, according
             to Bhai Gurdas II, "Stabilised the world." Today,
             the world can learn from the
        <i>
         shaheedi
        </i>
        of
             Guru Tegh Bahadur who opposed the religious
             bigotry of Emperor Aurungzeb.
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        The main reason for this publication is to produce
             a factual account for the research student and the
             mature lay reader. I am convinced that here we
             have, at least, the start of a serious study of
             Guru Tegh Bahadur's life and martyrdom - otherwise
             much distorted by
        <i>
         parcharaks
        </i>
        (traditional
             preachers) and Indian NCERT historians alike. In
             Guru Tegh Bahadur, the Sikhs have a most
             remarkable story to tell the world torn apart by
             religious conflict. It is the story of a great
             saint-martyr who gave his life for the religious
             freedom of all. He was witness to the end, to the
             founding belief of Guru Nanak's egalitarian
             ideology: that all have the fundamental human
             right to own chosen religious path to seek the
             Ultimate Reality described by numerous Names. His
             was a protest through his supreme sacrifice,
             against zealous proselytization and bigotry.
        <br/>
        (Gurmukh Singh)
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        The ebook is available at the following link -
        <a href="/sms/smspublications/20170416-Rev4-GuruTeghBahadurTheMartyr-Optimised.pdf">
         Life and the Unique Martyrdom of Guru Tegh Bahadur (1621-1675)
        </a>
       </p>
      </div>
     </div>
    </div>

   <section class="sms-section content5 sms-section-head sms-parallax-background" id="contentdelhi1984-article-h">
    <div class="sms-overlay" style="opacity: 0.4; background-color: rgb(35, 35, 35);">
    </div>
    <div class="container">
     <div class="media-container-row">
      <div class="title col-12 col-md-8">
       <h3 class="sms-section-subtitle align-center sms-light sms-white pb-3 sms-fonts-style display-5">
        Remembering Delhi Pogrom 1984
       </h3>
      </div>
     </div>
    </div>
   </section>
   <section class="sms-section article content1 sms-article-body" id="contentdelhi1984-article-body-1">
    <div class="container">
     <div class="media-container-row">
      <div class="sms-text col-12 col-md-8 sms-fonts-style display-7">
       <p>
        Sikhs worldwide remember the
             1984 pogrom in which, according to
             official figures, at least 3,000 Sikhs
             were killed by organized mobs in Delhi
             in the first 3 days of November 1984.
             Thousands of Sikhs were also killed in
             other cities of India. While the terror
             of the human slaughter within such a
             short time was horrifying, the contrived
             completeness of the failure of the
             Indian administrative system was
             inexcusable.
       </p>
      </div>
     </div>
    </div>
   </section>
   <section class="sms-section content7 sms-article-image-right-text-left" id="contentdelhi1984-article-body-2">
    <div class="container">
     <div class="media-container-row">
      <div class="col-12 col-md-8">
       <div class="media-container-row">
        <div class="media-content">
         <div class="sms-section-text sms-text align-left sms-fonts-style display-7">
          <p>
           Those killed, the widows, and their
                   children who grew up without much
                   support or succor, are the direct
                   victims of the pogrom. The world Sikh
                   community suffering from the collective
                   trauma and remembering the pogrom, is
                   the second victim. It may be argued that
                   the Indian democracy, which failed to
                   protect own citizens and continues to
                   deny justice to the victims, is the
                   third victim of this tragedy.
          </p>
         </div>
        </div>
        <div class="sms-figure" style="width: 80%;">
         <img alt="The bodies of butchered Sikhs being quickly desposed off by the Indian Government." src="/sms/smsimages/SikhsButcheredInIndia.jpg"/>
        </div>
       </div>
      </div>
     </div>
    </div>
   </section>
   <section class="sms-section article content1 sms-article-body" id="contentdelhi1984-article-body-3">
    <div class="container">
     <div class="media-container-row">
      <div class="sms-text col-12 col-md-8 sms-fonts-style display-7">
       <p>
        Pogroms, genocides and human tragedies,
             should unite all right thinking,
             fair-minded people above communal and
             religio-ethnic divides so that lessons
             are learnt, and history does not repeat
             itself. The politics of forgetfulness
             must not be allowed to suppress the
             traditional Sikhi spirit of remembrance
             expressed in the daily Ardaas
             (supplication).
       </p>
       <p>
        In an ever shrinking world, no one can
             remain immune from large scale selective
             massacre of one community and prolonged
             delay in the delivery of justice. We
             remember those who lost their lives in
             the Sikh genocide of November 1984 and
             their families who continue to be denied
             justice to this day.
       </p>

       </h3>
      </div>
     </div>
    </div>
   </section>
   <section class="sms-section article content1 sms-article-body" id="contentdelhi1984-article-body-1">
    <div class="container">
     <div class="media-container-row">
      <div class="sms-text col-12 col-md-8 sms-fonts-style display-7">
       <p>
        Achieving this Guidance on the wearing of Sikh
             Articles of Faith in the workplace and
             public spaces by the Equalities and Human
             Rights Commission (EHRC) is an important
             step forward in recognition of the Sikh
             religious identity in the UK. The Sikh
             Missionary Society UK was represented by
             Gurmukh Singh (UK) in the drafting of the
             Equality and Human Rights Commission
             guidelines.
       </p>
      </div>
     </div>
    </div>
   </section>
   <section class="sms-section content7 sms-article-image-right-text-left" id="contentdelhi1984-article-body-2">
    <div class="container">
     <div class="media-container-row">
      <div class="col-12 col-md-8">
       <div class="media-container-row">
        <div class="media-content">
         <div class="sms-section-text sms-text align-left sms-fonts-style display-7">
          <p>
           You should read this guidance if you require:
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li>
            clarification on how
                     the law currently applies to the
                     wearing of Sikh articles of faith
           </li>
           <li>
            examples of best
                     practice in dealing sensitively and
                     fairly with observers of the Sikh
                     faith
           </li>
           <li>
            a tool to strengthen
                     good relations by promoting greater
                     understanding between Sikhs and others
           </li>
           <li>
            a guide for private
                     and public sector organisations in
                     terms of dignity and fairness at work,
                     and service delivery with regards to
                     the Sikh community, and in promoting
                     good relations, and
           </li>
           <li>
            links to other
                     guidance on this topic
           </li>
          </ul>
