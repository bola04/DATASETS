       <h1>
        <a href="http://medialoper.com/certain-songs-1277-neil-young-crazy-horse-crime-in-the-city-pittsburgh-02-17-1991/" title="Certain Songs #1277: Neil Young &amp; Crazy Horse – “Crime in the City (Pittsburgh 02-17-1991)”">
         Certain Songs #1277: Neil Young &amp; Crazy Horse – “Crime in the City (Pittsburgh 02-17-1991)”
        </a>
       </h1>
       <div class="postMeta">
        <p class="post_category">
         Posted by
         <a href="http://medialoper.com/author/jim/" rel="author" title="Posts by Jim Connelly">
          Jim Connelly
         </a>
         in
         <a href="http://medialoper.com/category/certain-songs/" rel="category tag">
          Certain Songs
         </a>
         on  Jul 29, 2018
        </p>
       </div>
       <p>
        <a href="http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Weld.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-18364" height="200" sizes="(max-width: 200px) 100vw, 200px" src="http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Weld-300x300.jpg" srcset="http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Weld-300x300.jpg 300w, http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Weld-150x150.jpg 150w, http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Weld.jpg 600w" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <em>
         Album:
         <strong>
          Arc-Weld
         </strong>
         <br/>
         Year: 1991
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Recorded at the Civic Auditorium, Pittsburgh, on February 17, 1991
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        I don’t know if it’s still available this way, but when Neil Young &amp; Crazy Horse’s second live album,
        <strong>
         Weld
        </strong>
        , came out during the insanely overstuffed with great music autumn of 1991, there was an extra disc attached to it called
        <strong>
         Arc
        </strong>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        And one of the coolest things about the packaging of
        <strong>
         Arc-Weld
        </strong>
        all of the pictures accompanying the CD, Neil Young looks totally possessed.  Check the cover and he’s out of his head playing his guitar while the rest of Crazy Horse are deep into a groove.  On the back cover, Neil is kissing his guitar, thanking it for all of the insane noises that he was able to coax out of it that evening.
       </p>
       <p>

         <span aria-label="Continue reading Certain Songs #1277: Neil Young &amp; Crazy Horse – “Crime in the City (Pittsburgh 02-17-1991)”">
          (more…)
         </span>
        </a>
       </p>

         Certain Songs #1277: Neil Young &amp; Crazy Horse – “Over and Over”
        </a>
       </h1>
       <div class="postMeta">
        <p class="post_category">
         Posted by
         <a href="http://medialoper.com/author/jim/" rel="author" title="Posts by Jim Connelly">
          Jim Connelly
         </a>
         in
         <a href="http://medialoper.com/category/certain-songs/" rel="category tag">
          Certain Songs
         </a>
         on  Jul 28, 2018
        </p>
       </div>
       <p>
        <a href="http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Ragged-Glory.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-18310" height="200" sizes="(max-width: 200px) 100vw, 200px" src="http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Ragged-Glory-300x300.jpg" srcset="http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Ragged-Glory-300x300.jpg 300w, http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Ragged-Glory-150x150.jpg 150w, http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Ragged-Glory.jpg 500w" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <em>
         Album:
         <strong>
          Ragged Glory
         </strong>
         <br/>
         Year: 1990
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Recorded at Plywood Digital, Broken Arrow Ranch on April 27, 1990
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        But the best track on
        <strong>
         Ragged Glory
        </strong>
        kinda slipped in under the radar.
       </p>
       <p>
        Unlike “Love To Burn” and “Love and Only Love,” “Over and Over” was under 10 minutes long; unlike “
        <a href="http://medialoper.com/certain-songs-1276-neil-young-crazy-horse-fin-up/">
         Fuckin’ Up
        </a>
        ” and “Mansion on the Hill” it didn’t have an instantly-memorable chorus; and unlike all of the above (plus “Country Home” &amp; “Days That Used To Be”) it wasn’t played on the
        <strong>
         Ragged Glory
        </strong>
        tour, and apparently not played live ever until 2012.
       </p>
       <p>
        <a class="more-link" href="http://medialoper.com/certain-songs-1277-neil-young-crazy-horse-over-and-over/#more-18358">

So when Neil decided he wanted to do another Crazy Horse album in early 1990, they all met at the Harris Ranch Restaurant on the I-5 to discuss the possibility of recording together again.
       </p>
       <p>
        Which, of course, they did. I mean, if your choice is recording with Neil Young or not recording with Neil Young, it really isn’t much of a choice, tbh.

       <h1>
        <a href="http://medialoper.com/certain-songs-1274-neil-young-rockin-in-the-free-world/" title="Certain Songs #1274: Neil Young – “Rockin’ in the Free World”">
         Certain Songs #1274: Neil Young – “Rockin’ in the Free World”
        </a>
       </h1>
       <div class="postMeta">
        <p class="post_category">
         Posted by
         <a href="http://medialoper.com/author/jim/" rel="author" title="Posts by Jim Connelly">
          Jim Connelly
         </a>
         in
         <a href="http://medialoper.com/category/certain-songs/" rel="category tag">
          Certain Songs
         </a>
         on  Jul 25, 2018
        </p>
       </div>
       <p>
        <a href="http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Rockin-in-the-Free-World.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-18297" height="187" src="http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Rockin-in-the-Free-World-300x297.jpg" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <em>
         <strong>
          Album: Freedom
         </strong>
         <br/>
         Year: 1989
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Recorded at Plywood Digital, Broken Arrow Ranch on March 10, 1989
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        There were a lot of warning signs that
        <strong>
         Freedom
        </strong>
        was going to be a major album for Neil Young: not only was it was his first album with his hand-scrawled title since
        <strong>
         Rust Never Sleeps
        </strong>
        , it was only the third (and final, as of this writing) of his albums with different versions of the same song opening and closing the album.
       </p>
       <p>
        But for those of us who wanted, needed, craved a great new Neil Young album in our lives, what really clinched it was his performance on
        <strong>
         Saturday Night Live
        </strong>
        the weekend before the album came out. While I’m sure that there was early buzz on the album, and the song called “Rockin’ in the Free World” that bookended it, there was no way we could have been prepared for what the song actually was.
       </p>
       <p>
        <a class="more-link" href="http://medialoper.com/certain-songs-1274-neil-young-rockin-in-the-free-world/#more-18296">
         <span aria-label="Continue reading Certain Songs #1274: Neil Young – “Rockin’ in the Free World”">
          (more…)
         </span>
        </a>
       </p>
       <div class="fix">
       </div>

       <h2>
        <a href="http://medialoper.com/certain-songs-1277-neil-young-crazy-horse-crime-in-the-city-pittsburgh-02-17-1991-2/">
         The Daily Loper
        </a>
       </h2>
       <p>
        <a href="http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Weld.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-18364" height="200" sizes="(max-width: 200px) 100vw, 200px" src="http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Weld-300x300.jpg" srcset="http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Weld-300x300.jpg 300w, http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Weld-150x150.jpg 150w, http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Weld.jpg 600w" width="200"/>
        </a>
        <em>
         Album:
         <strong>
          Arc-Weld
         </strong>
         <br/>
         Year: 1991
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        <em>
         Recorded at the Civic Auditorium, Pittsburgh, on February 17, 1991
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        I don’t know if it’s still available this way, but when Neil Young &amp; Crazy Horse’s second live album,
        <strong>
         Weld
        </strong>
        , came out during the insanely overstuffed with great music autumn of 1991, there was an extra disc attached to it called
        <strong>
         Arc
        </strong>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        And one of the coolest things about the packaging of
        <strong>
         Arc-Weld
        </strong>
        all of the pictures accompanying the CD, Neil Young looks totally possessed.  Check the cover and he’s out of his head playing his guitar while the rest of Crazy Horse are deep into a groove.  On the back cover, Neil is kissing his guitar, thanking it for all of the insane noises that he was able to coax out of it that evening.
       </p>
       <p>
        <span>
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        But it’s the cover of the
        <strong>
         Arc
        </strong>
        disc that I love the most.
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Arc.jpg">
         <img alt="" class="alignright size-medium wp-image-18374" height="198" sizes="(max-width: 200px) 100vw, 200px" src="http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Arc-300x296.jpg" srcset="http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Arc-300x296.jpg 300w, http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Arc-150x150.jpg 150w, http://media.medialoper.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/Neil-Young-Arc.jpg 599w" width="200"/>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        It’s Neil in front of one of the giant prop amps they brought back out for this tour, guitar slung behind him, face waxy with effort &amp; sweat, holding up his hands like the aliens in their silver spaceships had just entered the arena and he’s trying to wave them off because the show isn’t over yet.
       </p>
       <p>
        The
        <strong>
         Arc
        </strong>
        disc, BTW, is essentially a half-hour of feedback and lyrical snippets, and the best use I ever found for it was that Halloween — the week after the album came out — when I stuck my speakers in the window of Tower District apt on Linden, and put it on repeat to freak out all of the trick-or-treaters while I went out for the evening.
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         <em>
          Well, the cop made the showdown
          <br/>
          He was sure he was right
          <br/>
          He had all of the lowdown
          <br/>
          From the bank heist last night
          <br/>
          His best friend was the robber
          <br/>
          And his wife was a thief
          <br/>
          All the children were killers
          <br/>
          They couldn’t get no relief
          <br/>
          The bungalow was surrounded
          <br/>
          When a voice loud and clear
          <br/>
          Said, “Come on out
          <br/>
          with your hands up
          <br/>
          Or we’ll blow you out of here”
          <br/>
          There was a face in the window
          <br/>
          The TV cameras rolled
          <br/>
          Then they cut to the announcer
          <br/>
          And the story was told
         </em>
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        And the packaging matched the music inside: whereas
        <strong>
         Live Rust
        </strong>
        was designed to be a capstone to one of the best decades any musician had ever had,
        <strong>
         Weld
        </strong>
        was more like a crazed, cranked celebration of both a muse rediscovered and a fellowship that was perhaps on shakier ground than it seemed.
       </p>
       <p>
        And so while
        <strong>
         Weld
        </strong>
        reprised every song from the electric disc of
        <strong>
         Live Rust
        </strong>
        , every version was hoarser, rawer and more desperate than it had been a dozen years prior; an ethos that also permeated the
        <strong>
         Freedom
        </strong>
        &amp;
        <strong>
         Ragged Glory
        </strong>
        songs that made up most of the rest of the album.  Especially “Crime in the City.”
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         <em>
          There’s still crime in the city,
          <br/>
          Said the cop on the beat,
          <br/>
          I don’t know if I can stop it
          <br/>
          I feel like meat on the street
          <br/>
          They paint my car like a target
          <br/>
          I take my orders from fools
          <br/>
          Meanwhile some punk
          <br/>
          Blows my head off
          <br/>
          When I play by their rules
          <br/>
          That’s why I’m doin’ it my way
          <br/>
          I took the law in my hands
          <br/>
          So here I am in the alleyway
          <br/>
          A wad of cash in my pants
          <br/>
          I get paid by a ten year old
          <br/>
          He says he looks up to me
          <br/>
          There’s still crime in the city
          <br/>
          But it’s good to be free
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         Freeedommmmmmmm!
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        “Crime in the City” was one of the songs that featured in the run-up to the release of
        <strong>
         Freedom
        </strong>
        , I definitely remember reading about it — apparently there were like a zillion verses — prior to hearing it.  And while the album version was fine — recorded with the Bluenotes — it was also low-key and mostly acoustic, despite the fact that even with the Bluenotes, Neil was playing a noisy version in concert.
       </p>
       <p>
        But on
        <strong>
         Ragged Glory
        </strong>
        , “Crime in the City” was totally and utterly cranked, Neil anchoring the song with a melodic guitar hook that appeared and disappeared at will, Crazy Horse choogling throughout.  On
        <strong>
         Freedom
        </strong>
        , “Crime in the City” was delicate and dynamic, but live it’s one screaming howl of despair, especially after the second verse, when Neil screams “Freedommmmmmmmmm!” like he’s pretty sure he’s never going to find any.
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         <em>
          Now I come from a family
          <br/>
          That has a broken home
          <br/>
          Sometimes I talk to Daddy
          <br/>
          On the telephone
          <br/>
          When he says that he loves me
          <br/>
          I know that he does
          <br/>
          But I wish I could see him
          <br/>
          I wish I knew where he was
          <br/>
          But that’s the way
          <br/>
          All my friends are
          <br/>
          Except maybe one or two
          <br/>
          Wish I could
          <br/>
          see him this weekend
          <br/>
          Wish I could walk in his shoes
          <br/>
          But now I’m doin’ my own thing
          <br/>
          Sometimes I’m good, then I’m bad
          <br/>
          Although my home has been broken
          <br/>
          It’s the best home I ever had
          <br/>
          Yeah
         </em>
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        One of Neil’s more Dylanesque songs — it’s not a coincidence that its followed on
        <strong>
         Weld
        </strong>
        by their Gulf War hymnal version of “Blowin’ in the Wind” — there are at least three verses missing from this utterly cranked “Crime in the City” which showed up on other versions.  That said, they all come to a head in the batshit crazy final verse.
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         <em>
          Well, I keep gettin’ younger
          <br/>
          My life’s been funny that way
          <br/>
          Before I ever learned to talk
          <br/>
          I forgot what to say
          <br/>
          I sassed back to my mom
          <br/>
          I sassed back to my teacher
          <br/>
          I got thrown out of Bible school
          <br/>
          For giving the finger to the preacher
          <br/>
          Then I grew up to be a fireman
          <br/>
          Put out every fire in town
          <br/>
          Put out anything smokin’
          <br/>
          But when I put the hose down
          <br/>
          The judge sent me to prison
          <br/>
          He gave me life without parole
          <br/>
          Wish I never put the hose down
          <br/>
          Wish I never got old
          <br/>
          Wish I never got old
          <br/>
          Old
          <br/>
          Ollllld
         </em>
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        I love the way his voice breaks on the word “old.” Like he can’t quite bring himself to believe that he’s actually growing old. In February 1991, Neil Young was 45 years old, which seemed old to my 28-year-old self, but less old than 34-year-old Neil seemed when I discovered him at 17. And what seemed more important was the fact that he was still kicking out the jams at this level.
       </p>
       <p>
        A quarter-century later, in the twilight of the gods, I kinda wish that all of my favorite rock stars were still 45, but of course that’s not how it works, is it?
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         “Crime in the City” 2-1989 (Weld version)
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         “Crime in the City” 7-1988 (Freedom version) (Audio only)
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         “Crime in the City” 8-1988 (Electric Bluenotes live version) (Audio only)
        </strong>
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         “Crime in the City” live with the Bluenotes 8-1988 (18 minute version)
        </strong>
