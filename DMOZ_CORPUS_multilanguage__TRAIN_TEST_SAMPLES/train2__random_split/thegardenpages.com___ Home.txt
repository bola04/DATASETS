        <h3 align="center">
         Minimize Water Rationing Woes
        </h3>
        <p style="text-align: center;">
         Here are a few tips to
         <a href="http://thegardenpages.com/gardening-tips/water-graywater/">
          help save water in the garden
         </a>
         and maybe a few of your favorite flowers!
        </p>
        <h2 style="text-align: center;">
         More Quick Gardening Tips:
        </h2>
        <h3 style="text-align: center;">
         New Plant Transplanting Tips or How to Keep Your Plants From Frying in a Heat Wave
        </h3>
        <p>
         Give your new transplants some
         <b>
          shade
         </b>
         for the first few days after planting.  It helps them adjust to their new home and reduces transplant stress.
         <i>
          This info also works for plants that need emergency shade during 100 degree weather.
         </i>
        </p>
        <p>
         <b>
          <i>
           Be creative:
          </i>
         </b>
         Try shading with a small umbrella (great way to recycle old umbrellas), a garden banner or flag, an old t-shirt or a small piece of shade cloth.  You can hang fabric over tomato cages, old wire fencing or a cool branch left over from your last pruning project.  I suppose you could actually build a
         <i>
          real
         </i>
         frame and staple on the shade cloth too.
        </p>
        <p>
         Try not to do planting during the heat of the day – it just wears everyone out!  In warmest areas it it best to give new transplants a little water every day for the first two or three days.  Resist the temptation to give them too much water or they will get rot.
        </p>
        <div align="center">
         <h3>
          <span style="font-family: Tahoma;">
           Quick Tomato Tip
          </span>
         </h3>
        </div>
        <p>
         <span style="font-family: Tahoma;">
          Tomato plants prefer acid soil. Adding used
          <b>
           coffee grounds
          </b>
          helps increase the acidity of the soil.
          <b>
           Pickle juice
          </b>
          and
          <b>
           tea
          </b>
          also increase soil acidity.  Other shrubs that appreciate a cup of java are camellia, azalea, gardenia and roses.
         </span>
        </p>
        <h3 align="center">
         <span style="color: #003300; font-family: Times;">
          Keeping Sweet Basil Fresh
         </span>
        </h3>
        <div align="center">
         <span style="color: #003300; font-family: Times;">
          <strong>
           <span style="color: #003300; font-family: Times;">
            Get ready for Pesto!
           </span>
          </strong>
          Sweet Basil herb cuttings will stay fresh for weeks in a clean glass of water.  Keep your herb sprigs on the kitchen counter out of direct sunlight. Change your sweet basil water every few days. Basil will eventually form roots in the water so you can make more plants!
          <br/>
         </span>
        </div>
        <p>
        </p>
        <div align="center">
         <h3>
          <span style="font-family: Arial;">
           Dealing with Mosquitoes
           <br/>
          </span>
         </h3>
        </div>
        <p>
         <span style="font-family: Arial;">
          Mosquitoes are becoming a problem in areas where they carry the deadly West Nile virus.  Agencies can’t inspect every single inch so they need your help.  Abandoned pools in foreclosed homes have become a large problem in some areas.  Do yourself a favor and keep an eye out.
         </span>
        </p>
        <p>
         <b>
          Simple, organic, biological mosquito control:
         </b>
         A few guppies (mosquitofish or
         <i>
          Gambusia affinis)
         </i>
         will gobble up the problem and say thanks!  Read more on the
         <a href="http://www.lawestvector.org/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
          Los Angeles Vector Control Site…
         </a>
        </p>
        <h3 style="text-align: center;">
         What’s My USDA Planting Zone?
        </h3>
        <p>
         In order to help gardeners determine the best times and plants for their area, The United States Department of Agriculture zone map shows in detail the lowest temperatures that can be expected each year in the United States, Canada, and Mexico.  The map shows different zones, each of which represents an area of winter hardiness for the plants of agriculture and our natural landscape.  Their site also gives planting, growing recommendations and cold hardiness for your area.
        </p>
        <p>
         Use this handy link to the
         <b>
          <a href="http://www.usna.usda.gov/Hardzone/ushzmap.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
           US Arboretum’s Online USDA Zone Map
          </a>
         </b>
        </p>
        <h3 style="text-align: center;">
         Yeah, but what’s my USDA Planting Zone if I’m in
         <i>
          Australia
         </i>
         ?
        </h3>
        <p>
         We share the same climate, (and a totally awesome surf culture) so why not swap gardening
         <br/>
         tips over the digital back fence?  In gratitude to the visitors I get from Australia, I thought it would be neighborly of me to help sort out all this crazy Planting Zone business.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="https://www.anbg.gov.au/gardens/research/hort.research/zones.html" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
          Plant Hardiness Zones for Australia
         </a>
         , by Iain Dawson (Horticultural Research Unit, ANBG) 1991 (Iain has since retired from the Australian National Botanic Gardens)   Our thanks to
         <b>
          Iain Dawson
         </b>
         (Horticultural Research Unit, ANBG).
