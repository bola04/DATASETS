     <section class="background-white" id="sp-research-section">
      <div class="container">
       <div class="row">
        <div class="col-sm-12 col-md-12" id="sp-research">
         <div class="sp-column ">
          <div class="sp-module ">
           <div class="sp-module-content">
            <div class="custom">
             <!-- START: Articles Anywhere -->
             <div class="container d-flex flex-column pf-app-wrapper justify-content-start">
              <div class="d-flex flex-row pf-app-row flex-auto">
               <div class="pf-app-inner d-flex flex-column pf-app-shadow">
                <div class="browser pf-content-wrapper pf-content-scroll px-2 px-sm-4 px-md-5 pt-2 pt-sm-3" id="pf-body">
                 <div class="pf-12 print-only" id="printfriendly">
                  <div class="clearfix js-print-area content-unmask direction-ltr" id="pf-print-area">
                   <div class="js-pf-content pf-12" id="pf-content">
                    <div class="">
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      <b class="">
                       Back pain
                      </b>
                      is pain felt in the back. It is divided into neck pain (cervical), middle back pain (thoracic), lower back pain (lumbar) or coccydynia (tailbone or sacral pain) based on the segment affected.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origin_1-0">
                       [1]
                      </sup>
                      The lumbar area is the most common area for pain, as it supports most of the weight in the upper body.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_2-0">
                       [2]
                      </sup>
                      Episodes of back pain may be acute, sub-acute, or chronic depending on the duration. The pain may be characterized as a dull ache, shooting or piercing pain, or a burning sensation. Discomfort can radiate into the arms and hands as well as the legs or feet, and may include numbness,
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-origin_1-1">
                       [1]
                      </sup>
                      or weakness in the legs and arms.
                     </p>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      Back pain can originate from the muscles, nerves, bones, joints or other structures in the spine. Internal structures such as the gallbladder, pancreas, aorta, and kidneys may also cause referred pain in the back.
                     </p>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      Back pain is common, with about nine out of ten adults experiencing it at some point in their life, and five out of ten working adults having it every year.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AAFP_3-0">
                       [3]
                      </sup>
                      Some estimate up to 95% of Americans will experience back pain at some point in their lifetime.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_2-1">
                       [2]
                      </sup>
                      It is the most common cause of chronic pain, and is a major contributor of missed work and disability.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_2-2">
                       [2]
                      </sup>
                      However, it is rare for back pain to be permanently disabling. In most cases of herniated disks and stenosis, rest, injections or surgery have similar general pain resolution outcomes on average after one year. In the United States, acute low back pain is the fifth most common reason for physician visits and causes 40% of missed days off work.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_4-0">
                       [4]
                      </sup>
                      Additionally, it is the single leading cause of disability worldwide.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2010_Global_Burden_of_Disease_Study_5-0">
                       [5]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <h2 class="">
                      <span class="mw-headline" id="Classification">
                       Classification
                      </span>
                     </h2>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      Back pain may be classified by various methods to aid its diagnosis and management. The duration of back pain is considered in three categories, following the expected pattern of healing of connective tissue. Acute pain lasts up to 12 weeks, subacute pain refers to the second half of the acute period (6 to 12 weeks), and chronic pain is pain which persists beyond 12 weeks.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6">
                       [6]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <h2 class="">
                      <span class="mw-headline" id="Causes">
                       Causes
                      </span>
                     </h2>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      In as many as 85% of cases, no physiological cause can be found.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7">
                       [7]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      There are many causes of back pain, including blood vessels, internal organs, infections, mechanical, and autoimmune causes.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_8-0">
                       [8]
                      </sup>
                      The spinal cord, nerve roots, vertebral column, and muscles around the spine can all be sources of back pain.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_8-1">
                       [8]
                      </sup>
                      The anterior ligaments of the intervertebral disc are extremely sensitive, and even the slightest injury can cause significant pain.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9">
                       [9]
                      </sup>
                      In osteoporosis, the bones become weaker and can develop small cracks, or fractures, in the bones, resulting in pain.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_10-0">
                       [10]
                      </sup>
                      Arthritis in the joints of the back can also result in discomfort.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:4_10-1">
                       [10]
                      </sup>
                      The synovial joints of the spine (e.g. zygapophysial joints/facet joints) have been identified as the primary source of the pain in approximately one third of people with chronic low back pain, and in most people with neck pain following whiplash.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bogduk_2005_11-0">
                       [11]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <div class="thumb tright">
                      <div class="thumbinner pf-caption flex-width mediumImage pf-size-full">
                       <img alt="" class="thumbimage caption-img mediumImage" data-file-height="1024" data-file-width="768" src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/Discogenic_Pain.png/220px-Discogenic_Pain.png" srcset="" width="220"/>
                       <p class="thumbcaption added-to-list1" id="">
                        Back pain can be caused by the vertebrae compressing the intervertebral discs.
                       </p>
                      </div>
                     </div>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      Approximately 98 percent of people with back pain are diagnosed with nonspecific acute back pain in which there is no serious underlying pathology.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12">
                       [12]
                      </sup>
                      Less than 2 percent are attributed to secondary factors, with metastatic cancers and serious infections, such as spinal osteomyelitis and epidural abscesses, accounting for around 1 percent.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13">
                       [13]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      Back pain can be divided into non-radicular pain and radiculopathy. Radiculopathy occurs when there is irritation in the nerve root, causing neurologic symptoms, such as numbness and tingling. Disk herniation and foraminal stenosis are the most common causes of radiculopathy.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_8-2">
                       [8]
                      </sup>
                      Non-radicular back pain is most commonly caused by injury to the spinal muscles or ligaments, degenerative spinal disease, or a herniated disk.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_8-3">
                       [8]
                      </sup>
                      Spondylosis, or spinal degeneration, occurs when the intervertebral disc undergoes degenerative changes, causing the disc to fail at cushioning the vertebrae. The space between the vertebrae becomes more narrow, resulting in compression and irritation of the nerves.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14">
                       [14]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      Back pain can also be due to referred pain from another source. Referred pain occurs when pain is felt at a location different from the source of the pain. An abdominal aortic aneurysmand ureteral colic can both result in pain felt in the back.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_8-4">
                       [8]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      Another possible cause of chronic back pain in people with otherwise normal scans is central sensitization, where an initial injury or infection causes a longer-lasting state of heightened sensitivity to pain. This persistent state maintains pain even after the initial injury has healed.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15">
                       [15]
                      </sup>
                      Treatment of sensitization typically involves low doses of anti-depressants.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16">
                       [16]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <h3>
                      <span class="mw-headline" id="Risk_factors">
                       Risk factors
                      </span>
                     </h3>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      Obesity, sedentary lifestyle, and lack of exercise can increase a person's risk of back pain.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_2-3">
                       [2]
                      </sup>
                      People who smoke are more likely to experience back pain than others.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17">
                       [17]
                      </sup>
                      Poor posture and weight gain in pregnancy are also risk factors for back pain. In general, fatigue can worsen pain.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_2-4">
                       [2]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      A few studies suggest that psychosocial factors such as on-the-job stress and dysfunctional family relationships may correlate more closely with back pain than structural abnormalities revealed in X-rays and other medical imaging scans.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18">
                       [18]
                      </sup>
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19">
                       [19]
                      </sup>
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20">
                       [20]
                      </sup>
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21">
                       [21]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <h2>
                      <span class="mw-headline" id="Diagnosis">
                       Diagnosis
                      </span>
                     </h2>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      In most cases of low back pain, medical consensus advises not seeking an exact diagnosis but instead beginning to treat the pain.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHSfive2_22-0">
                       [22]
                      </sup>
                      This assumes that there is no reason to expect that the person has an underlying problem.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHSfive2_22-1">
                       [22]
                      </sup>
                      In most cases, the pain goes away naturally after a few weeks.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHSfive2_22-2">
                       [22]
                      </sup>
                      Typically, people who do seek diagnosis through imaging are not likely to have a better outcome than those who wait for the condition to resolve.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AHSfive2_22-3">
                       [22]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      Laboratory testing may include white blood cell (WBC) count, erythrocyte sedimentation rate (ESR), and C-reactive protein (CRP).
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_8-5">
                       [8]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <ul class="added-to-list1">
                      <li>
                       Elevated ESR could indicate infection, malignancy, chronic disease, inflammation, trauma, or tissue ischemia.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_8-6">
                        [8]
                       </sup>
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       Elevated CRP levels are associated with infection.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_8-7">
                        [8]
                       </sup>
                      </li>
                     </ul>
                     <div class="thumb tright">
                      <div class="thumbinner pf-caption flex-width mediumImage pf-size-full">
                       <img alt="" class="thumbimage caption-img mediumImage" data-file-height="456" data-file-width="738" src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Ossifikation_Ligamentum_longitudinale_posterius_BWS_-_CT_axial_und_sagittal.jpg/220px-Ossifikation_Ligamentum_longitudinale_posterius_BWS_-_CT_axial_und_sagittal.jpg" srcset="" width="220"/>
                       <p class="thumbcaption added-to-list1" id="">
                        CT scan of the spine, showing calcification of the longitudinal posterior ligament.
                       </p>
                      </div>
                     </div>
                     <h3>
                      <span class="mw-headline" id="Red_flags">
                       Red flags
                      </span>
                     </h3>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      Imaging is not typically needed in the initial diagnosis or treatment of back pain. However, if there are certain "red flag" symptoms present plain radiographs (x-ray), CT scan, or magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) may be recommended. These red flags include:
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23">
                       [23]
                      </sup>
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_8-8">
                       [8]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <ul class="added-to-list1">
                      <li>
                       History of cancer
                      </li>
                      <li class="">
                       Unexplained weight loss
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       Immunosuppression
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       Urinary infection
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       Intravenous drug use
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       Prolonged use of corticosteroids
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       Back pain not improved with conservative management
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       History of significant trauma
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       Minor fall or heavy lift in a potentially osteoporotic or elderly individual
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       Acute onset of urinary retention, overflow incontinence, loss of anal sphincter tone, or fecal incontinence
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       Saddle anesthesia
                      </li>
                      <li class="">
                       Global or progressive motor weakness in the lower limbs
                      </li>
                     </ul>
                     <h2 class="">
                      <span class="mw-headline" id="Prevention">
                       Prevention
                      </span>
                     </h2>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      There is moderate quality evidence that suggests the combination of education and exercise may reduce an individual's risk of developing an episode of low back pain.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steffens2016_24-0">
                       [24]
                      </sup>
                      Lesser quality evidence points to exercise alone as a possible deterrent to the risk of the onset of this condition.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Steffens2016_24-1">
                       [24]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <h2>
                      <span class="mw-headline" id="Management">
                       Management
                      </span>
                     </h2>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      The management goals when treating back pain are to achieve maximal reduction in pain intensity as rapidly as possible, to restore the individual's ability to function in everyday activities, to help the patient cope with residual pain, to assess for side-effects of therapy, and to facilitate the patient's passage through the legal and socioeconomic impediments to recovery. For many, the goal is to keep the pain to a manageable level to progress with rehabilitation, which then can lead to long-term pain relief. Also, for some people the goal is to use non-surgical therapies to manage the pain and avoid major surgery, while for others surgery may be the quickest way to feel better.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25">
                       [25]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      Not all treatments work for all conditions or for all individuals with the same condition, and many find that they need to try several treatment options to determine what works best for them. The present stage of the condition (acute or chronic) is also a determining factor in the choice of treatment. Only a minority of people with back pain (most estimates are 1% - 10%) require surgery.
                     </p>
                     <h3>
                      <span class="mw-headline" id="Non_medical">
                       Non medical
                      </span>
                     </h3>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      Back pain is generally treated with non-pharmacological therapy first, as it typically resolves without the use of medication. Superficial heat and massage, acupuncture, and spinal manipulation therapy may be recommended.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_26-0">
                       [26]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <ul class="added-to-list1">
                      <li>
                       Heat therapy is useful for back spasms or other conditions. A review concluded that heat therapy can reduce symptoms of acute and sub-acute low-back pain.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid166417762_27-0">
                        [27]
                       </sup>
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       Regular activity and gentle stretching exercises is encouraged in uncomplicated back pain, and is associated with better long-term outcomes.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_8-9">
                        [8]
                       </sup>
                       Physical therapy to strengthen the muscles in the abdomen and around the spine may also be recommended.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_28-0">
                        [28]
                       </sup>
                       These exercises are associated with better patient satisfaction, although it has not been shown to provide functional improvement.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_8-10">
                        [8]
                       </sup>
                       However, one study found that exercise is effective for chronic back pain, but not for acute pain. If used, they should be performed under supervision of a licensed health professional.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid160348512_29-0">
                        [29]
                       </sup>
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       Massage therapy may give short-term pain relief, but not functional improvement, for those with acute lower back pain.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Furlan20152_30-0">
                        [30]
                       </sup>
                       It may also give short-term pain relief and functional improvement for those with long-term (chronic) and sub-acute lower pack pain, but this benefit does not appear to be sustained after 6 months of treatment.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Furlan20152_30-1">
                        [30]
                       </sup>
                       There does not appear to be any serious adverse effects associated with massage.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Furlan20152_30-2">
                        [30]
                       </sup>
                      </li>
                      <li class="">
                       Acupuncture may provide some relief for back pain. However, further research with stronger evidence needs to be done.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31">
                        [31]
                       </sup>
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       Spinal manipulation is a widely-used method of treating back pain, although there is no evidence of long-term benefits.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_28-1">
                        [28]
                       </sup>
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       "Back school" is an intervention that consists of both education and physical exercises.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_32-0">
                        [32]
                       </sup>
                       A 2016 Cochrane review found the evidence concerning back school to be very low quality and was not able to make generalizations as to whether back school is effective or not.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:7_32-1">
                        [32]
                       </sup>
                      </li>
                     </ul>
                     <h3>
                      <span class="mw-headline" id="Medication">
                       Medication
                      </span>
                     </h3>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      If non-pharmacological measures are not effective, medications may be tried.
                     </p>
                     <ul class="added-to-list1">
                      <li>
                       Non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) are typically tried first.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_26-1">
                        [26]
                       </sup>
                       NSAIDs have been shown to be more effective than placebo, and are usually more effective than paracetamol (acetaminophen).
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid186460782_33-0">
                        [33]
                       </sup>
                      </li>
                      <li class="">
                       In severe back pain not relieved by NSAIDs or acetaminophen, opioids may be used. However, long-term use of opioids has not been proven to be effective at treating back pain.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_34-0">
                        [34]
                       </sup>
                       Opioids have not always been shown to be better than placebo for chronic back pain when the risks and benefits are considered.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35">
                        [35]
                       </sup>
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       Skeletal muscle relaxers may also be used.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:0_26-2">
                        [26]
                       </sup>
                       Their short term use has been shown to be effective in the relief of acute back pain.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid128045072_36-0">
                        [36]
                       </sup>
                       However, the evidence of this effect has been disputed, and these medications do have negative side-effects.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:1_34-1">
                        [34]
                       </sup>
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       In people with nerve root pain and acute radiculopathy, there is evidence that a single dose of steroids, such as dexamethasone, may provide pain relief.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:8_8-11">
                        [8]
                       </sup>
                      </li>
                      <li>
                       Epidural corticosteroid injection (ESI) is a procedure in which steroid medications are injected into the epidural space. The steroid medications reduce inflammation and thus decrease pain and improve function.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37">
                        [37]
                       </sup>
                       ESI has long been used to both diagnose and treat back pain, although recent studies have shown a lack of efficacy in treating low back pain.
                       <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38">
                        [38]
                       </sup>
                      </li>
                     </ul>
                     <h3 class="">
                      <span class="mw-headline" id="Surgery">
                       Surgery
                      </span>
                     </h3>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      Surgery for back pain is typically used as a last resort, when serious neurological deficit is evident.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_28-2">
                       [28]
                      </sup>
                      A 2009 systematic review of back surgery studies found that, for certain diagnoses, surgery is moderately better than other common treatments, but the benefits of surgery often decline in the long term.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pmid193634552_39-0">
                       [39]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      Surgery may sometimes be appropriate for people with severe myelopathy or cauda equina syndrome.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_28-3">
                       [28]
                      </sup>
                      Causes of neurological deficits can include spinal disc herniation, spinal stenosis, degenerative disc disease, tumor, infection, and spinal hematomas, all of which can impinge on the nerve roots around the spinal cord.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_28-4">
                       [28]
                      </sup>
                      There are multiple surgical options to treat back pain, and these options vary depending on the cause of the pain.
                     </p>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      When a herniated disc is compressing the nerve roots, hemi- or partial- laminectomy or discectomy may be performed, in which the material compressing on the nerve is removed.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_28-5">
                       [28]
                      </sup>
                      A mutli-level laminectomy can be done to widen the spinal canal in the case of spinal stenosis. A foraminotomy or foraminectomy may also be necessary, if the vertebrae are causing significant nerve root compression.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_28-6">
                       [28]
                      </sup>
                      A discectomy is performed when the intervertebral disc has herniated or torn. It involves removing the protruding disc, either a portion of it or all of it, that is placing pressure on the nerve root.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40">
                       [40]
                      </sup>
                      Total disc replacement can also be performed, in which the source of the pain (the damaged disc) is removed and replaced, while maintaining spinal mobility.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41">
                       [41]
                      </sup>
                      When an entire disc is removed (as in discectomy), or when the vertebrae are unstable, spinal fusion surgery may be performed. Spinal fusion is a procedure in which bone grafts and metal hardware is used to fix together two or more vertebrae, thus preventing the bones of the spinal column from compressing on the spinal cord or nerve roots.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Burke22_42-0">
                       [42]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      If infection, such as a spinal epidural abscess, is the source of the back pain, surgery may be indicated when a trial of antibiotics is ineffective.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_28-7">
                       [28]
                      </sup>
                      Surgical evacuation of spinal hematoma can also be attempted, if the blood products fail to break down on their own.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:2_28-8">
                       [28]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <h2>
                      <span class="mw-headline" id="Pregnancy">
                       Pregnancy
                      </span>
                     </h2>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      About 50% of women experience low back pain during pregnancy.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43">
                       [43]
                      </sup>
                      Some studies have suggested women who have experienced back pain before pregnancy are at a higher risk of having back pain during pregnancy.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:03_44-0">
                       [44]
                      </sup>
                      It may be severe enough to cause significant pain and disability in up to a third of pregnant women.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Katonis20112_45-0">
                       [45]
                      </sup>
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:13_46-0">
                       [46]
                      </sup>
                      Back pain typically begins at around 18 weeks gestation, and peaks between 24 and 36 weeks gestation.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:13_46-1">
                       [46]
                      </sup>
                      Approximately 16% of women who experienced back pain during pregnancy report continued back pain years after pregnancy, indicating those with significant back pain are at greater risk of back pain following pregnancy.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Katonis20112_45-1">
                       [45]
                      </sup>
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:13_46-2">
                       [46]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      Biomechanical factors of pregnancy shown to be associated with back pain include increased curvature of the lower back, or lumbar lordosis, to support the added weight on the abdomen.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:13_46-3">
                       [46]
                      </sup>
                      Also, a hormone called relaxin is released during pregnancy that softens the structural tissues in the pelvis and lower back to prepare for vaginal delivery. This softening and increased flexibility of the ligaments and joints in the lower back can result in pain.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:13_46-4">
                       [46]
                      </sup>
                      Back pain in pregnancy is often accompanied by radicular symptoms, suggested to be caused by the fetus pressing on the sacral plexus and lumbar plexus in the pelvis.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:13_46-5">
                       [46]
                      </sup>
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:03_44-1">
                       [44]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      Typical factors aggravating the back pain of pregnancy include standing, sitting, forward bending, lifting, and walking. Back pain in pregnancy may also be characterized by pain radiating into the thigh and buttocks, night-time pain severe enough to wake the patient, pain that is increased during the night-time, or pain that is increased during the day-time.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Katonis20112_45-2">
                       [45]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      Local heat, acetaminophen (paracetamol), and massage can be used to help relieve the pain. Avoiding standing for prolonged periods of time is also suggested.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47">
                       [47]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
                     <h2>
                      <span class="mw-headline" id="Economics">
                       Economics
                      </span>
                     </h2>
                     <p class="added-to-list1">
                      Although back pain is does not typically cause permanent disability, it is a significant contributor to physician visits and missed work days in the United States, and is the single leading cause of disability worldwide.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:6_4-1">
                       [4]
                      </sup>
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2010_Global_Burden_of_Disease_Study_5-1">
                       [5]
                      </sup>
                      The American Academy of Orthopaedic Surgeons report approximately 12 million visits to doctor's offices each year are due to back pain.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_2-5">
                       [2]
                      </sup>
                      Missed work and disability related to low back pain costs over $50 billion each year in the United States.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_2-6">
                       [2]
                      </sup>
                      In the United Kingdom in 1998, approximately £1.6 billion per year was spent on expenses related to disability from back pain.
                      <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-:5_2-7">
                       [2]
                      </sup>
                     </p>
