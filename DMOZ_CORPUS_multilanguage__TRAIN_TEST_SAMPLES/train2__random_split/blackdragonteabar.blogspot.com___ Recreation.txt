              <meta content="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-Aiykts3mRyU/WdveLARS1MI/AAAAAAAAIBA/I3zbTWwf6vAp7vCi4LHIRw_AGGfiF2IdgCEwYBhgL/s320/IMG_4868.jpg" itemprop="image_url"/>

              <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
               <a href="http://blackdragonteabar.blogspot.com/2017/10/northwest-tea-festival-2017-and.html">
                Northwest Tea Festival 2017 and Colombian Black Tea Cupping
               </a>
              </h3>
              <div class="post-header">
               <div class="post-header-line-1">
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-5595574420987852748" itemprop="description articleBody">
               On September 30th and October 1st I attended the
               <a href="http://nwteafestival.com/">
                Northwest Tea Festival
               </a>
               at Seattle Center. Both days, I presented a session in 
 the tea tasting booths called
               <u>
                Oolongs with Brett
               </u>
               . As the name suggests my sessions featured me 
 serving and discussing oolong tea (along with a bit of ukulele strumming). It felt great to get behind a bamboo serving tray once again!
               <br/>
               <br/>
               After my tasting sessions I spent my time sampling tea 
 and connecting with friends on the expo floor. I picked up some fantastic Alishan from
               <a href="https://www.floatingleavestea.com/">
                Floating Leaves
               </a>
               and miscellaneous other goodies from some of my favorite stores (like
               <a href="http://www.phoenixteashop.com/">
                Phoenix Tea
               </a>
               ,
               <a href="http://smacha.com/">
                Smacha
               </a>
               , and
               <a href="https://youngmountaintea.com/">
                Young Mountain
               </a>
               ). My tea cupboard is now ready for Autumn!
               <br/>
               <br/>
               At one point my old boss Brian
  Keating wanted to introduce me to a
               <a href="https://bitacotea.com/">
                Colombian tea producer called Bitaco.
               </a>
               I had never tried Colombian tea so I was excited to give it a shot. After chatting with Brian and the Bitaco representatives, I had faith that Bitaco cares about the people in their community and the lush mountain environment at which their tea is grown. Also, their tea looked and smelled super fresh and the price was great.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               I picked up these 3 Colombian black teas:
               <br/>
               <br/>
               <div style="text-align: center;">
                Tippy 2
               </div>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                <a href="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-Aiykts3mRyU/WdveLARS1MI/AAAAAAAAIBA/I3zbTWwf6vAp7vCi4LHIRw_AGGfiF2IdgCEwYBhgL/s1600/IMG_4868.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                 <img border="0" data-original-height="1196" data-original-width="1600" height="239" src="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-Aiykts3mRyU/WdveLARS1MI/AAAAAAAAIBA/I3zbTWwf6vAp7vCi4LHIRw_AGGfiF2IdgCEwYBhgL/s320/IMG_4868.jpg" width="320"/>
                </a>
               </div>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                <br/>
               </div>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                Wiry 2
               </div>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                <a href="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-IhvOZ4GJ7Fg/WdveOKLMe5I/AAAAAAAAIBA/v4szQGQ7vBExCG1D30VxsJZQkBFh7iu2gCEwYBhgL/s1600/IMG_4866.jpg" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                 <img border="0" data-original-height="1196" data-original-width="1600" height="239" src="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-IhvOZ4GJ7Fg/WdveOKLMe5I/AAAAAAAAIBA/v4szQGQ7vBExCG1D30VxsJZQkBFh7iu2gCEwYBhgL/s320/IMG_4866.jpg" width="320"/>
                </a>
               </div>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                <br/>
               </div>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                Black Wiry 1
               </div>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                <a href="https://4.bp.blogspot.com/-ga70SzMNnZw/WdveM60EhyI/AAAAAAAAIBA/Wc9YwbrfJ3Er3sZI63GVdqn4X7eSrjnXwCEwYBhgL/s1600/IMG_4869.jpg" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                 <img border="0" data-original-height="1196" data-original-width="1600" height="239" src="https://4.bp.blogspot.com/-ga70SzMNnZw/WdveM60EhyI/AAAAAAAAIBA/Wc9YwbrfJ3Er3sZI63GVdqn4X7eSrjnXwCEwYBhgL/s320/IMG_4869.jpg" width="320"/>
                </a>
               </div>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                <br/>
               </div>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                During the past week I enjoyed mugs of each tea, but today I decided to cup all three side by side to see how they really compared. I used 3 grams of leaf in three identical 6 ounce glass mugs. Each tea was steeped 3 minutes with boiling water.
               </div>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: left;">
                <br/>
               </div>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                Wiry 2 (top left)
               </div>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                Tippy 2 (top right)
               </div>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                Wiry 1 (bottom middle)
               </div>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                <a href="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-YGa-926_uYg/Wdvefz5EzrI/AAAAAAAAIBA/oXX5r_IPEH0NSpEQn0qZJdhqy0dvK-r3QCEwYBhgL/s1600/IMG_4878.jpg" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                 <img border="0" data-original-height="1196" data-original-width="1600" height="239" src="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-YGa-926_uYg/Wdvefz5EzrI/AAAAAAAAIBA/oXX5r_IPEH0NSpEQn0qZJdhqy0dvK-r3QCEwYBhgL/s320/IMG_4878.jpg" width="320"/>
                </a>
               </div>
               <br/>
               The teas cupped up nicely. Here are my tasting notes:
               <br/>
               <span class="st">
                ✵
               </span>
               Wiry 2 had the lightest body, but not by much. It was brisk, sweet and clean tasting. Compared to the others it was mellow, but the delicate notes of fruit, honey, and wood made it the most complex. It's a black tea that drinks more like a dark oolong.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               <span class="st">
                ✵
               </span>
               Wiry 1 was the most peppery. It reminded me of a mid-grade Yunnan hongcha. It is a great cup of tea but it slightly lacked complexity when sipped alongside the other two.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               <span class="st">
                ✵
               </span>
               Tippy 2 was very nice. It had the most body and mouthfeel. It was malty with a sweet stone-fruit aftertaste. If you like high quality Assam tea this would be one to try.

               <a href="http://blackdragonteabar.blogspot.com/2017/01/red-miso-is-done.html">
                Red Miso is Done!
               </a>
              </h3>
              <div class="post-header">
               <div class="post-header-line-1">
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-8070534571445257367" itemprop="description articleBody">
               <br/>
               After 3 whole years fermenting in my basement I am excited to announce that my homemade Red Miso is finally ready! Yesterday I packaged up the entire batch into 3 quart sized mason jars. That's a lot of Miso. I'll definitely be sharing with local friends and family.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               For more information on how I made this Miso please check out my post from last January called
               <a href="http://blackdragonteabar.blogspot.com/2016/01/red-misos-second-birthday.html">
                Red Miso's Second Birthday
               </a>
               .
               <br/>
               <br/>
               It turned out great! I tasted it side by side with an all natural, no preservative, store bought Red Miso that I had in my fridge. Mine is bolder, smokier, saltier, and less sweet, but still a little sweet, very rich and delicious.

              <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
               <a href="http://blackdragonteabar.blogspot.com/2016/12/shell-bretts-original-board-game.html">
                SHELL - Brett's original Board Game!
               </a>
              </h3>
              <div class="post-header">
               <div class="post-header-line-1">
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-6192037586964461025" itemprop="description articleBody">
               Origin Story: One night in 2008, I had this dream. I was in a
  youth hostel in Vancouver B.C. watching a young Canadian woman with 
 massive dreadlocks putting away dishes in a cramped kitchenette. I 
 watched her for quite a while as the cups and plates kept popping back 
 out at her from the overloaded cupboard.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               Out of the blue, a young man with a shaven head and Northern European 
 accent asked me, "Do you want to play The Princess Shell?" As I turned 
 to face the man, he said, "It is the best game in the world."
               <br/>
               <br/>
               I was startled and intrigued. "Uh, OK," I replied. At this, the man 
 reached up to pull a large solid-wood game board off of a high 
 bookshelf. With dramatic flair, he blew all the dust off the board 
 before setting it before us on a low coffee table.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               "These small tiles are called Secrets," he explained. "They are blown 
 around the board by an ever-present Wind. This one is the Blind Dog, 
 this one is the Liar's Mask, and this one is called Grandfather's Axe...."
               <br/>
               <br/>
               Unfortunately, at this point in the dream, my brain decided I'd had 
 enough intrigue and pulled the plug. Even though it was 2:00 AM, I was 
 too excited to fall back to sleep. With the dream fresh in my mind, I 
 went into our office (now our kid's bedroom) and started making 
 notes. I knew I wanted to make The Princess Shell into a real board 
 game, but I had no idea where to start. After I made a few sketches and 
 notes, I forced myself to go back to bed.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               Over the last 8 years, I have worked on my game in fits and starts. 
 Thanks to massive amounts of help from my family and friends, it has 
 evolved into a very original and fun board game with clear, 
 easy-to-learn rules. I named my game SHELL (the "Princess" part of the 
 title was abandoned early on) and it is a game of strategy, memory and 
 luck. I love my game and would like it to reach a larger 
 audience. Visit the official
               <a href="http://shellboardgame.blogspot.com/">
                SHELL website here
               </a>
               .
               <br/>
               <br/>
               Please email me (blackdragontea AT gmail DOT com ) if you...
               <br/>
               ... have any questions about SHELL (or tea or ukuleles :)
               <br/>
               ... are in the Seattle area and would like to play SHELL.
               <br/>
               ... would like to order your very own handmade 
 SHELL board game.
               <br/>
               ... have connections or advice regarding the 
 board game and/or computer/app industries.

              <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
               <a href="http://blackdragonteabar.blogspot.com/2016/09/bitter-melon-tie-guanyin.html">
                Bitter Melon Tie Guanyin - 苦瓜鐵觀音
               </a>
              </h3>
              <div class="post-header">
               <div class="post-header-line-1">
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-6016379233841397498" itemprop="description articleBody">
               Several months ago while drinking tea with my friends Jason and Andrew at Smacha, I received (among other things) a little sample of Bitter Melon Tie Guanyin (苦瓜
               <span class="st">
                鐵觀音
               </span>
               ) oolong tea.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               These days I typically choose either an oolong or a hongcha for my daily brew, and I tend to stick with teas I know. But today I was feeling more adventurous so I decided to give this novelty a try.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               I was surprised to find the tea leaves are actually packed into a dried bitter-melon rind shaped like a little bumpy cylinder! The leaves themselves look and smell pleasant. It is a roasted oolong tea.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                <a href="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-MqQc1XtkeM4/V-BIJgPaFLI/AAAAAAAAH1Y/-nZK_BYqKhMtDzQbKxdLkTpw3TgrRSX3gCLcB/s1600/IMG_3059.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                 <img border="0" height="320" src="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-MqQc1XtkeM4/V-BIJgPaFLI/AAAAAAAAH1Y/-nZK_BYqKhMtDzQbKxdLkTpw3TgrRSX3gCLcB/s320/IMG_3059.jpg" width="239"/>
                </a>
               </div>
               <br/>
               I used freshly boiled water and gave the "unit" a three second rinse in my gaiwan. It smells quite good, roasty, sweet and mellow.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               The first infusion was about 30 seconds and poured pretty light. As you might imagine it took the water a few infusions to get into the center of this "wheel of leaves" and start to push the real flavor out. I continued to do short steeps because I was afraid of the bitterness that actually never came. After the second infusion, with the leaves pushing their way out of the melon rind, the liquor turned a medium-dark amber color. I enjoyed the flavor but it does have a weird vegetable note. The sweet caramel-like oolong flavors are muddied up by a tart cucumber-skin aftertaste.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                <a href="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-lEhwTx27A-g/V-BINWT3UFI/AAAAAAAAH1c/nZpxdWkRPtsLGs5sRVZGTcdLoXFcR-BZwCLcB/s1600/IMG_3060.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                 <img border="0" height="320" src="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-lEhwTx27A-g/V-BINWT3UFI/AAAAAAAAH1c/nZpxdWkRPtsLGs5sRVZGTcdLoXFcR-BZwCLcB/s320/IMG_3060.jpg" width="239"/>
                </a>
               </div>
               <br/>
               After 15 years as a heavy tea drinker I have a pretty good understanding of what my body craves. I drink tea for the satisfied feeling it gives me, the flavor, and the energy (
               <i>
                qi)
               </i>
               (
               <span class="_Tgc">
                氣
               </span>
               ). Maybe it's because I do not have any sentimental connections to bitter melon or it's potential health benefits that I found that this tea was ultimately not worth my time.
               <div style="clear: both;">
               </div>

           <h2 class="date-header">
            <span>
             Sunday, May 8, 2016
            </span>
           </h2>
           <div class="date-posts">
            <div class="post-outer">
             <div class="post hentry uncustomized-post-template" itemprop="blogPost" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/BlogPosting">
              <meta content="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-KhG2k2QRwXw/T39m03POjSI/AAAAAAAAGjw/QaCHXsZOm70/s320/DSC02121.JPG" itemprop="image_url"/>

              <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
               <a href="http://blackdragonteabar.blogspot.com/2016/05/10-years-ago-today.html">
                10 Years Ago Today
               </a>
              </h3>
              <div class="post-header">
               <div class="post-header-line-1">
               </div>
              </div>
              <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-3341386355709574531" itemprop="description articleBody">
               On May 8, 2006, during my second trip to Taiwan, my friend
               <a href="http://floatingleaves.com/">
                Shiuwen
               </a>
               treated my buddy Darald and me to an unforgettable tea tour. We met up with her bright and early in Taipei and had a hearty breakfast of steamed vegetable buns, fried
               <span style="font-style: italic;">
                youtiao
               </span>
               and fresh soymilk. After our meal, and after we took care of a few errands (including sending my mom a Mother's Day card), we proceeded via metro to Xindian station, which is where we got the bus up to Pinglin.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               It was a very hot day in Taipei and the air was barely moving but once we got off the bus in Pinglin we were soothed by a lower temperature and a gentle breeze. The air in Pinglin is sweet and the surrounding hills are lush and green. Our first stop in town was Shiuwen's friend Farmer Chen's tea shop where we drank some unforgettable Wenshan Baozhong tea. After tea we had a satisfying lunch of tea oil noodles (a local specialty), fried tofu, and soup with lotus flowers.
               <br/>
               <br/>
               After lunch, we headed up to Farmer Chen's tea farm by car. Once at the farm I was excited to learn that we would be able to pick tea leaves. Darald and I were shown the basic technique, given a hat and basket, and put to work. In one hour I had plucked less than one tenth of the tea the locals had harvested. I watched as their hands moved quickly and skillfully over the tea bushes while my hands brought to mind those of an intoxicated sloth. I was still given a pat on the back and told that I didn't do too bad for my first try. It was a wonderful experience that left me with even greater respect and appreciation for the professionals.
               <br/>
               <div style="text-align: center;">
                <div style="text-align: left;">
                 <br/>
                </div>
                <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-KhG2k2QRwXw/T39m03POjSI/AAAAAAAAGjw/QaCHXsZOm70/s1600/DSC02121.JPG">
                 <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5728410309405609250" src="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-KhG2k2QRwXw/T39m03POjSI/AAAAAAAAGjw/QaCHXsZOm70/s320/DSC02121.JPG" style="cursor: hand; cursor: pointer; height: 320px; width: 240px;"/>
                </a>
                <br/>
                <br/>
                <div style="text-align: left;">
                 After tea picking time we spread our leaves on rattan trays to wither...
                </div>
                <br/>
                <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-H8cVLiDVZyw/T4JL8naaplI/AAAAAAAAGkQ/ZPuPkqbdtng/s1600/DSC02127.JPG">
                 <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5729225180712707666" src="https://1.bp.blogspot.com/-H8cVLiDVZyw/T4JL8naaplI/AAAAAAAAGkQ/ZPuPkqbdtng/s320/DSC02127.JPG" style="cursor: hand; cursor: pointer; height: 240px; width: 320px;"/>
                </a>
                <br/>
                <br/>
                <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-O3RAispXkhM/T4JMQ1KwrjI/AAAAAAAAGk0/CkLLDrbZhIw/s1600/014_11A.JPG">
                 <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5729225528002522674" src="https://4.bp.blogspot.com/-O3RAispXkhM/T4JMQ1KwrjI/AAAAAAAAGk0/CkLLDrbZhIw/s320/014_11A.JPG" style="cursor: hand; cursor: pointer; height: 320px; width: 214px;"/>
                </a>
                <br/>
                <br/>
                <div style="text-align: left;">
                 ...then we walked over to the neighbor's farm to see Dong Fang Mei Ren (
                 <span class="st">
                  東方美人)
                 </span>
                 oolong tea in various stages of production.
                </div>
                <br/>
                <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-HIeuLKokJSw/T4JMawONBaI/AAAAAAAAGlA/RehOr0tQAW4/s1600/013_10A.JPG">
                 <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5729225698473477538" src="https://1.bp.blogspot.com/-HIeuLKokJSw/T4JMawONBaI/AAAAAAAAGlA/RehOr0tQAW4/s320/013_10A.JPG" style="cursor: hand; cursor: pointer; height: 320px; width: 214px;"/>
                </a>
                <br/>
                <br/>
                <div style="text-align: left;">
                 As we walked around the misty, green farms, Chen and Shiuwen pointed out the different cultivars of tea bushes we passed. Qing Xin (青心) was the most common, but Jin Xuan (金萱), Fo Shou (佛手)  and even Wuyi Mountain Shui Xian (水仙) tea bushes were planted in this area.
                 <br/>
                 <br/>
                 <div style="text-align: center;">
                  (This one is Fo Shou.)
                 </div>
                 <div style="text-align: center;">
                  <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-DHoM-uWdZmY/T4JUlZoTKyI/AAAAAAAAGlM/-OFHQQKfVIE/s1600/DSC02117.JPG">
                   <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5729234677480500002" src="https://1.bp.blogspot.com/-DHoM-uWdZmY/T4JUlZoTKyI/AAAAAAAAGlM/-OFHQQKfVIE/s320/DSC02117.JPG" style="cursor: hand; cursor: pointer; height: 240px; width: 320px;"/>
                  </a>
                 </div>
                </div>
                <br/>
                <div style="text-align: left;">
                 We drank some more fresh tea and then traveled back to town. Shiuwen, Darald and I visited a large facility for de-stemming and sorting hundreds of pounds of Wenshan Baozhong tea. It was fascinating.
                </div>
                <br/>
                <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-viJQJrbeOrU/T4JV25na5wI/AAAAAAAAGlY/I3SVu5pD9CE/s1600/DSC02132.JPG">
                 <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5729236077636151042" src="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-viJQJrbeOrU/T4JV25na5wI/AAAAAAAAGlY/I3SVu5pD9CE/s320/DSC02132.JPG" style="cursor: hand; cursor: pointer; height: 240px; width: 320px;"/>
                </a>
                <br/>
                <br/>
                <div style="text-align: left;">
                 We stopped into another tea shop where I purchased some baked Baozhong for Teacup (the store I was managing at the time), and two clay teapots (for myself). I also took a small shot of Baozhong wine offered to me by some locals. It was strong stuff.
                 <br/>
                 <br/>
                 In the evening we said goodbye to Pinglin and hopped aboard a bus headed back to Taipei. The ride was somewhat eventful because an air conditioning unit was leaking water all over one vinyl seat. About halfway to Taipei the bus was full except for that seat. An old lady came in with two plastic bags. She looked all around for a seat and was dismayed by her only option being soggy. I was close by so I sprung up to offer my seat. She tried to protest but I insisted and she sat down while I stood. Then she gave me a present. It was a special local food. Some type of  gummy rice pocket with a bright, artificial-red, raw-onion-tasting filling. I thanked her and ate a little bit. I gave the old lady a smile and a big thumbs up... but I really didn't like it, so I put it in my pocket to deal with later.
                 <br/>
                 <br/>
                 The evening in Taipei was not too hot and we all had a great dinner at a Buddhist buffet. Then we headed over to Shiuwen's friend Lao Ji Zi's shop for even more great tea.
                </div>
                <br/>
                (That's our own fresh picked Baozhong leaves on the tray.)
                <br/>
                <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-3ZItD-FvuIs/T4JMANoUPZI/AAAAAAAAGkc/n0Y3o3IANOI/s1600/DSC02139.JPG">
                 <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5729225242511162770" src="https://1.bp.blogspot.com/-3ZItD-FvuIs/T4JMANoUPZI/AAAAAAAAGkc/n0Y3o3IANOI/s320/DSC02139.JPG" style="cursor: hand; cursor: pointer; height: 240px; width: 320px;"/>
                </a>
                <br/>
                <br/>
                <div style="text-align: left;">
                 Although we were already quite tea drunk we made room for some more amazing tea. I had an Alishan High Mountian Oolong that was so incredible. It was the type of tea that makes a tea-person empty out their wallet and say, "How much will this get me?"
                 <br/>
                 <br/>
                 We also cupped up the Baozhong leaves that we picked that afternoon. By this point they had only been withered slightly and jostled about in our bags during the commute back to town. They had a subtle sweet floral aroma and clean clear broth with notes of lilac and dew. A very refreshing way to end the night.
                 <br/>
                 <br/>
                 <div style="text-align: center;">
                  (Those are our jade green leaves in the large bowl.)
                 </div>
                </div>
                <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-g2o9eixlAn8/T4JMDw73MLI/AAAAAAAAGko/knHTpMaR5yU/s1600/DSC02141.JPG">
                 <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5729225303527993522" src="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-g2o9eixlAn8/T4JMDw73MLI/AAAAAAAAGko/knHTpMaR5yU/s320/DSC02141.JPG" style="cursor: hand; cursor: pointer; height: 320px; width: 240px;"/>
                </a>
                <br/>
                <div style="text-align: left;">
                 <br/>
                 All in all it was a wonderful day in Northern Taiwan. I can never thank Shiuwen enough!
                </div>
