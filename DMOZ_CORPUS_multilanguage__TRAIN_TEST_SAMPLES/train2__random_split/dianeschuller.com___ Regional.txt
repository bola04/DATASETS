          <article class="sc post-9101 post type-post status-publish format-standard hentry category-a-story category-my-life tag-littlestoriesofmylife tag-ecology tag-memories" id="article-9101">
           <div class="article-wrap sc content-bg">
            <div class="article-wrap-inner">
             <div class="article-header normal" data-role="header">
              <span class="article-date article-meta-item">
               <span>
                Monday, July 23, 2018
               </span>
              </span>
              <div class="article-title-wrap">
               <h2 class="article-title">
                <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/2018/07/23/a-little-story-about-observing/" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to A little story about observing">
                 A little story about observing
                </a>
               </h2>
              </div>
              <div class="article-meta article-meta-top">
               <span class="article-category-list article-meta-item">
                Posted in
                <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/category/a-story/" rel="category tag">
                 A Story
                </a>
                ,
                <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/category/my-life/" rel="category tag">
                 my life
                </a>
               </span>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="article-content sc" data-role="content">
              <p>
               Susan Sontag wrote, “Do stuff. be clenched, curious. Not waiting for inspiration’s shove or society’s kiss on your forehead. Pay attention. It’s all about paying attention. attention is vitality. It connects you with others. It makes you eager. stay eager.” She’s basically suggesting we get involved in life and continually observe.
              </p>
              <p>
               <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/2013/08/07/random-photos-plus-recommendations/dsc_8809/" rel="attachment wp-att-6097">
                <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-6097 ov-done" height="600" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/08/DSC_8809.jpg" width="600"/>
               </a>
              </p>
              <p>
               I’m a born observer. It’s in my DNA. You’ll find it’s what I used to call my online space even though now it’s simply mentioned in the header. Being an observer has saved my butt — literally and figuratively — on numerous occasions. In fact, being a keen observer is likely also why I’ve always had such an innate ability to ‘read’ animals.
              </p>
              <p>
               Many of us lay claim to be strongly aware of the countless tiny, private observances that enrich our day: the stripes of sunlight painted across our favourite chair, the steam swirling up from your morning coffee, the dappled shadows on the lawn under a tree. These little observances happen all day, every day, yet each day they take on a unique manifestation.
              </p>
              <p>
               <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/2014/05/30/bursting/dsc_1089/" rel="attachment wp-att-6679">
                <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-6679 ov-done" height="600" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/05/DSC_1089.jpg" width="399"/>
               </a>
              </p>
              <p>
               In school, I was a very shy, quiet person. Quite the opposite of how I walk through the world now. Quiet people always know more than what they seem to know. Multiple times, as a kid, when I knew an answer to something the teacher might be asking, I never raised my hand in the air, nor would I utter an answer I already knew if called upon by a teacher. Teachers and students alike surely thought I had little knowledge of anything or that I paid no attention. But they were quite wrong in that assumption. I simply didn’t wish to bring attention to myself in those days. The irony is, because I appeared ‘dumb’ and appeared to not know any of the answers, I brought negative attention to myself.
              </p>
              <p>
               In Junior High School (referred to as Middle School in some parts), I remember a science project we were to do and it was to have a significant percentage toward our final mark in the class. We had a choice of topics which would demonstrate our knowledge of the various sections learned through the year. I chose the section on ecology. It was a chance to actually do something that I enjoyed and knew something about while not having to worry about a stressful test. Most of my fellow science students were upset and many were stressed and would have preferred a test.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9105 ov-done" height="591" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSC_1645.jpg" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               I set out to the bush that I knew so well. Yes, I used to spend a great deal of time walking through the brush, breaking spider webs with my face, arms and legs being scratched from the wild roses and dried deadfall. I felt comfortable there and always was intrigued by what I observed. For my project, I took photographs of things likes mosses &amp; lichens growing on fallen logs; how fungi grow on the trees; feathers scattered from where an owl had made a recent kill; the dried remains of a hare; a cluster of busy ants; droplets of dew that provided water for insects; and so on. After getting the black &amp; white photographs developed I glued them on pages and wrote descriptions for each detailing the relationships and interactions of the things I had observed. It was the easiest thing I had ever done for school.  It wasn’t fancy and perhaps even had a crude presentation with the photos being glued to the paper.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9106 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSC_6348.jpg" height="592" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9110 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSC_0825.jpg" height="800" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="800"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9107 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSC_6354.jpg" height="800" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="532"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9108 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSC_6529.jpg" height="890" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9109 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSC_6807.jpg" height="800" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="532"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               Boy, did my ‘paper’ ever surprise the science teacher. Not only did it earn one of the highest marks I’d ever received, but the teacher wrote a note saying what an excellent project it was and how impressed he was with my interpretations. Little did he know, those were simple observances I was used to making every day and how easy it came to me. I admit to being incredibly proud of that project and, to this day, it warms my heart having been acknowledged for my simple act of observing.
              </p>
              <p style="text-align: center;">
               <em>
                “Observation is a dying art.”
               </em>
               — Stanley Kubrick
              </p>
              <p style="text-align: center;">
               ==========
              </p>
              <p>
               I’ve been working on a short little video that would have been the ideal pairing for this post. Unfortunately it is not complete yet because … I’m busy taking in life. Instead I thought I’d share a little something for the writers out there:
              </p>
              <p>
               Writers know that observation is key to enriching your works. So I’m providing a bonus link for you.
               <a href="http://www.writersdigest.com/editor-blogs/there-are-no-rules/the-power-of-observation-how-to-observe-and-improve-your-writing" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
                The Power of Observation: How to Observe and Improve Your Writing
               </a>
               by Cris Freese with additional advice from Barbara Baig.
              </p>
              <p>
               Wishing you all a week full of curiosity.
              </p>

             <div class="article-content sc" data-role="content">
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9084 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSCN1407.jpg" height="668" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               Wild ferns and cedar boughs trace the pathway. The air is fresh, the sun desperately fighting it’s way through the canopy of trees, and soon the chatter takes over as we enter the private garden. This private acreage hosts over 90 island artists and creators including stone sculptures, pottery, paintings, soap, fibre art, glass work, blacksmith art, and more, all set to live music. Two thousand people will walk through over the two days and the total funds raised for charity shall bring the total to $30K.
              </p>
              <p style="text-align: center;">
               <em>
                “Curiosity about life in all of its aspects, I think, is still the secret of great creative people”
               </em>
               – Leo Burnett
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9085 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSCN1410.jpg" height="668" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               The variety and uniqueness of artwork and creations makes this an annual event for many. And the beautiful, relaxing atmosphere certainly is as significant a draw as the art.
              </p>
              <p>
               <em>
                “Creativity is more than just being different. Anybody can plan weird; that’s easy. What’s hard is to be as simple as Bach. Making the simple, awesomely simple, that’s creativity.”
               </em>
               – Charles Mingus
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9086 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSCN1413.jpg" height="668" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               Live music is something everyone always enjoys.
              </p>
              <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_9087" style="width: 900px">
               <img alt="" class="size-full wp-image-9087 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSCN1425.jpg" height="668" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
               <p class="wp-caption-text">
                and the band played on
               </p>
              </div>
              <p>
               Food and treats may be purchased while walking through the loops of displays.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9088 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSCN1416.jpg" height="668" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               It’s a converted horse trailer.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9089 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSCN1415.jpg" height="668" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               I love the quaintness such as these hand drawn signs showing us the way. The buntings must number in the thousands. They line both sides of the forest trails leading visitors to the many display areas, gardens, food and refreshment stands, and show the way in and out. Obviously there are a lot of volunteer hours put into this event, and others like it.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9090 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSCN1423.jpg" height="668" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               I’ve always had an admiration for people who create. Some artists are also incredibly innovative and attempt to work with their medium in different and unique ways.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9091 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSCN1418.jpg" height="668" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9092 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSCN1420.jpg" height="890" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               Sitting and relaxing around the pond was pretty much a ‘full house’. On the day we went, the resident lab was out fishing in the pond (sorry no photos, even though he did make it in the last photo — it’s hard to find him and impossible to know he’s fishing! Apparently it’s something he does with great frequency.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9093 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSCN1421.jpg" height="890" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               The photos were only taken with my little point and shoot camera. Having to walk a few kilometres to get to the garden and then knowing how long we might be wandering the property, I didn’t care to haul my heavy camera. I hope they give you an idea of some of what I found interesting. Other than the ceramic garden ‘sticks’ and baskets of hand spun yarn at the beginning, I chose not to photograph the artists’ work out of respect. Instead I focused on the people and a few items of interest.
              </p>
              <p>
               Days like these, free of all social media, taking in such an array of creativity, the interest and conversation of the people, all while being immersed in a natural setting, is one of the best kinds of days. Don’t you think?
              </p>
              <p>
               Thank you for shadowing me on our afternoon spent at the Nanoose Bay Art in the Garden.
              </p>
              <p style="text-align: center;">
               <em>
                “Creativity is contagious, pass it on”
               </em>
               – Albert Einstein
              </p>
              <p>
              </p>

          <article class="sc post-9060 post type-post status-publish format-standard has-post-thumbnail hentry category-a-story category-my-life tag-littlestoriesofmylife tag-food tag-food-memory tag-memories tag-quotes" id="article-9060">
           <div class="article-wrap sc content-bg">
            <div class="article-wrap-inner">
             <div class="article-header normal" data-role="header">
              <span class="article-date article-meta-item">
               <span>
                Monday, July 9, 2018
               </span>
              </span>
              <div class="article-title-wrap">
               <h2 class="article-title">
                <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/2018/07/09/people-who-love-to-eat/" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to People who love to eat">
                 People who love to eat
                </a>
               </h2>
              </div>
              <div class="article-meta article-meta-top">
               <span class="article-category-list article-meta-item">
                Posted in
                <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/category/a-story/" rel="category tag">
                 A Story
                </a>
                ,
                <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/category/my-life/" rel="category tag">
                 my life
                </a>
               </span>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="article-content sc" data-role="content">
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9061 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/diptych-food-2.jpg" height="445" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
               <br/>
               Julia Child said,”People who love to eat are the best people.” Though I agree there’s a modicum of truth in that, in my view, it should be balanced with something Anna Thomas was quoted as saying, “We all eat, and it would be a sad waste of opportunity to eat badly.”
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9062 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSC_6980.jpg" height="591" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9063 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSC_6991.jpg" height="591" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               I’m one of those offbeat people who still cooks nearly everything from scratch. Processed foods are not in any kind of abundance in our home, though I definitely admit to having a few commercially made crackers, multiple bags of pasta, a handful of ethnic sauces, and often some frozen tortellini or ravioli on hand (more pasta).
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9064 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSCN1400.jpg" height="668" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p style="text-align: center;">
               <em>
                local halibut &amp; asparagus with roasted yam
               </em>
              </p>
              <p>
               For years now, my entire body has taken on a different shape due, in part, to my love of food. But we all know gaining weight isn’t just food. It’s how much food we eat (my biggest problem), how much starch we eat (my second biggest problem), the kind of food we eat, and all wrapped up with the ribbons of lifestyle and exercise (or lack thereof). True, I’m not nearly as active as I was up until about my forties, which is really when my exercise level began to wane. The other truth is that I continued to eat just as much as I did when I was a highly active adult. So now I’m a slow moving, daily walking senior who carries more weight than is healthy.
              </p>
              <p style="text-align: center;">
               <em>
                “My doctor told me I had to stop throwing intimate dinners for four unless there are three other people.”
               </em>
               — Orson Welles is responsible for that clever quip
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9065 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSC_7069.jpg" height="591" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               We love to entertain. Having friends and family over for a meal is one of my favourite ways to spend time with those whose presence we love to share. It’s an opportunity for interesting conversation, some laughter, and a way to become closer. Cesar Chavez was on the right track, “If you really want to make a friend, go to someone’s house and eat with him … the people who give you their food give you their heart.”  My mom was that person who gave you her food along with her heart.
              </p>
              <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_9066" style="width: 900px">
               <img alt="" class="size-full wp-image-9066 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSC_7082.jpg" height="591" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
               <p class="wp-caption-text">
                Rosemary, Sage, Thyme
               </p>
              </div>
              <p>
               There have always been diets around and, being what they are — fads or trends — they come and go with varying amounts of success. I’m not a believer in diets; never have been. The problem with diets is that they are only a temporary rung on the ladder of life. Once a person steps off that rung, they put their feet back onto the other permanent rungs of the ladder, and so it goes. If a person is genuinely serious about becoming healthier and losing some weight, then the real solution — in my personal view — is to make lifestyle changes. To eat differently, to eat less, to become active on a regular basis and to incorporate all of this as part of a lifelong lifestyle.
              </p>
              <p>
               The reason for all this talk is because, you guessed it, I’m making some shifts in my lifestyle. It’s never too late. I’m incorporating the H.A.M. diet. What!? But I just said I don’t agree with diets. Okay, H.A.M. simply stands for half-as-much. So I’m doing something I should have begun years ago and simply eating smaller portions. I’m also making effort to reduce the amount of starchy food I’ve been eating. I hadn’t realized how much starchy food I eat on a daily basis (for instance: pasta, rice, potatoes, yams, and a slow increase in how frequently we eat bread). I should mention that I’m not cutting them out entirely because some of the starchy foods (such as beans, yam, and potatoes) have important nutrient value. One more lifestyle change I’m incorporating is getting back on my bike like I used to. Since I have some joint issues, the bike allows me to get more of a workout than simply walking does. So those are the keystones to this lifestyle shift I’ve begun to incorporate. Guess what the most difficult thing is for me with these simple changes? It’s the starchy foods. It’s like I became addicted to the stuff. I want starchy things all the time.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9067 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/07/DSC_7071.jpg" height="591" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p style="text-align: center;">
               <em>
                “Vegetables are a must on a diet. I suggest carrot cake, zucchini bread, and pumpkin pie.”
               </em>
               –Jim Davis
              </p>
              <p>
               Food also has a
               <a href="https://www.huffingtonpost.ca/entry/power-of-food-memories_us_5908b1d7e4b02655f8413610" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
                powerful recall to times in our past
               </a>
               . Susan Whitborne, professor of psychological and brain sciences at the University of Massachusetts states, “A lot of our memories as children, it’s not so much the apple pie, for example, but the whole experience of being a family, being nourished, and that acquires a lot of symbolism apart from the sensory quality.” At this time of year swiss chard and yellow beans are abundant at the farmer’s market and each time we have them (which is a lot lately) I can’t help but recall my childhood and my mother. Mom always grew a large vegetable garden so summer was when we ate the best. I remember feeling like we were rich because of the abundance of fresh garden goodies and those amazing flavours. The fresh and earthy tastes really hold such fond memories. Powerful food memory is a subject I could write an entire blog post about.
              </p>
              <p>
               Now, after showing you all this food and talking about my efforts at shifting lifestyle, here is a six-second clip of Heidi demonstrating the definition of glee!
              </p>
              <p>
               <iframe allow="autoplay; encrypted-media" allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="281" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/YYrT7a8R3iM?feature=oembed" width="500">
               </iframe>
              </p>
              <p>
               Alright my lovelies. May you share some meals and find moments of glee this coming week!
              </p>

               <h2 class="article-title">
                <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/2018/07/06/why/" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to Why?">
                 Why?
                </a>
               </h2>
              </div>
              <div class="article-meta article-meta-top">
               <span class="article-category-list article-meta-item">
                Posted in
                <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/category/my-life/" rel="category tag">
                 my life
                </a>
               </span>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="article-content sc" data-role="content">
              <p>
               So why do I blog?
              </p>
              <p>
               I used to be able to answer that. Much like driving in thick fog, my answer to that now is unable to fully reveal itself. I’ve even had thoughts of retiring my blog. Yet something — I don’t know what — holds me back from doing so.
              </p>
              <p>
               The relatives who used to read my blog are now strictly obtaining their photo fix or updates on Facebook. Even friends no longer check out blogs. And hardly anyone seems to email either, never mind a handwritten letter.
              </p>
              <p>
               There are sometimes a handful of ever-so-loyal Internet friends who cheerily pop in to see what’s on tap and, for those lovely souls, I am more grateful than you know.
              </p>
              <p>
               At one point my blog became a business-related online venue and those days of blogging were likely the least interesting for me. I’ve even deleted most of those old posts. For a time I also dabbled in creating thought-provoking topics of interest to hopefully induce some conversation. Those never caught on — maybe it was the timing that wasn’t right because there are huge numbers of blogs doing that very thing these days. Blogging for me is much more casual than that anyway.
              </p>
              <p>
               What I do enjoy is simply being able to post some of my photos — you’re fortunate I don’t post them all! Though admittedly, when I post a few photos to
               <a href="https://www.flickr.com/photos/27458974@N00/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
                Flickr
               </a>
               , there are far more people who take a look. But I’ve never been one of the people who is more concerned about how many people stop in or how many ‘subscribe.’ That has never been one of my motivations to blog. It’s also been helpful to be able to share my happenings or interests here on the blog. In fact, being such a huge proponent of stories, I feel happy when I can share a snippet of my life through #littlestoriesofmylife.
              </p>
              <p>
               Going on nearly two years, I’ve made a point of doing regular Monday posts. That actually had benefits for me. It kept me from  going fallow for months and then occasional posts planted here and there. When I had lots to share I could simply schedule them ahead and they would automatically post on the scheduled date into the future.
              </p>
              <p>
               Perhaps some of my discontent lately stems purely from losing my piano teacher and the search for another. It has not been easy. Perhaps my thoughts of weaning off the blog are influenced by all the things I’m involved in or working on. Yet I am so reluctant to cut the ties completely. Now there’s a topic a psychiatrist might like to delve into.
              </p>
              <p>
               I’m simply writing free fall here. Whatever comes to mind when thinking about this blog. It’s not like I need it for business or promotion purposes. I’m certainly not making money from it — nor has that ever been an intention. I’m also quite dis-pleased that my blog is now part of BlogLovin. It is impossible to undo as I’ve discovered. Yes, completely unable to undo unless I were to take down my blog. The other thing about BlogLovin that annoys me endlessly is how there are multiple strangers who keep subscribing and they are connected with … sex links. BlogLovin actually does not allow me to unsubscribe or block those people — can you believe that? So, you can tell that I have highly charged feelings about BlogLovin and they are not on the positive side of any scale.
              </p>
              <p>
               I’m just unsettled at the moment. Yet I feel I need to connect those thoughts and feelings to those who do stop in. It’s a connection though isn’t it? And it’s likely that connection that binds me to its existence.
              </p>
              <p>
               Is this the end? No. Not yet, anyway. But I’m still considering that possibility.
              </p>
              <p>
              </p>

               </a>
              </p>
              <p>
               I hear people who say they don’t buy local because it costs more. That’s often true, but not always. But there is also a very good reason for the slightly higher price tags. To me the price difference falls away when I consider the increase in local employment as well as the relationships that grow when purchasing from people I have gotten to know. Even greater than that, my main reason for supporting locally grown and made products is that I know what I’m getting. I’m not eating food that was grown in contaminated soil, sprayed endlessly with chemicals, nor spent days being shipped after being plucked pre-maturity. In the case of local meat, it’s raised and grown right here, not fed chemicals, injected with pharmaceuticals, but instead they walk on actual grass in natural conditions eating foods they were meant to eat. That’s my way and I realize not everyone agrees and that’s what makes this world interesting.
              </p>
              <p>
               Taking pride in shopping at my local farmer’s market inspires me to create more in the kitchen.
               <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/2018/06/25/shopping-on-a-main-street-like-mine/diptych-food-2/" rel="attachment wp-att-9032">

               </a>
              </p>
              <p style="text-align: center;">
               {garden fresh and local cheese, above}

               </a>
               {cooking wine 😉 + Herf’s Halibut, above}
              </p>
              <p style="text-align: center;">
               <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/2018/06/25/shopping-on-a-main-street-like-mine/dsc_7040/" rel="attachment wp-att-9050">

          <article class="sc post-9014 post type-post status-publish format-standard has-post-thumbnail hentry category-a-story category-my-life tag-littlestoriesofmylife tag-piano tag-piano-teacher" id="article-9014">
           <div class="article-wrap sc content-bg">
            <div class="article-wrap-inner">
             <div class="article-header normal" data-role="header">
              <span class="article-date article-meta-item">
               <span>
                Monday, June 18, 2018
               </span>
              </span>
              <div class="article-title-wrap">
               <h2 class="article-title">
                <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/2018/06/18/when-theres-a-shift/" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to when there’s a shift">
                 when there’s a shift
                </a>
               </h2>
              </div>
              <div class="article-meta article-meta-top">
               <span class="article-category-list article-meta-item">
                Posted in
                <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/category/a-story/" rel="category tag">
                 A Story
                </a>
                ,
                <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/category/my-life/" rel="category tag">
                 my life
                </a>
               </span>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="article-content sc" data-role="content">
              <p>
               Shifts come in many forms. When tectonic plates within the earth shift, an earthquake occurs, for instance. When the gears on our car shift, we advance to a smoother running gear appropriate for the speed. A mental shift can occur when we change the way we look at things.
              </p>
              <p>
               So why this talk about shifts?
              </p>
              <p>
               <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/2018/06/18/when-theres-a-shift/dsc_0330-2/" rel="attachment wp-att-9020">
                <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9020 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/DSC_0330.jpg" height="591" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
               </a>
              </p>
              <p>
               At my last piano lesson a shift I was not expecting, nor wanting, came to be. My amazing piano teacher advised me that he unfortunately would have to bring the lessons to a close. You see, this handful of wonderful years he has driven at least an hour-and-a-half to come to this part of the island to provide lessons to adults in my area. Over the years, some have died, some have moved into care homes, a couple have moved away. I had not realized that for months, there have only been two of us he has been driving all this way to give Wednesday lessons. Of the two of us the other lady, I have now learned, is no longer able to keep up with the lessons and had given her notice to Keith.
              </p>
              <p style="text-align: center;">
               <em>
                “It’s almost like you’re not tethered to the earth anymore. You’re just flying, nothing but space around you.”
               </em>
               — Riley Hart
              </p>
              <p>
               <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/2018/06/18/when-theres-a-shift/dsc_4916-3/" rel="attachment wp-att-9021">
                <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9021 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/DSC_4916.jpg" height="591" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
               </a>
              </p>
              <p>
               It has pained him to have to share the news that it is no longer feasible for him to drive all this way for one student. Though I completely understand, and even feel guilty that there were only two of us for the past several months, I have been devastated. Receiving the news, initially it was as though an earthquake were occurring within me. It was difficult to keep back the tears and my breath was held hostage in my lungs.
              </p>
              <p style="text-align: center;">
               <em>
                “The wise adapt themselves to circumstances, as water molds itself to the pitcher.”
               </em>
               — Proverb
              </p>
              <p>
               <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/2018/06/18/when-theres-a-shift/dsc_3911/" rel="attachment wp-att-9022">
                <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9022 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/DSC_3911.jpg" height="591" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
               </a>
              </p>
              <p>
               He implored me to go out and look for a piano teacher. One who teaches adults, one who understands and accepts my goals (which do not include learning the Royal Conservatory method taught by nearly all piano teachers). He made me promise I would go out and seek a new teacher. He will come once a month in July and August to ease me through the transition.
              </p>
              <p>
               Although the ground beneath me feels its shifting and it would be so easy to get sucked down into the way it was, I realize I need to take an uncomfortable and risky giant step, and see what happens.
              </p>
              <p>
               <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/2018/06/18/when-theres-a-shift/dsc_1596/" rel="attachment wp-att-9023">
                <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9023 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/DSC_1596.jpg" height="591" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
               </a>
              </p>
              <p>
               For some, you may wonder why on earth this is so ground shattering for me. As I’ve explained in the past, learning to play piano has been a lifelong dream — my personal bucket list — and I was fortunate the perfect piano teacher came into my life at the best possible time. He’s taken me from not knowing how to read a single musical note to actually playing a variety of beautiful tunes. He’s given me the tools to learn, in a gratifying way, and with so much patience, yet he always has pushed me to grow and realize what I’m capable of doing with those black and white keys. He’s been an important part of my life these past years.
              </p>
              <p>
               <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/2018/06/18/when-theres-a-shift/dsc_3044-2/" rel="attachment wp-att-9024">
                <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9024 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/DSC_3044.jpg" height="591" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
               </a>
              </p>
              <p>
               So now, as we all do when an unexpected — and unwanted — shift moves the earth beneath us, I’m reaching and taking the first tentative steps towards whatever comes next. It’s been a few days since the news. The panic has disappeared. I’m now open to what will come and, perhaps, the door that opens next will take me to a level I had not yet entertained. Perhaps the changes shall surprise me in ways I’ve not yet dreamed.
              </p>
              <p>
               Tell me, has an unexpected shift in your life taken you somewhere surprising, wonderful, or beyond your own dreams?
              </p>
              <p>
               <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/2018/01/15/downsized/dsc_6053-2/" rel="attachment wp-att-8315">
                <img alt="Loving Downsizing" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-8315 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/12/DSC_6053.jpg" height="500" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="700"/>
               </a>
              </p>

          <article class="sc post-9001 post type-post status-publish format-standard has-post-thumbnail hentry category-a-story category-environment-tips-sustainability category-my-life tag-littlestoriesofmylife tag-minimalism tag-ocean tag-plastic" id="article-9001">
           <div class="article-wrap sc content-bg">
            <div class="article-wrap-inner">
             <div class="article-header normal" data-role="header">
              <span class="article-date article-meta-item">
               <span>
                Monday, June 11, 2018
               </span>
              </span>
              <div class="article-title-wrap">
               <h2 class="article-title">
                <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/2018/06/11/let-go/" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to the pond that we are in">
                 the pond that we are in
                </a>
               </h2>
              </div>
              <div class="article-meta article-meta-top">
               <span class="article-category-list article-meta-item">
                Posted in
                <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/category/a-story/" rel="category tag">
                 A Story
                </a>
                ,
                <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/category/environment-tips-sustainability/" rel="category tag">
                 Environment-Tips-Sustainability
                </a>
                ,
                <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/category/my-life/" rel="category tag">
                 my life
                </a>
               </span>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="article-content sc" data-role="content">
              <p>
               <em>
                What’s one thing you’ve let go recently?
               </em>
              </p>
              <p>
               That’s a question asked by the
               <a href="https://www.theminimalists.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
                Minimalists
               </a>
               on their Twitter feed this past week. I was able to recall three things I let go a week earlier, though the letting go was pretty easy.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9004 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/DSC_6996.jpg" height="591" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               I couldn’t help but read some of the responses (over 150 when I stumbled upon the question). Naturally, I was thinking of material things that people may have let go but was pleasantly surprised to read responses such as:
              </p>
              <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
               <span style="font-family: -webkit-standard; font-size: medium;">
                “I let go the idea that everything needs to be perfect before taking any action.”
               </span>
              </p>
              <div style="padding-left: 30px;">
               <span style="font-family: -webkit-standard; font-size: medium;">
                “My television. It’s been gone for a few weeks and I don’t miss it a bit.”
               </span>
              </div>
              <div style="padding-left: 30px;">
               <span style="font-family: -webkit-standard; font-size: medium;">
                “facebook”
               </span>
              </div>
              <div style="padding-left: 30px;">
               <span style="font-family: -webkit-standard; font-size: medium;">
                “baskets — they were a problem masquerading as a solution.”
               </span>
              </div>
              <div style="padding-left: 30px;">
               <span style="font-family: -webkit-standard; font-size: medium;">
                “sugar”
               </span>
              </div>
              <div style="padding-left: 30px;">
               <span style="font-family: -webkit-standard; font-size: medium;">
                “eating out”
               </span>
              </div>
              <div style="padding-left: 30px;">
               <span style="font-family: Times; font-size: medium;">
                “
                <span style="color: #14171a;">
                 Other people’s expectations. I realized I’m not happy trying to be the person other people think I should be.
                </span>
               </span>
               <span style="color: #14171a; font-family: Times; font-size: medium;">
                ”
               </span>
              </div>
              <div style="padding-left: 30px;">
               <span style="font-family: Times; font-size: medium;">
                <span style="color: #14171a;">
                 “subscriptions
                </span>
               </span>
               <span style="color: #14171a; font-family: Times; font-size: medium;">
                ”
               </span>
              </div>
              <p>
               Yes, many people listed material things they had let go. It was the multitude of responses from those who had more interesting and even profound statements of their letting go.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9005 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/DSC_6997.jpg" height="890" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               I can understand how life-changing it can be entering a new chapter in life, such as choosing to clear out the clutter, both material and emotional. I’ve certainly felt far more at-ease — rejuvenated really — since clearing out boxes of things that never got looked at, never mind used; duplicates of anything; clothes I never wore; decor items that gave me no pleasure; and so on. Since our nearly 100% purge last year, I feel a huge weight off my shoulders. It was as if the material things held me down, as if the excess was holding me back from feeling free and unencumbered. So when I read some of those responses, I understood how they came to make those decisions and how very good it feels to free yourself of the things (whether they are material or emotional) that weigh a person down.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9006 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/DSC_7000.jpg" height="890" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               <em>
                “We can’t be afraid of change. You may feel very secure in the pond that you are in, but if you never venture out of it, you will never know that there is such a thing as an ocean, a sea. Holding onto something …, may be the very reason why you don’t have something better.”
               </em>
               — C. JoyBell C.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9007 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/DSC_7004.jpg" height="890" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               And as I’m writing this in advance, it is
               <a href="http://www.worldoceansday.org/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
                World Ocean Day
               </a>
               . Many of you know I’ve been a decades-long proponent of the reduce-reuse-recycle philosophy. Probably part of the reason why minimalism has come so easily to me. I read a statement by former Olympian Silken Laumen on how she is making a public pledge to do better in the coming year, beginning on World Ocean Day. Reading her pledge inspired me to also declare my plan to do better going forward. We already recycle everything we possibly can and in the past year have made a significant jump towards reducing our use/purchase of things plastic. But I can do better. So I have just ordered a variety of cotton bags in a range of sizes that will be kept in our reusable shopping bags. The little cotton ones will now hold any produce we otherwise would have put into the store-provided plastic produce bags. But that’s not all. Because so much plastic ends up in the ocean, I also plan to ask every single restaurant we go to in the coming year if they will consider committing to reducing their use of one-time-use plastics. If the people they depend on for their business begin asking that one single question, perhaps it will at least get them to think about it. So that’s my little pledge.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-9008 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/DSC_6639.jpg" height="591" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               So my lovelies, what’s one thing you’ve let go recently?
              </p>

          <article class="sc post-8991 post type-post status-publish format-standard has-post-thumbnail hentry category-a-story category-my-life tag-littlestoriesofmylife tag-floribunda tag-jump-for-joy tag-mary-oliver tag-poem tag-rose" id="article-8991">
           <div class="article-wrap sc content-bg">
            <div class="article-wrap-inner">
             <div class="article-header normal" data-role="header">
              <span class="article-date article-meta-item">
               <span>
                Monday, June 4, 2018
               </span>
              </span>
              <div class="article-title-wrap">
               <h2 class="article-title">
                <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/2018/06/04/a-sweet-apple-fragrance/" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to a sweet apple fragrance">
                 a sweet apple fragrance
                </a>
               </h2>
              </div>
              <div class="article-meta article-meta-top">
               <span class="article-category-list article-meta-item">
                Posted in
                <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/category/a-story/" rel="category tag">
                 A Story
                </a>
                ,
                <a href="http://www.dianeschuller.com/category/my-life/" rel="category tag">
                 my life
                </a>
               </span>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="article-content sc" data-role="content">
              <p>
               <em>
                “I like this place and could willingly waste my time in it.”
               </em>
               –William Shakespeare’s words
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-8992 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/DSC_6940.jpg" height="591" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               Lusciously lovely, showy clusters of large delectable peachy-pink, long-lived flowers. Round bushy super-flowerful habit, loads of glossy green leaves and consistent dark red new growth. Now, if you read this description, found on a tag from Weeks Roses, wouldn’t that intrigue and draw you in? I spied a single bloom among the multiple flowers at one of our local greenhouses, striding over to determine if that single beauty had a fragrance. The sad thing about roses these days, is that they’ve bred out fragrance in favour of longevity and other marketable traits. Much to my delight, the lone bloom had a sweet mild apple fragrance. I was smitten.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-8993 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/DSC_6944.jpg" height="591" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               I’ve never owned a rose, at least not the ‘fancy’ ones. Originating and living most of my life on the northern prairies of Canada, the only rose I was able to grow is an old reliable bush rose that could survive the extreme winters, a Hansa rose. It’s a prolific bloomer emitting a strong clove-like fragrance though it’s a bit of a gnarly tangle of a bush and as rugged as the prairie immigrants. It’s like having a sweet smelling mongrel: not a show piece in the garden but pays dividends through the fragrance wafting amid the yard from its long-lived flowers.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-8994 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/Screen-Shot-2018-06-03-at-9.08.31-AM.png" height="629" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="818"/>
              </p>
              <p>
               No flower is more studied or beloved than the rose. Poetry, songs, and legend have long adopted the showy rose. Take a trip to photography sites such as Flickr, Instagram and the like and you’ll find the ancient rose takes its place firmly as a standard. It’s a ubiquitous subject for photographers. Perhaps that is partly why I have resisted owning a rose. But on that day, something deep within me urged me to take that rose home. I cannot tell you how smitten I am with that beautiful lady of the rose world. She has a name and pedigree. She’s a Floribunda named
               <em>
                Jump for Joy
               </em>
               and is the offspring of
               <em>
                Julie Newmar
               </em>
               and
               <em>
                Julia Child
               </em>
               . Apparently, according to the information I found about her she even has a sibling; she’s sister of
               <em>
                Sparkle &amp; Shine
               </em>
               .
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-8995 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/DSC_6955.jpg" height="591" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
               Every single time I am at the piano, sitting in the living room, or walking towards that window, I am nearly startled at her beauty and colour. She’s in full bloom at the moment, every bud gloriously open. The moment I step outside her apple fragrance captivates me. She speaks and I pay attention.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-8996 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/DSC_6950.jpg" height="591" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p style="padding-left: 60px;">
               WHEN THE ROSES SPEAK, I PAY ATTENTION
               <br/>
               by Mary Oliver from
               <span style="text-decoration: underline;">
                Thirst
               </span>
               (Beacon Press)
              </p>
              <p style="padding-left: 60px;">
               “As long as we are able to
               <br/>
               be extravagant we will be
               <br/>
               hugely and damply
               <br/>
               extravagant. Then we will drop
               <br/>
               foil by foil to the ground. This
               <br/>
               is our unalterable task, joyfully.”
              </p>
              <p style="padding-left: 60px;">
               And they went on, “Listen,
               <br/>
               the heart-shackles are not as you think,
               <br/>
               death, illness, pain,
               <br/>
               unrequited hope, not loneliness, but
              </p>
              <p style="padding-left: 60px;">
               lassitude, rue, vainglory, fear, anxiety,
               <br/>
               selfishness.”
              </p>
              <p style="padding-left: 60px;">
               Their fragrance all the while rising
               <br/>
               from their blind bodies, making me
               <br/>
               spin with joy.
              </p>
              <p>
               <img alt="" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-8997 ov-done" data-lazyload-src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/DSC_6951.jpg" height="591" src="http://www.dianeschuller.com/wp-content/themes/prophoto4/images/blank.gif" width="890"/>
              </p>
              <p style="padding-left: 60px;">
               <em>
                The roses laughed softly. “Forgive us,”
               </em>
               <br/>
               <em>
                they said. “But as you can see, we are
               </em>
               <br/>
               <em>
                just now entirely busy being roses.”
               </em>
               … excerpt from “Roses” by Mary Oliver
              </p>
              <p>
              </p>
