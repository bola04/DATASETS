        <h2>
         Explore the fascinating history of Madeley, Shropshire, England
        </h2>
        <p>
         Madeley is situated in the south of the East Shropshire new town of Telford, approximately forty miles west of Birmingham, a couple of miles north of the somewhat better known township of Ironbridge. (Prior to the building of the Iron Bridge, and for many years after, most of the Ironbridge area was known as “Madeley Wood”.)
        </p>
        <p>
         Originally a thriving market and industrial town in its own right, Madeley has been somewhat submerged by the growth of the surrounding new town developments of Sutton Hill and Woodside, which together with other new housing built since the late 1960s, and the historic core of Madeley itself, make up the modern parish of Madeley. The many wooded pit mounts around the town are a reminder of its former importance as a mining town.
        </p>
        <p>
         The Local Studies Group worked hard to gather this collection of personal memories and factual accounts. Unfortunately the group is no longer meeting (2016). A number of personal opinions are expressed within the site and some may seem controversial – they are the views of some longstanding Madeley residents and not of any ‘official’ body!
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          New venture
         </strong>
         – the website has just been updated (September 2016) to avoid having to use out of date software – thanks to
         <a href="http://www.runtimeuk.com/" itemprop="url">
          Runtime of Wellington
         </a>
         , Telford! Tidying and updating of the content will be happening soon, along with some new content.
        </p>

        <h3>
         Mining
        </h3>
        <p>
         <img alt="small book" border="0" class="fleft" height="60" itemprop="image" src="http://madeleylocalhistory.org/wp-content/uploads/small-book.jpg" width="75"/>
         The Madeley Wood Colliery, as was common elsewhere in coal mining, actually consisted of two separate pits — the Halesfield (
         <i>
          circa
         </i>
         1861) and Kemberton (1864) Collieries, which were subsequently connected underground in 1939. The Halesfield shafts reached approximately 300 yards, the Kemberton pit was deeper at 364 yards. The whole colliery site, the former spoil heaps and the extensive railway sidings were levelled on closure in 1967 and the area is now a major industrial estate — Halesfield.
        </p>

        <h3>
         Railways
        </h3>
        <p>
         <img alt="small book" border="0" class="fleft" height="60" itemprop="image" src="http://madeleylocalhistory.org/wp-content/uploads/small-book.jpg" width="75"/>
         The Coalport branch line, the main railway to serve Madeley for nearly a hundred years, came about as the result of an early Shropshire “new town” – the building of the nearby village of Coalport around 1794 to serve as an inland port for traffic on the River Severn and the Shropshire Canal.
        </p>
        <p>
         <img alt="Tunnel at Blists Hill" class="alignright" height="152" itemprop="image" src="http://madeleylocalhistory.org/wp-content/uploads/tunnel-small.jpg" width="200"/>
         The idea was for the products of the East Shropshire coalfield to be sent downriver to the ports of Gloucester and Bristol, in the famous “Severn Trows” (large shallow draught sailing barges, hauled by teams of either horses or men for much of the upstream journey), whilst other goods could be brought upstream on the return trip. Coalport itself had a famous china works, and was also the home of an early chemical works – Lord Dundonald’s Tar Distillery, initially using tar from the “Tar Tunnel” at Coalport as a raw material.
        </p>
        <p>
         <a href="http://madeleylocalhistory.org/railways.html" itemprop="url" title="Railways in Madeley">
          Read more …
         </a>
        </p>

        <p>
         <img alt="small book" border="0" class="fleft" height="60" itemprop="image" src="http://madeleylocalhistory.org/wp-content/uploads/small-book.jpg" width="75"/>

         <a href="http://madeleylocalhistory.org/newtown.html" itemprop="url" title="The Coming of the New Town">
          ‘The Coming of the New Town’
         </a>
         happened just over 30 years ago — well within living memory. To many people living locally, this memory is clear, painful and liable to arouse anger.
        </p>
        <p>
         Most of the sources are contemporary with the events — news items and articles from the then local weekly newspaper,
         <i>
          The Dawley Observer,
         </i>
         official reports from the Dawley, later Telford Development Corporation endorsed by the official history of the New Town, written by Maurice de Soissons in 1991. Other sources are the words and memories of Madeley people as recorded in
         <i>
          ‘The Madeley Book’
         </i>
         , which was also published in 1991 and as collected now.
        </p>
