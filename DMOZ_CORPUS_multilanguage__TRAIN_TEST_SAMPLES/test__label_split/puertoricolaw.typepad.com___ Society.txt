          <h2 class="date-header">
           11/20/2009
          </h2>
          <div class="entry-category-business_coaching entry-author-robert_alex_fleming entry-has-title entry-type-post entry" id="entry-6a00d83451bad469e20120a6bcac5f970b">
           <h3 class="entry-header">
            <a href="http://puertoricolaw.typepad.com/doing_business_in_puerto_/2009/11/4-tactics-to-hire-long-term-committed-employees.html">
             4 Tactics to Hire  Long Term Committed Employees
            </a>
           </h3>
           <div class="entry-content">
            <div class="entry-body">
             <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt; font-weight: normal;">
                By the ActionCOACH Team
                <br/>
               </span>
              </strong>
             </p>
             <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt; font-weight: normal;">
                <br/>
               </span>
              </strong>
             </p>
             <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt; font-weight: normal;">
                Do you know that  2 out of 3 new hires prove to be a mistake within the first year?  It's a cost of thousands of dollars for each departing employee.
                <br/>
               </span>
              </strong>
             </p>
             <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt; font-weight: normal;">
                Hiring mistakes negatively
 impact productivity and erode your company’s profits. In Jim Collins’ landmark
 study and book “Good to Great” he states: “The good-to-great leaders understood
 three simple truths.”
                <o:p>
                </o:p>
               </span>
              </strong>
             </p>
             <ol start="1" style="margin-top: 0in;" type="1">
              <li class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
               <strong>
                <span style="font-size: 11pt; font-weight: normal;">
                 “If
  you begin with ‘who’, rather than ‘what’, you can more easily adapt to a
  changing world”
                 <o:p>
                 </o:p>
                </span>
               </strong>
              </li>
              <li class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
               <strong>
                <span style="font-size: 11pt; font-weight: normal;">
                 “If
  you have the right people on the bus, the problem of how to motivate and
  manage people largely goes away”
                 <o:p>
                 </o:p>
                </span>
               </strong>
              </li>
              <li class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
               <strong>
                <span style="font-size: 11pt; font-weight: normal;">
                 “If
  you have the wrong people, it doesn’t matter whether you discover the
  right direction; you still won’t have a great company.”
                </span>
               </strong>
              </li>
             </ol>
             <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt; font-weight: normal;">
                What would your business
 look like if your hiring system delivered the right person 75% of the
 time?
                <span>
                </span>
                Here are some tips to stack the
 odds in your favor.
                <o:p>
                </o:p>
               </span>
              </strong>
             </p>
             <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt;">
                Create thorough job descriptions
               </span>
              </strong>
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt; font-weight: normal;">
                <o:p>
                </o:p>
               </span>
              </strong>
             </p>
             <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt; font-weight: normal;">
                Most business leaders use
 job descriptions to insure the candidate understands the positional duties and
 responsibilities; but here’s where many fall short. A good job description
 should also include a list of the skills and competencies required to perform
 the tasks and duties expected and critical objectives. Many job descriptions
 simply state duties but you need pry deeper and outline what is a priority so all
 team members.
                <span>
                </span>
                This will help keep you
 team
                <span>
                </span>
                focused on what you want, facilitate
 quick hiring when the need arises, and communicate your expectations to
 candidates and current employees.
                <em>
                </em>
                <o:p>
                </o:p>
               </span>
              </strong>
             </p>
             <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt;">
                Market for employees, just like customers
               </span>
              </strong>
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt; font-weight: normal;">
                <o:p>
                </o:p>
               </span>
              </strong>
             </p>
             <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt; font-weight: normal;">
                Recruiting, like
 marketing, is most effective when it is targeted, planned and executed with
 consistency.
                <span>
                </span>
                Start with the basic
 question, “
                <em>
                 What makes your company a
 great choice for potential employees
                </em>
                ?” Having a written Culture Statement
 that is alive and well will help you to answer this question. Cross checking
 your Culture Statement with your employees’ experience should be a regular
 practice. Now is a good time to ask your current team – they’ll tell you.
                <o:p>
                </o:p>
               </span>
              </strong>
             </p>
             <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt; font-weight: normal;">
                Next, identify the best
 ways to reach potential employees for the position you are hiring. It will likely
 vary based on the position. Develop the ‘advertisement’ to attract them and be
 creative. The rules of good advertising apply – great heading, good content and
 a call to action. Include the aspects of your company that make it a great
 place for the type of person you wish to attract, as well as the “spirit” of
 the person you desire. Additionally, always be on the lookout for good people who
 can bring value to your organization. Make recruiting something that is ongoing,
 not crisis driven!
                <span>
                </span>
                Remember “Good to
 Great”.
                <span>
                </span>
                <span>
                </span>
                <o:p>
                </o:p>
               </span>
              </strong>
             </p>
             <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt;">
                Make hiring a De-Selection Process
               </span>
              </strong>
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt; font-weight: normal;">
                <o:p>
                </o:p>
               </span>
              </strong>
             </p>
             <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt; font-weight: normal;">
                Your time is valuable! Don’t
 waste it interviewing potential employees who don’t possess the character
 qualities you need or don’t fit into your culture. You can improve your results
 and save time by incorporating a de-selection process, using a phone screen and
 group pre-interview assessment to test for integrity, work ethic, and passion.
                <span>
                </span>
                If you design the group pre-interview
 assessment properly, those that have the “fire in the oven” will stand out and
 those who don’t will leave (de-select themselves), saving you time and hiring
 mistakes.
                <o:p>
                 <br/>
                </o:p>
               </span>
              </strong>
             </p>
             <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt; font-weight: normal;">
                Your written Vision,
 Mission, and Culture statements are a valuable tool at this point to assist in
 the de-selection process. During the group pre-interview meeting use these
 tools with the position’s KPIs to communicate the high standards of behavior
 and performance expected by all within your company. If you effectively
 communicate these criteria, those that you would not want on your team will depart,
 leaving you with the “cream of the crop”.
                <o:p>
                </o:p>
               </span>
              </strong>
             </p>
             <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt; font-weight: normal;">
                What are you looking for
 at this stage? – Passion, confidence, and the desire to grow. These attributes are
 much more important than knowledge or experience, which can be taught. Stay
 disciplined and only interview candidates who make it through both the phone
 and group interview steps.
                <o:p>
                </o:p>
               </span>
              </strong>
             </p>
             <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt; font-weight: normal;">
                Once you decide to
 schedule someone for an interview, email them the job description so expectations
 are clear before they arrive. Have the candidate meet with multiple people,
 when possible, and employ behavioral interview techniques and tests to probe
 for the skills and competencies that are most critical. Be willing to turn down
 candidates who are not the right fit and don’t settle for less than the best!
                <span>
                </span>
                <o:p>
                </o:p>
               </span>
              </strong>
             </p>
             <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt;">
                Believe You Can and You Will
               </span>
              </strong>
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt; font-weight: normal;">
                <o:p>
                </o:p>
               </span>
              </strong>
             </p>
             <p class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
              <strong>
               <span style="font-size: 11pt; font-weight: normal;">
                Sounds simple, but too
 often we defeat ourselves before we start. Telling yourself ‘I just can’t get
 good help’ becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy. Instead, start telling yourself
 “I have a great team committed to achieving our goals”. Even if it’s not yet
 true – YOU can make it so. Remember the objective when hiring is to be able to
 say 3 years later that, “Knowing what I know now, I’d hire this person again in
 a heartbeat!”
                <o:p>
                </o:p>
               </span>
              </strong>
             </p>
            </div>

          <h2 class="date-header">
           08/23/2009
          </h2>
          <div class="entry-author-robert_alex_fleming entry-has-title entry-type-post entry" id="entry-6a00d83451bad469e20120a56b82dd970c">
           <h3 class="entry-header">
            <a href="http://puertoricolaw.typepad.com/doing_business_in_puerto_/2009/08/recession-proof-your-business.html">
             Recession-Proof Your Business
            </a>
           </h3>
           <div class="entry-content">
            <div class="entry-body">
             <p>
              It's about how to survive and thrive, no matter what the economic outlook.
             </p>
             <p>
              Why some businesses thrive while others implode during an economic recession remains a puzzle to many business-owning entrepreneurs. Some mistakenly assume that all businesses must suffer through recessionary cycles. But the fact is that some companies are essentially recession-proof, and it is not necessarily because they are bigger,
              <br/>
              better known, or more generously capitalized.
             </p>
             <p>
              What really separates the successful enterprises from those that collapse during down cycles is that they are organized around a uniquely dynamic and healthier approach to business. They plan for the future and have contingencies in place to confront whatever challenges might arise. They sell products and services that do not go out of fashion as easily; and they focus on profits in an intelligent manner that helps them grow and prosper in both good and bad economic environments.
             </p>
             <p>
              Here are some ideas for creating a company that will greet a recession successfully:
             </p>
             <p>
              · Set, measure, and consistently monitor inventory targets, sales goals, and revenues. But make profit – net profit – the main overriding focus.
             </p>
             <p>
              · Weed out unprofitable accounts and lackluster ventures, services, campaigns, or products. Reward top performers and either reposition those who are not contributing to make them valuable or let them go for the benefit of the overall team.
             </p>
             <p>
              · Keep in close touch with customers and track their demographic patterns and consumer preferences. The better one knows his or her customers, the better one understands one’s own business.
             </p>
             <p>
              · Develop strategies to attract new customers, retain existing customers, and sell more products or services to each and every customer. Grow the customer base but also grow net profits in order to capture tangible bottom line benefits.
             </p>
             <p>
              · Advertise and market aggressively but constantly refine and redefine the campaigns to strengthen the brand and enhance the equity of the brand name to give it greater power, meaning, and recognition.
             </p>
             <p>
              Excerpt from ActionCOACH's white paper "Recession Proof Your Business".
