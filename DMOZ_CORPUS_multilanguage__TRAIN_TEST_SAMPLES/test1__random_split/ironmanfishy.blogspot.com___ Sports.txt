             <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
              <a href="http://ironmanfishy.blogspot.com/2010/11/ouch.html">
               Ouch
              </a>
             </h3>
             <div class="post-header-line-1">
             </div>
             <div class="post-body entry-content">
              It's been a couple months since I raced hard at a trail event.  I felt that big time on Saturday.  Started the Wild Hare 25k race at a comfy clip, which on any of my previous year's events would have been easy to build from, however that "build" never really came to visit.  I had a collegiate runner on my heals for the first part of the race, and never quite shook her off.  I was looking at this girl, who weighed about as my as my right pinky and thought to myself, "I don't even think I weighed that much when my momma gave birth to me!"  Anyhoot, she was persistent and I knew I had a race on my hands.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              My mind kept telling me to not worry as age, wisdom, and experience would give me the upper hand here today.  Instead, they gave me the back hand on the last loop during the last section.  My build was petering, my body was heating up way too much from the weather, and my legs were starting to shut down big time.  I watched the little Div I college runner spring away across the field.  All I had to do now was just run it in as strong as I could, all be it strong as I could for the last 3 miles was not something to write home about.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              I clocked a 2 hr 25k run, and I was done.  Nothing more in me and nothing left out there.  It was good to go through this race, as I needed the ass kicking and the mental exercise.  Too many times I'm left to my own gauge, and it was good to actually have a head to head race for most of the day. This is the kind of situation that only lends to better races down the path.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              <div style="text-align: center;">
               <a href="http://www.facebook.com/photo.php?pid=3539732&amp;id=622968056" id="myphotolink">
                <img height="720" id="myphoto" src="http://sphotos.ak.fbcdn.net/hphotos-ak-snc4/hs1190.snc4/154223_481165538056_622968056_5453370_3759564_n.jpg" width="479"/>
               </a>
              </div>
              <div align="center">
               <em>
                Photo courtesy of John Chung﻿
               </em>
              </div>
              <br/>
              I'm taking it easy the next couple days, as my body was just completely wiped out after last week and this race.  I'm learning more and more how to respect that and allow the healing time each race requires, since each one is different.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              On a really kick ass note my training partner Louie ROCKED IT out there.  Overall 25k winner and had a smashing great run.  My hubby also won the 10k only weeks after knee surgery, so kudos to both of my boys!
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Happy Thanksgiving to all.  See you in Huntsville come December.
              <br/>
              Cheers,
              <br/>
              Fish

             <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
              <a href="http://ironmanfishy.blogspot.com/2010/10/my-new-bucket-in-hand.html">
               My new bucket in hand.
              </a>
             </h3>
             <div class="post-header-line-1">
             </div>
             <div class="post-body entry-content">
              I woke up to some cooler temperatures this morning.  I experienced the same thing a few weeks back, here in Austin, but just as I was starting to get frisky for some cooler run workouts the heat and humidity crept back.  This morning I was reminded of that wonderful feeling, where the coolness in the air inspires me to wanna run!  I'm looking forward to winter in Austin this year.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              It's been an interesting year.  One that has had it's ups and it's downs.  One that threw me some curve balls and hugs.  It's been one hell of a year that I will never forget, that's for sure!  I've been going thru some self reflection these past few weeks.  Trying to figure out what Andrea Fisher is going to do with the next step in her life, in her athletic career.  What is Fish going to put on the plate to keep her going, allow her to be the person she really is inside?  I came to the final answer, even though I pretty much knew this answer after I stepped into my first trail run about 5 years ago.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              I started out as a full fledged "Sprint Wimp".  Yep, that term is affectionately given to any sprint freestyle swimmer who only does 50's and maybe the occasional 100 race.  My race stage took anywhere from 20 sec to 50 seconds to finish.  I was built, trained and groomed to perform like a speeding bullet.  I was a Lady Longhorn NCAA Div I champion because of this ability.  I could explode off the starting block, lay down a 50 and 100 freestyle like few could, and I could touch that wall faster then most women at the time.  My training workout never lasted very long, while the other swimmers logged hundreds of meters.  I loved my Sprint Wimp title, and if you asked me at the time if I would be able to run a marathon I would have laughed my ass off and called you insane.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Then I moved on, and did what most post collegiate female athletes do at this point - WORKOUT SO THEY DON'T GET FAT.  I didn't want to be a member of the chub club, and I was purely burnt out on structured and competitive swimming.  Easiest thing I can think of is to try and run.  Apparently the word "try" is hardly an understatement when I say this.  I went for a run with one of my friends, barely got 1 mile logged, and about DIED.  I didn't even know how I was going to make it back to my car.  1 mile, that's it.  Legs shot, feet killing me, wheezing unstoppable, and I had to get back to the car.  WTF?!?!?! I was a champion athlete, and this was the last thing I needed to experience on my quest not to own a muffin top.  I swore right then and there I was going to become a better runner.  I would be able to run 2 miles by the end of this all if it killed me (at that point this wasn't too far off from happening).
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Fast forward a couple years and I find myself swimming again, riding a bike and able to run 6 miles.  WOW! But for some reason this wasn't enough.  I had to pick something extreme, something so outlandish that my friends and family would think I was on crack to try and do it.  I picked Ironman, and soon found myself entering a whole new world and lifestyle that would define the next 14 years of my life.  I became a professional triathlete, competing at Ironman races around the world and experiencing something that Sprint Wimp would have never pictured herself doing.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Triathlon provided me with my life, my work, my business, my husband, my friends, and my lifestyle.  I would never trade a moment of it for anything.  Yet, during the last few years I found my bucket becoming more empty.  What's "my bucket" one might ask?  I define my bucket as the place I go to, reach in to, and dig from when I need to push myself beyond normal capabilities.  That bucket holds the secret to racing success, so I'm able to push myself beyond anything that I would have ever thought possible.  That bucket has won me many a hard race, and aided in performances I would never imagine.  But the bucket can only hold so much, and only for one sport at a time.  Over the last couple years I've come to realize that the bucket was becoming empty as far as professional Ironman racing goes.  I had nothing to pull out of it when I needed to.  I could see the bottom.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Then on a whim, I entered a trail race.  No previous trail running experience other then my perfectly groomed Lady Bird Lake Trail runs.  Nope, I went into this race cold turkey and figured I would do pretty well.  Hell, as far as I was concerned I was an Ironman athlete who could do anything.  A trail race would be a joke to my ability.  Let's just say that by the end of this 25k trail race I was balling my eyes out, figuring out how to quit in the middle of nowhere, and wanted to have my daddy hug me and tell me it would be ok.  I crossed the finish line, sulked in the corner for awhile, and then realized something.....that trail race just took me back to that mile I could barely finish.  I had suddenly been given a new goal.  Just like I promised I was going to be able to run 2 miles all the way back to my car I WAS GOING TO DO THIS RACE AGAIN AND OWN IT!  I was just handed a new bucket with the words "Trail Racing" on it.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              I've since then been holding my two buckets, one in each hand.  One labeled "Long Course Professional Triathlon Racing" and the other "Trail Racing".  I've only dipped my hand into that trail bucket a couple times, and you know what has happened when I do?  I did some amazing things I would have never imagined possible.  I won events, set records, ran further then I ever thought possible for me, plus I have passion and inspiration for each and every trail event I enter.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              I'm ending the 2010 year by setting my triathlon bucket down, just within reach so I can still do a handful of events here and there.  Just short course stuff to keep things interesting and fun.  However I am now holding on to, with both hand firmly grasped, my trail bucket.  I'm looking into it and it's extremely deep and heavy, with no bottom in site.  I'm grasping at my ability as a professional athlete to take this to a higher level with new priority and focus.
              <br/>
              <br/>
              I am Andrea Fisher, and I am now a full time trail runner.  Watch me soar!
              <br/>
              <br/>
              Cheers,
              <br/>
              Fish
