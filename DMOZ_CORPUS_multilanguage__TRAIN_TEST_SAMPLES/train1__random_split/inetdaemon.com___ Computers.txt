             <div class="inetd-postcontent">
              <p>
               InetDaemon.Com started seeing an increase in fake registrations at the site, around 10-20 daily up from around 1-2 per week.  Periodically, I go back and clean out old unused accounts.  This is just part of routine maintenance and security any site using a dynamic web application like WordPress should be doing.  More than 99% of these bogus accounts were never used, so this means that the people (or bots) didn’t sign up to use the site, but registered for another purpose.   Since we don’t sell anything here or take credit card numbers, I can only assume they want to harvest the local logins and email addresses, which are the only thing we have stored here. I have shut down the membership registration functions temporarily until I can figure out a solution that is so unpalatable to the spammers and other criminals that it’s not worth their time.
              </p>

                Autonomous System Number tutorial updated

              </h1>
              <div class="inetd-postheadericons inetd-metadata-icons">

              <h1 class="inetd-postheader">
               <a href="https://www.inetdaemon.com/technology/diy-vmwarew-whitebox/" rel="bookmark" title="DIY VMWare Whitebox">
                DIY VMWare Whitebox
               </a>
              </h1>
              <div class="inetd-postheadericons inetd-metadata-icons">
               <span class="inetd-postdateicon">
                <span class="date">
                 Published
                </span>
                <span class="entry-date" title="11:41 pm">
                 January 25, 2014
                </span>
               </span>
               |
               <span class="inetd-postauthoricon">
                <span class="author">
                 By
                </span>
                <span class="author vcard">
                 <a class="url fn n" href="https://www.inetdaemon.com/author/inetdaemon/" title="View all posts by InetDaemon">
                  InetDaemon
                 </a>
                </span>
               </span>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="inetd-postcontent">
              <p style="text-align: left;">
               Back in July this year, I wanted to build a virtual lab for self training purposes but I didn’t have the cash required to build what VMWare considers a minimal lab on hardware that VMWare has certified.  Server  hardware is
               <em>
                expensive
               </em>
               .
              </p>
              <p>
               I knew that VMWare’s basic server has always been free, albeit without support, and will run on most Intel compatible hardware. The trick with VMWare is whether it will pass certain hardware features, such as access to components plugged into the PCI express bus, such as the video card functions.  This is where IOMMU comes in, and finding a motherboard that supports IOMMU is difficult, because manufacturers don’t want you to know that a $130 desktop motherboard has the same features as their $900 server motherboard.
              </p>
              <h2>
               Whitebox Hardware List
              </h2>
              <p>
               Here’s the hardware I selected:
              </p>
              <ul>
               <li>
                <strong>
                 $179.00 – CPU:
                </strong>
                AMD FX-8350
               </li>
               <li>
                <strong>
                 $139.00 – Motherboard:
                </strong>
                Gigabyte 990FXA-UD3
               </li>
               <li>
                <strong>
                 $139.00 – RAM:
                </strong>
                16 GB, 1600 Mhz (8 CAS Latency)
               </li>
               <li>
                <strong>
                 $179.00 – VIDEO:
                </strong>
                Gigabyte GEFORCE GTX 660 Ti Boost w/2GB GDDR5
               </li>
               <li>
                <strong>
                 $239.00 – SSD HD:
                </strong>
                256 GB SATA III (6Gbps) SSD OCX Vector3, 100k IOPS random read/write
               </li>
               <li>
                <strong>
                 $ 59.00 – HD:
                </strong>
                Toshiba 1 TB 7200 RPM drive
               </li>
               <li>
                <strong>
                 $149.00 – CASE:
                </strong>
                NZXT Phantom (White)
               </li>
              </ul>
              <p>
               <strong>
                CPU:  AMD FX-8350 – $179
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               The AMD FX-8350 is an 8-core, multi-threaded CPU which will give me more VM’s and is cheaper than the Intel CPU with the same performance specs. It’s on the new Vishera 32nm fabrication process and cooler than the previous generation of this architecture. First, I’m using Windows to do an initial burn-in test and test the components on the OS they were designed for.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                Motherboard: Gigabyte 990FXZ-UD3
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               The Gigabyte 990FXA-UD3 supports up to 32 GB of RAM, and IOMMU for pass-through of PCIe components into VMWare, and up to four PCIe video cards, if you want gaming FPS.  I just want a basic server for learning virtualization. With 32 GB of RAM, I can fit seven VM’s on this box comfortably with a host OS, Enough for a small lab of computers.  Revision 4.0 of this board supports hot-pluggable eSATA (SATA 6), but the external eSATA only supports RAID 0 and RAID 1.  The internal SATA controller is fully SATA 6 and supports all RAID levels.
              </p>
              <p>
               I was
               <em>
                extremely
               </em>
               impressed with how well this board is marked and labeled.  A beginner would have an easy time assembling a system with this board. The included driver CD adds in everything Windows doesn’t recognize and the system has been rock-solid since the day I built it. Plays Diablo III on the highest settings, which isn’t saying much, but running Diablo III on one monitor while it is performing full HD video processing on another monitor (jumps a bit occasionally) is fairly impressive for a single video card rig.  Never fear, there’s a second slot for video cards and the ability to bridge them.
              </p>
              <p>
               <strong>
                Video:  Gigabyte GEFORCE GTX 660Ti Boost
               </strong>
              </p>
              <p>
               I chose the Gigabyte…

                <span class="meta-nav">

                </span>
               </a>
              </p>

              <h1 class="inetd-postheader">
               <a href="https://www.inetdaemon.com/technology/steampowered-503-service-unavailable/" rel="bookmark" title="Steampowered: 503 Service Unavailable">
                Steampowered: 503 Service Unavailable
               </a>
              </h1>
              <div class="inetd-postheadericons inetd-metadata-icons">
               <span class="inetd-postdateicon">
                <span class="date">
                 Published
                </span>
                <span class="entry-date" title="4:32 pm">
                 December 25, 2013
                </span>
               </span>
               |
               <span class="inetd-postauthoricon">
                <span class="author">
                 By
                </span>
                <span class="author vcard">
                 <a class="url fn n" href="https://www.inetdaemon.com/author/inetdaemon/" title="View all posts by InetDaemon">
                  InetDaemon
                 </a>
                </span>
               </span>
              </div>
             </div>
             <div class="inetd-postcontent">
              <p>
               As of 9:29 AM December 25th, 2014,  store.steampowered.com
               <a href="http://www.isitdownrightnow.com/steampowered.com.html" title="Steampowered.com down? Error 503 Service Unavailable">
                appears to be “offline” according to “isitdownrightnow.com”
               </a>
               , as is the ‘steamcommunity.com’ site.
              </p>
              <p>
               Steampowered is offering a free copy of
               <a href="http://store.steampowered.com/news/12151/" title="Left 3 Dead 2 Free on Steampowered">
                Left 4 Dead 2
               </a>
               (promotion ends December 26 10am Pacific), which along with the usual Christmas registrations and Steam engine downloads and installs, is jamming the “store” servers and overloading the ‘Valve’ network. Many visitors are receiving “503 Service Unavailable” errors when visiting the site via browser or the Steam client.  I was able to successfully download and install the steam engine. Everything else, such as validating purchases via email, and gaming, appears to be very slow, intermittently offline or unavailable.
              </p>
              <p>
               <span style="line-height: 1.5em;">
                I’m not a gamer, but I play Skyrim from time to time. At present, it appears that there’s the following issues currently:
               </span>
              </p>
              <ol>
               <li>
                May/May not be able to log into the Steam powered store via browser or the steam client.
               </li>
               <li>
                Email confirmations do not appear to be working (mail server issues?)
               </li>
               <li>
                Cannot get the list of installed products
               </li>
               <li>
                The Steampowered store “Blog” is offline/inaccessible.
               </li>
              </ol>
              <p>
               There may be other issues I can’t detect from here.
              </p>
              <p>
              </p>
              <p>
              </p>
