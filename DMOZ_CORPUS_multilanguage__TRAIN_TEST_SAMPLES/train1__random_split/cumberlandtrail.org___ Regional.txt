           </a>
           <strong>
            <span style="color: #000000;">

            </span>

          </p>
          <p style="text-indent: 35px;">
           <span style="color: #000000;">
            The Cumberland Trail is an ambitious hiking trail project under development in East Tennessee. When completed, the Cumberland Trail (CT) will extend more than 300 miles from its northern terminus in Cumberland Gap National Historical Park (TN/KY) to its southern terminus at the Chickamauga-Chattanooga National Military Park located on Signal Mountain just outside Chattanooga, Tennessee.  As of November 2016 over 210 miles of the trail are open for hiking. And work continues.  By the end of 2017 an additional 17 miles were complete but not open. These sections are waiting for bridges, final parking arrangements, signage or official opening dates. Estimated completion date for all trail segments on currently owned state land is late 2019.
            <br/>
           </span>
          </p>
          <p style="text-indent: 35px;">
           <span style="color: #000000;">
            The scenic footpath follows a line of high ridges and deep gorges along or near the rugged, eastern edge of Tennessee’s Cumberland Plateau.  The trail offers a unique wilderness experience and many scenic views, waterfalls, landscapes, gorges, wildlife, and widely varying flora. As a remote, back-country trail, it meanders through eleven Tennessee counties, on lands managed by Tennessee’s Departments of Environment and Conservation (TDEC), Tennessee Wildlife Resources Agency (TWRA), and Tennessee Forestry.
           </span>
          </p>
          <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_2362" style="width: 281px">
           <a href="http://cumberlandtrailsconference.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/20150710_Soddy-Br-Ver-CRPt.jpg">
            <img alt="20150710_Soddy Br Ver CRPt" class="wp-image-2362" height="434" sizes="(max-width: 271px) 100vw, 271px" src="https://cumberlandtrailsconference.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/20150710_Soddy-Br-Ver-CRPt-187x300.jpg" srcset="https://www.cumberlandtrail.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/20150710_Soddy-Br-Ver-CRPt-187x300.jpg 187w, https://www.cumberlandtrail.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/20150710_Soddy-Br-Ver-CRPt-638x1024.jpg 638w" width="271"/>
           </a>
           <p class="wp-caption-text">
            <span style="color: #993300;">
             “The Jewel of the Cumberland Trail” July 2015. Big Soddy Creek Bridge.
            </span>
            (Ranger Daniel Basham)
           </p>
          </div>
          <p style="text-indent: 35px;">
           The trail also connects two national parks and passes through a National Wild &amp; Scenic River area. In 1998 the trail was defined as an ambitious State Park project, designated as the Justin P. Wilson Cumberland Trail State Park, Tennessee’s first linear state park. As it evolved, the park is much more than a hiking trail. It is now the second largest Tennessee state park. But the trail is more than the state park, extending beyond the park boundaries through segments that traverse other state and some federal properties through agreements with TDEC.  The CT is an official component of the Tennessee Recreational Trails System and a legislatively designated State Scenic Trail. Additionally the Cumberland Trail is a part of the
           <a href="http://cumberlandtrailsconference.com/great-eastern-trail/" title="Great Eastern Trail">
            Great Eastern Trail
           </a>
           ,
           <span style="color: #000000;">
            which is under development and will extend from Alabama to New York when completed.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p style="text-indent: 35px;">
           The CTC is a member of the
           <a href="http://southeasternfoottrails.org/">
            Southeastern Foot Trails Collation
           </a>
           which is organized to make interconnections between eight long distance trails in the Southeast region.
          </p>
          <p style="text-indent: 35px;">
           <span style="color: #000000;">
            The conti
            <a href="http://cumberlandtrailsconference.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/01/SEFTC-logo.jpg" rel="attachment wp-att-3063">
             <img alt="SEFTC-logo" class="size-full wp-image-3063 alignleft" height="155" src="https://cumberlandtrailsconference.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/01/SEFTC-logo.jpg" width="149"/>
            </a>
            nued development and construction of the Cumberland Trail is accomplished through a working relationship between the Cumberland Trails Conference (CTC), the CT State Park and TDEC. The CTC, other nonprofits, private corporations, foundations, individuals, and others assist TDEC in raising funds for land acquisition, providing maintenance, and further developing the Cumberland Trail.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p style="text-indent: 35px;">
           <span style="color: #000000;">
            This extensive trail is being constructed and maintained largely by volunteers from Tennessee and across the nation. Efforts are organized and managed b
            <a href="http://cumberlandtrailsconference.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/01/DSC03466_RTP_BW_logoLR-1.jpg" rel="attachment wp-att-3042" style="color: #000000;">
             <img alt="RTP" class="alignright wp-image-3042" height="194" sizes="(max-width: 153px) 100vw, 153px" src="https://cumberlandtrailsconference.com/wp-content/uploads/2016/01/DSC03466_RTP_BW_logoLR-1-236x300.jpg" srcset="https://www.cumberlandtrail.org/wp-content/uploads/2016/01/DSC03466_RTP_BW_logoLR-1-236x300.jpg 236w, https://www.cumberlandtrail.org/wp-content/uploads/2016/01/DSC03466_RTP_BW_logoLR-1-768x975.jpg 768w, https://www.cumberlandtrail.org/wp-content/uploads/2016/01/DSC03466_RTP_BW_logoLR-1-806x1024.jpg 806w, https://www.cumberlandtrail.org/wp-content/uploads/2016/01/DSC03466_RTP_BW_logoLR-1.jpg 904w" width="153"/>
            </a>
            y TDEC.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p style="text-indent: 35px;">
           <span style="color: #000000;">
            The Cumberland Trail State Scenic Trail operates a paid trail crew mostly in the north sections. The CTC maintains two paid crews for the central and southern sections. Additional labor comes from thousands of hours of volunteer service provided through the CTC. The Cumberland Trails Conference
           </span>
           (CTC)
           <span style="color: #000000;">
            a non-profit 501-(c)(3) membership organization. The mission of the CTC is to provide paid and volunteer labor, equipment, supplies and vehicles to design and construct the trail under the auspices of TDEC.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p style="text-indent: 35px;">
           <span style="color: #000000;">
            Building the CT is a grassroots effort, driven by communities along the trail, government agencies, hiking clubs and a broad network of individuals.   This successful private/public partnership is a model often cited to demonstrate the power of volunteerism and public/private partnerships.
           </span>
          </p>
          <div class="wp-caption aligncenter" id="attachment_2110" style="width: 436px">
           <a href="https://www.cumberlandtrail.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/100_1118-e1469813509853.jpg">
            <img alt="College Students Grooming Trail" class="wp-image-2110" height="289" src="https://cumberlandtrailsconference.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/06/100_1118-300x200.jpg" width="426"/>
           </a>
           <p class="wp-caption-text">
            Hundreds of college students help make the CT possible donating time and energy during their Spring Break. (Richie)
           </p>
          </div>
          <p>
          </p>
          <p style="text-indent: 35px; text-align: left;">
           <span style="color: #000000;">
            Crucial to the construction of the trail is the participation of college students.  The CTC is a host site for the national Alternative Spring Break program. Since 1996 college students have constructed over fifty miles of trail or nearly 25% of the open trail. Pretty impressive.
           </span>
          </p>
          <p style="text-indent: 35px;">
           <span style="color: #000000;">
