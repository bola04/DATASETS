              <a href="http://christkirknc.com/keeping-little-hands-minds-engaged-worship/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link: Keeping Little Hands and Minds Engaged in Worship">
               Keeping Little Hands and Minds Engaged in Worship
               <span class="post-format-icon minor-meta">
               </span>
              </a>
             </h2>
             <p>
              <a href="http://christkirknc.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/IMG_2162.jpg">
               <img alt="" class="size-medium wp-image-1477 alignright" height="225" sizes="(max-width: 300px) 100vw, 300px" src="http://christkirknc.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/IMG_2162-300x225.jpg" srcset="http://christkirknc.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/IMG_2162-300x225.jpg 300w, http://christkirknc.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/IMG_2162-768x576.jpg 768w, http://christkirknc.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/IMG_2162-1024x768.jpg 1024w, http://christkirknc.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/IMG_2162-930x698.jpg 930w" width="300"/>
              </a>
              I have to confess something right out of the gate. I am a doodler. If I am in a meeting or listening to a lecture and I have a pen in my hand, I am drawing little cartoon characters, cars, trees, flowers and the occasional crazy-awesome spacecraft. In between, I will stop to scribble a phrase or a reference or a book title that I will want to remember later. But if I’m in a room sitting still, my pen is probably moving.
             </p>
             <p>
             </p>
             <p>
              I know this is controversial, and I know that many would say that if someone is doodling or drawing then they are not paying attention.

             <h2 class="post-title offset-by-one alpha">
              <a href="http://christkirknc.com/minimizing-distractions-worship/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link: Minimizing Distractions in Worship">
               Minimizing Distractions in Worship
               <span class="post-format-icon minor-meta">
               </span>
              </a>
             </h2>
             <p>
              <em>
               <img alt="Related image" src="https://4.bp.blogspot.com/-9OymfTA64BE/WKCbSvKDutI/AAAAAAAADB0/DkZJM9PmXCA7GFK1UKKzx9t1dhjSGrQlwCLcB/s1600/Palha%25C3%25A7o2.jpg"/>
              </em>
             </p>
             <p>
              <em>
               This is part three in an ongoing series of posts. Part one is
               <a href="http://christkirknc.com/raising-worshippers/">
                here
               </a>
               and part two is
               <a href="http://christkirknc.com/setting-expectations-children-worship/">
                here.
               </a>
              </em>
             </p>
             <p>
              In the previous installment I discussed the importance of setting and managing our expectations for ourselves and our children in worship. Among those I mentioned was the expectation that “children over four-to-five years of age are ordinarily physically and emotionally capable of sitting in a chair, on their bottoms, facing forward, quietly, for the duration of the sermon.”
             </p>
             <p>
              That may seem like one of those things that go without saying, but given the fact that I have seen adults talk to each other throughout the entire sermon, and have on many occasions seen grown people get up in the middle of a Scripture reading and wander around the building for some reason, perhaps this is not something we can take for granted.
