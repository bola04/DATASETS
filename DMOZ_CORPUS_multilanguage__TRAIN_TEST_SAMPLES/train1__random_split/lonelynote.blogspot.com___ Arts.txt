                      <a href="http://lonelynote.blogspot.com/2016/03/deerhunter-snakeskin.html">
                       Deerhunter - 'Snakeskin'
                      </a>
                     </h3>
                     <div class="post-header">
                      <div class="post-header-line-1">
                      </div>
                     </div>
                     <div class="post-body entry-content">
                      <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                       <a href="https://4.bp.blogspot.com/-EtYeICjVsPU/VtbG8jqRQNI/AAAAAAAAU14/YBbTRBxPImc/s1600/Deerhunter.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: left; float: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">

                      Bradford Cox's eccentric vocals have always been a welcome addition to
                      <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/deerhunter-mn0000280897/biography">
                       Deerhunter
                      </a>

                      <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/scott-weiland-mn0000836226">
                       Scott Weiland
                      </a>
                      speak-into-a-mega-phone-a-la-Dead-and-Bloated playbook, which adds a delightful touch.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>

                       Fading Frontier,

                     <div class="post-body entry-content">
                      <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-otVvumLCHiQ/VsoTv6eoJAI/AAAAAAAAU1g/fjkyw9Dnhg4/s1600/the1975.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: left; float: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">

                       Chastity Belt - 'Time to Go Home'
                      </a>
                     </h3>
                     <div class="post-header">
                      <div class="post-header-line-1">
                      </div>
                     </div>
                     <div class="post-body entry-content">
                      <div style="text-align: justify;">
                       <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-7z2vDSqw6io/VsoKL0NaH4I/AAAAAAAAU1Q/KuATzVd6-zE/s1600/Chastity%2BBelt.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;">

                       </a>
                       Oh how so happy I have been to finally see the pendulum begin swaying back toward the raw, guitar-heavy sounds that were once so prominent two decades ago.  Yes, indie rock is starting to see a resurgence of Grunge-inspired bands and anthems, and I am a major cheerleader for such a development.  (Refer to the relative success of
                       <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/courtney-barnett-mn0003165278/biography">
                        Courtney Barnett
                       </a>
                       if you remain unconvinced that there is a hunger for gritty, punk-inspired rock).
                      </div>
                      <br/>
                      <a href="http://lonelynote.blogspot.com/2013/12/speedy-ortiz-everythings-bigger.html">
                       Previously, we had posted on Speedy Ortiz
                      </a>
                      , whose sounds certainly embody the sounds of that time.  Today, we present to you 'Time to Go Home' by Walla Walla-based (of course)
                      <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/chastity-belt-mn0003139368/biography">
                       Chastity Belt
                      </a>
                      .  And while Speedy's sound tends to gravitate more toward the
                      <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/veruca-salt-mn0000806168/biography">
                       Veruca Salt
                      </a>
                      side of the grunge-revival spectrum, Chastity Belt bolts directly for
                      <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/sonic-youth-mn0000755156">
                       Sonic Youth
                      </a>
                      territory.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      'Time to Go Home' starts off slow and moody, but quickly adds some rapidity to the mix, ending on a steady, accelerating gait.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Watch the video to 'Time to Go Home' below:

                      <a href="http://lonelynote.blogspot.com/2015/10/album-review-vast-visual-audio-sensory.html">
                       Album Review:  V.A.S.T. - Visual Audio Sensory Theater
                      </a>
                     </h3>
                     <div class="post-header">
                      <div class="post-header-line-1">
                      </div>
                     </div>
                     <div class="post-body entry-content">
                      <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                       <a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-bTtPHvK_-ls/Vg9uE8EeeNI/AAAAAAAAUpk/93toNHAiaNw/s1600/MI0001750582VAST.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;">
                        <span style="font-family: Georgia, Times New Roman, serif;">
                         <img border="0" height="320" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-bTtPHvK_-ls/Vg9uE8EeeNI/AAAAAAAAUpk/93toNHAiaNw/s320/MI0001750582VAST.jpg" width="320"/>
                        </span>
                       </a>
                      </div>
                      <span style="font-family: Georgia, Times New Roman, serif;">
                       I remember when I first heard
                       <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/vast-mn0000313521/biography">
                        V.A.S.T
                       </a>
                       .---aka
                       <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/jon-crosby-mn0000821646">
                        Jon Crosby
                       </a>
                       's solo project---on the radio.  It was 1998, I was still in high school, and as I was laying in bed late on a Sunday night, I was listening to a radio show called
                       <a href="http://www.harddriveradio.com/">
                        hardDrive with Lou Brutus
                       </a>
                       on my clock radio.  When the lead single, 'Touched,' was broadcast on the program, I quickly took note, as I hadn't ever heard of anything quite like that at the time.  The song contained acoustic guitar, plus
                       <a href="https://www.google.com/url?sa=t&amp;rct=j&amp;q=&amp;esrc=s&amp;source=web&amp;cd=3&amp;cad=rja&amp;uact=8&amp;ved=0CCUQFjACahUKEwj3uJuoz6XIAhUBGj4KHbSEATw&amp;url=https%3A%2F%2Fen.wikipedia.org%2Fwiki%2FOrder_of_Saint_Benedict&amp;usg=AFQjCNGTqC3Cz7hWDPcfdutVMyrzzCIsAQ&amp;sig2=ByVBk3XMuZeK3taWAa62gA">
                        Benedictine Monks
                       </a>
                       , plus hard industrial sounds reminiscent of
                       <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/nine-inch-nails-mn0000351733">
                        Nine Inch Nails
                       </a>
                       , plus orchestral accompaniment.  Indeed, it was quite different from the hard hitting sounds of
                       <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/korn-mn0000112789">
                        Korn
                       </a>
                       ,
                       <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/rob-zombie-mn0000235169">
                        Rob Zombie
                       </a>
                       , and
                       <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/monster-magnet-mn0000495455">
                        Monster Magnet
                       </a>
                       receiving heavy airplay at that time.
                      </span>
                      <br/>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Georgia, Times New Roman, serif;">
                        <br/>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       <span style="font-family: Georgia, Times New Roman, serif;">
                        Afterward, I recall going to a faraway mall two hours away and buying the album.  On the way home, as my mother drove, I remember popping the self-titled disc into my CD-Walkman and finding myself engrossed by just how distinctive and diverse all the tracks were.  For example, 'Dirty Hole' is comprised of several layers systematically building atop one another, not the least of which includes those same Monk chants and Cathedral pipe organ.   And 'Three Doors' beautifully meshes Industrial Pop with the Classical canon.  And 'Here' is probably one of the coolest, cinematic-sounding openings to an album in recent times.
                       </span>
                       <br/>
                       <span style="font-family: Georgia, Times New Roman, serif;">
                        <br/>
                       </span>
                       <span style="font-family: Georgia, Times New Roman, serif;">
                        I have posted a few YouTube videos featuring a few of the great tracks from this album.  In descending order, they are 'Touched,' 'Three Doors,' and 'Here.'  Feel free to stream them and enjoy the greatness that is
                        <i>
                         Visual Audio Sensory Theater
                        </i>
                        , one of my favorite under-the-radar albums of the 90s.
                       </span>

                     <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
                      <a href="http://lonelynote.blogspot.com/2015/09/forgotten-gems-jay-ferguson-spirit.html">
                       Forgotten Gems:  Jay Ferguson + Spirit
                      </a>
                     </h3>
                     <div class="post-header">
                      <div class="post-header-line-1">
                      </div>
                     </div>
                     <div class="post-body entry-content">
                      Last weekend, while picking through crates of vinyl at an Antique Jamboree, I was beckoned by the thunderous call of
                      <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/jay-ferguson-mn0000220065">
                       Jay Ferguson
                      </a>
                      's
                      <i>
                       Thunder Island
                      </i>
                      .
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                       <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-IVpqyJUnNa0/VftMOvgTn2I/AAAAAAAAA98/uADbqdoBzPM/s1600/Thunder_Island_Cover_art.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: left; float: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                        <img border="0" height="318" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-IVpqyJUnNa0/VftMOvgTn2I/AAAAAAAAA98/uADbqdoBzPM/s320/Thunder_Island_Cover_art.jpg" width="320"/>
                       </a>
                      </div>
                      It was on sale for a buck.  And although a bit dusty and well-played -- particularly the title track -- it was well worth the investment.
                      <i>
                       Thunder Island
                      </i>
                      is, of course, a classic album of the Yacht Rock era.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Heck, even if the record had been missing, the cover photo is easily worth the dollar alone...
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      For those uninitiated, the single "Thunder Island" is a tasty - albeit cheesy - relic of '70s soft rock.  The instantly recognizable guitar hook and "doo doo doo doo!" backing vocals suck you right in from the get-go.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      The song has a Jimmy Buffet-meets-Eagles vibe to it; a song for rockin' out while sipping margaritas in some tropical paradise.  And it's fitting, because
                      <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/eagles-mn0000144847">
                       The Eagles
                      </a>
                      ' Joe Walsh contributed his guitar chops to much of the album.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Thunder Island was Ferguson's second, and most successful, solo outing after performing with bands such as Spirit and Jo Jo Gunne.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="315" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/PJMh0GJ6GWc" width="560">
                      </iframe>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Now, let's dig a little deeper and discuss
                      <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/spirit-mn0000746010/biography" target="_blank">
                       Spirit
                      </a>
                      -- an immensely talented, yet largely forgotten band of the late '60s-'70s.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Spirit formed in 1967 with Ferguson and 15-year-old
                      <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/randy-california-mn0000395888" target="_blank">
                       Randy California
                      </a>
                      (who had been in an earlier band with Jimi Hendrix) at the songwriting helm.  Spirit's 1968 debut album was as an eclectic a mix of musical styles as any of their contemporaries, mixing hard rock with jazz, blues, country and folk.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      While Ferguson wrote most of the debut's tracks, California contributed the acoustic ballad, "
                      <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=xd8AVbwB_6E" target="_blank">
                       Taurus
                      </a>
                      " -- an instrumental piece that inspired the intro to
                      <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/led-zeppelin-mn0000139026/biography" target="_blank">
                       Led Zeppelin
                      </a>
                      's epic "Stairway to Heaven".
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      While the band (in some formation) would go on to record 11 albums between 1968 and 1977, their only single to reach the Top 40 would be the 1968 follow-up, "I Got a Line On You".
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      "I Got a Line On You" is a hard-driving rocker with a great chorus.  It has all the workings to be a classic, but for some reason has gotten a bit lost amongst the other great singles of its time.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      The band split in different directions over the next decade.  California and Ferguson each went the solo route, while other members branched out to bands including
                      <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/nazareth-mn0000377891" target="_blank">
                       Nazareth
                      </a>
                      ,
                      <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/heart-mn0000671953" target="_blank">
                       Heart
                      </a>
                      and
                      <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/firefall-mn0000173647">
                       Firefall.
                      </a>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Spirit did reform in various incarnations into the early '90s.  In 1997, California was swimming in Hawaii with his family when he was tragically swept into a riptide and never seen again.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      A critically heralded and ambitiously innovative band during their heyday, Spirit lives on today through their recordings, which I'd recommend looking up.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Below is a video for "I Got a Line On You" from the band's brief reunion in 1984.  **Bonus: check out guitarist Skunk Baxter (the Doobie Brothers, Steely Dan) jamming out in the back!
                      <br/>

                      </iframe>

                     <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
                      <a href="http://lonelynote.blogspot.com/2015/08/torres-strange-hellos.html">
                       Torres - 'Strange Hellos'
                      </a>
                     </h3>
                     <div class="post-header">
                      <div class="post-header-line-1">
                      </div>
                     </div>
                     <div class="post-body entry-content">
                      <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                       <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-BrSnnuJim90/Vd2P1pijLII/AAAAAAAAUo4/_t8-3Wfz2zY/s1600/Torres.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;">
                        <img border="0" height="200" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-BrSnnuJim90/Vd2P1pijLII/AAAAAAAAUo4/_t8-3Wfz2zY/s200/Torres.jpg" width="200"/>
                       </a>
                      </div>
                      'Strange Hellos' by
                      <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/torres-mn0003108253">
                       Torres
                      </a>
                      could possibly be summed up as "angsty noir."
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      The beginning of the tune seems to materialize straight out of the bar prominently featured in the
                      <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/True_Detective_(season_2)">
                       second season of True Detective
                      </a>
                      , before building up to a full-frontal sonic assault.  There is a hint of
                      <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/jefferson-airplane-mn0000840102">
                       Jefferson Airplane
                      </a>
                      's 'White Rabbit' as well.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Checkout the video to 'Strange Hellos' below:
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <center>
                       <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="315" src="https://www.youtube.com/embed/Ol61WOSzLF8" width="560">

                     <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
                      <a href="http://lonelynote.blogspot.com/2015/07/behind-sample-notorious-bigs-juicy.html">
                       Behind the Sample:  The Notorious B.I.G.'s "Juicy"
                      </a>
                     </h3>
                     <div class="post-header">
                      <div class="post-header-line-1">
                      </div>
                     </div>
                     <div class="post-body entry-content">
                      "Super Nintendo. Sega Genesis.  When I was dead broke, man I couldn't picture this."
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                       <a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-YzzdBgRBAec/VbMoS99cmwI/AAAAAAAAA5E/xZpPaqIKHww/s1600/NotoriousBIG.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: left; float: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                        <img border="0" height="200" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-YzzdBgRBAec/VbMoS99cmwI/AAAAAAAAA5E/xZpPaqIKHww/s200/NotoriousBIG.jpg" width="177"/>
                       </a>
                      </div>
                      It was all a dream for Christopher Wallace, a.k.a.
                      <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/the-notorious-big-mn0000892827" target="_blank">
                       The Notorious B.I.G
                      </a>
                      .
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      The hip/hop artist took the rap world by storm in 1994 with the release of his monumental single, "Juicy" - a personalized rags-to-riches tale that went on to sell more than a half million copies and secured his status as one of the biggest rap stars in history.  It's been called one of the greatest hip hop songs of all time by critics from Rolling Stone to The Source.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Biggie was, as his name implied, a large man. And he was a man with big dreams, who details his struggle and aspirations in "Juicy".  The song discusses his childhood ambition to become a famous rapper, and to escape from his impoverished Brooklyn upbringing.  In "Juicy", Biggie name-drops many of the artists who inspired him as a youth, including
                      <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/heavy-d-mn0000669738" target="_blank">
                       Heavy D
                      </a>
                      - one of the first overweight rappers to find mainstream success.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      In his early teens, Biggie started dealing drugs and found himself in and out of a jail cell, but his sights were always set on music stardom.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      The opening of the song openly declares it's dedication, "...to all the teachers who said I'd never amount to nothin'".  Sure, many of the lyrics read like a laundry list of his lavish possessions, but for every boast he makes, he juxtaposes it with a hardship it took to get there.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      The deepest message of the song lies within the chorus -- "You know very well who you are, don't let 'em hold you down, reach for the stars..."
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      It's one of the most uplifting choruses in hip/hop, speaking to all of those other kids who grow up with nothing more than a dream.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      The Notorious B.I.G.'s life was cut tragically short when he was killed in a 1997 drive-by shooting, but his music - and his dream - proudly lives on.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      __________________________
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      But let it be known that the musical sample for "Juicy" dates back more than a decade earlier...
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      In 1983, the funk/soul group
                      <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/mtume-mn0000508391" target="_blank">
                       Mtume
                      </a>
                      - named after founder James Mtume - released a track called "Juicy Fruit".
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Mtume, an accomplished percussionist who'd previously toured with Miles Davis, formed his namesake group in the late '70s.  The band reached their height of popularity with "Juicy Fruit" - a midtempo track that proved immensely successful on R&amp;B stations and dance clubs.  While the single never quite reached the Top 40, it still sold more than a million copies.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      The popularity of "Juicy Fruit" established it as a staple in the hip/hop sampling community and has lived on successfully in various incarnations in the 30+ years since its release.  For a full list of artists who've sampled it, visit the song's
                      <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juicy_Fruit_(song)" target="_blank">
                       Wikipedia entry
                      </a>
                      .
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      <b>
                       "Cuz if ya don't know, now ya know..."

                     <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
                      <a href="http://lonelynote.blogspot.com/2015/07/toadies-away.html">
                       Toadies - 'Away'
                      </a>
                     </h3>
                     <div class="post-header">
                      <div class="post-header-line-1">
                      </div>
                     </div>
                     <div class="post-body entry-content">
                      Toadies came in like a lion and went out like a lamb during the mid-nineties.  Dominating alt-fm radio play in 1995, the Texas band's marquee single, '
                      <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=EkwD5rQ-_d4">
                       Possum Kingdom
                      </a>
                      ,' proved to be its Tour de Force.  The band never replicated the commercial success it enjoyed as a result of that track, even though the song's iconic call-to-action--"
                      <i>
                       SO HELP ME JESUS"--
                      </i>
                      remains embedded in the minds of even the most casual listener of rock radio today.
                      <br/>
                      <div>
                       <br/>
                      </div>
                      <div>
                       Despite the epic reach of 'Possum Kingdom,'
                       <i>
                        <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/album/rubberneck-mw0000118047">
                         Rubberneck
                        </a>
                       </i>
                       nevertheless featured several other really solid punk and grunge laced anthems.  Of course '
                       <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=eKbk7BPXBa4">
                        Tyler
                       </a>
                       ' comes to mind, an absolutely great song which slowly builds as it relays a story set in Tyler, Texas.  And '
                       <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9ZL27Aiw9_I">
                        Backslider
                       </a>
                       ' was one of
                       <i>
                        Rubberneck
                       </i>
                       's more punk-based of tunes.
                       <br/>
                       <br/>
                       But there is a track which is one of the band's more overlooked deep cuts:  'Away.'  Similar to 'Possum Kingdom,' 'Away' liberally borrows from the
                       <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/pixies-mn0000895136">
                        Pixies
                       </a>
                       , implementing a loud-quiet-loud-quiet song structure that drives toward the ultimate pleading choral hook:
                       <i>
                        WHEN I'M AWAY , I KNOW IN MY HEART THERE IS A HEAVEEENNNN!!
                       </i>
                       <br/>
                       <div>
                        <br/>
                       </div>
                       <div>
                        Relive the greatness of this track by watching 'Away' by Toadies in the video player below:

                      </a>
                     </h3>
                     <div class="post-header">
                      <div class="post-header-line-1">
                      </div>
                     </div>
                     <div class="post-body entry-content">
                      <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                       <a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-OIrwKnT6YF8/VHA5wmmq2dI/AAAAAAAAS3U/dAo7HW4dKaU/s1600/Meg_Myers.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: right; float: right; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-left: 1em;">

                       </a>
                      </div>
                      Probably one of the more visceral singles currently on alt-rock rotation, 'Desire' is the brilliant siren from Tennessee-born
                      <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/meg-myers-mn0003206669">
                       Meg Myers
                      </a>
                      .
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Why is the song so great?
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      First, it blends just the right amount of industrial beats with focused, intense lyrical stylings.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Second, the melody is--plain and simple--sensual and seductive;  indeed, the narrator exclaims, very plaintively, 'I want to f*** you.'
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Third, the track's tenor and attitude serve as the very embodiment of its title:  desire.  The meter of the lyrics and arrangement is one of concentration and fixation all throughout the entire production.

                      <div class="post-header-line-1">
                      </div>

                     <div class="post-body entry-content">
                      <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">

                       </a>
                      </div>
                      <div class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
                       British sensation
                       <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/sam-smith-mn0002509942">
                        Sam Smith
                       </a>
                       made
 incredible inroads with American audiences in 2014 with his epic ‘Stay with Me.’
  In fact, it was near impossible not to
 walk in public without hearing the song blasted someplace, whether it be the
 local mall, aquatic center, park, or public square.
                       <o:p>
                       </o:p>
                      </div>
                      <div class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
                       <br/>
                      </div>
                      <div class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
                       There is no doubt that ‘Stay with
 Me’ is one of the most soulful pop productions of the last decade.  Yet, ‘I’m Not the Only One’ strikes a
 different, but just as powerful chord, with Yours Truly.
                       <o:p>
                       </o:p>
                      </div>
                      <div class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
                       <br/>
                      </div>
                      <div class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
                       Maybe it’s the subject matter featuring a narrator who is deeply hurt? 
 Maybe it’s the wonderful instrumental accompaniments?   Or
 maybe it’s just the sheer fact that Sam Smith is a damned good vocalist??
                      </div>
                      <div class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
                       <br/>
                      </div>
                      <div class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
                       In any event, ‘I’m Not the Only One’
 resonates in an equally valuable, but very different, way as ‘Stay with Me’
 did.
                      </div>
                      <div class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
                       <br/>
                      </div>

                     <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
                      <a href="http://lonelynote.blogspot.com/2014/10/hozier-take-me-to-church.html">
                       Hozier - 'Take Me to Church'
                      </a>
                     </h3>
                     <div class="post-header">
                      <div class="post-header-line-1">
                      </div>
                     </div>
                     <div class="post-body entry-content">
                      <div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
                       <a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-Yj3vyhHpmpY/VEnRC39WgEI/AAAAAAAASnA/k4PbHIENbhU/s1600/Hozier.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="clear: left; float: left; margin-bottom: 1em; margin-right: 1em;">
                        <img border="0" height="197" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-Yj3vyhHpmpY/VEnRC39WgEI/AAAAAAAASnA/k4PbHIENbhU/s1600/Hozier.jpg" width="200"/>
                       </a>
                      </div>
                      <div class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
                       <span style='font-family: "Cambria","serif";'>
                        <a href="http://www.allmusic.com/artist/hozier-mn0003144637">
                         Hozier
                        </a>
                        —born
 Andrew Hozier-Byrne—presents a modern day Gospel with his contemporary
 masterpiece, ‘Take Me to Church.’
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Cambria, serif;">
                        <br/>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
                       <span style="font-family: Cambria, serif;">
                        T
                       </span>
                       <span style="font-family: Cambria, serif;">
                        he
 Irishman recently performed ‘Take Me to Church’ on
                        <a href="http://www.nbc.com/saturday-night-live">
                         SNL
                        </a>
                        , and he brought down the
 house.
                       </span>
                       <span style="font-family: Cambria, serif;">
                       </span>
                       <span style="font-family: Cambria, serif;">
                        The song effortlessly brings
 together blues, Gospel, soul, and rock.
                       </span>
                       <span style="font-family: Cambria, serif;">
                       </span>
                       <span style="font-family: Cambria, serif;">
                        Indeed,
 it is refreshing to hear an electric guitar-driven alternative track in an era
 dominated lately by synth, computers, acoustic instruments, and light
 percussion.
                       </span>
                       <span style="font-family: Cambria, serif;">
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
                       <span style='font-family: "Cambria","serif";'>
                        <br/>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <div class="MsoNormal" style="text-align: justify;">
                       <span style='font-family: "Cambria","serif";'>
                        Beyond
 the music, the lyrics are deeply powerful and moving.  One choral stanza reads:
                        <o:p>
                        </o:p>
                       </span>
                      </div>
                      <blockquote class="tr_bq" style="text-align: justify;">
                       <div style="text-align: justify;">
                        <span style='font-family: "Cambria","serif";'>
                         "Take
 me to Church;
                        </span>
                        <span style='font-family: "Cambria","serif";'>
                        </span>
                        <span style='font-family: "Cambria","serif";'>
                         I’ll
 worship like a dog at the shrine of your lies;
                        </span>
                        <span style='font-family: "Cambria","serif";'>
                         I’ll
 tell you my sins and you can sharpen your knife;
                        </span>
                        <span style='font-family: "Cambria","serif";'>
                         Offer
 me that deathless                  death;
                        </span>
                        <span style='font-family: "Cambria","serif";'>
                         Good
 God, let me give you my life."
                        </span>
                       </div>
                      </blockquote>
                      <div class="MsoNormalCxSpMiddle" style="text-align: justify;">
                       <span style='font-family: "Cambria","serif";'>
                        And when Hozier sings these words, he
 belts them out with passion and heart. 
 It may take a few spins for the words to connect to the listener, but once they do, you’ll
 feel you’ve, in fact, been taken to church.
                       </span>
                      </div>
