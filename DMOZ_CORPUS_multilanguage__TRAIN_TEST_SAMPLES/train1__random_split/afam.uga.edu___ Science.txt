            <div class="content">
             <article about="/welcome-institute-african-american-studies" role="article" typeof="schema:WebPage">
              <span class="hidden" content="Welcome to the Institute for African American Studies" property="schema:name">
              </span>
              <div>
               <div class="body" property="schema:text">
                <p>
                 The Institute for African American Studies (AFAM) draws upon a variety of traditions in Africana Studies and engages fields across the humanities, social sciences, and education. The faculty are united in its mission of promoting the study of people of African descent and their experiences throughout the Diaspora; promoting the field of African American Studies as a major academic discipline; and serving as a repository for cultural and historical research.
                </p>
                <p>
                 In pursuing its mission, the Institute offers an interdisciplinary and multifaceted approach to the field of African American Studies. Its principal objectives include:
                </p>
                <ul>
                 <li>
                  Providing students with a learning environment in which to appreciate the history, art, and culture of African Americans.
                 </li>
                 <li>
                  Developing the critical and analytical tools of inquiry for human enlightenment and informed citizenry.
                 </li>
                 <li>
                  Illuminating the history and culture of African Americans in global contexts.
                 </li>
                 <li>
                  Developing institutional research skills vital for success in graduate and professional studies.
                 </li>
                </ul>
                <p>
                 The Institute vision, like its mission and objectives, is grounded in the black intellectual tradition, building on the ideas of intellectuals and scholars such as W.E.B. Du Bois, Anna Julia Cooper, Langston Hughes, Carter C. Woodson, Ida B. Wells, and others who believed in the systematic study of the black experience. From this base it continues to explore new ways of understanding black life and experience through performance studies, transatlantic studies, visual culture, and music.
                </p>
               </div>
              </div>
             </article>

          <h2>
           Greetings from Director
          </h2>
          <div class="content">
           <div class="body">
            <blockquote>
             <p>
              "I am invisible, understand, simply because people refuse to see me. . . . When they approach me they see only my surroundings, themselves, or figments of their imagination—indeed, everything and anything except me."
             </p>
            </blockquote>
            <p>
             When Ralph Ellison opened his classic novel with these words, African American people and culture were indeed often invisible within larger United States society. Thanks to the hard work of those who fought to establish programs in the study of African American and black diasporic cultures, this invisibility has diminished. The Institute for African American Studies at the University of Georgia was part of this mission and embraces this legacy as it continues its commitment to future intellectual and historical discovery. Housed in the Holmes-Hunter Academic Building, an edifice commemorating the desegregation of University of Georgia, the Institute's site symbolizes the remembrance of history to impact future change.
            </p>
            <p>
             <a href="/greetings-director">
              Read more from our Director, Dr. Valerie Babb
             </a>
             .
            </p>
