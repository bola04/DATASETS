           The Gail Project at UCSC
          </a>
         </h1>
         <div class="entry-meta">
          <span class="sep">
           Posted on
          </span>
          <a href="http://okinawa.com/research/military-war-and-peace/gail-project-ucsc" rel="bookmark" title="7:35 pm">
           <time class="entry-date" datetime="2017-10-11T19:35:21+00:00">
            October 11, 2017
           </time>
          </a>
          <span class="by-author">
           <span class="sep">
            by
           </span>
           <span class="author vcard">
            <a class="url fn n" href="http://okinawa.com/author/artbobo" rel="author" title="View all posts by artbobo">
             artbobo
            </a>
           </span>
          </span>
         </div>
         <!-- .entry-meta -->
        </header>
        <!-- .entry-header -->
        <div class="entry-content">
         <span style="font-size: medium;">
          <a href="https://gailproject.ucsc.edu/">
           "The Gail Project
          </a>
          is a collaborative, international public history project that explores the founding years of the American military occupation of Okinawa. The project is inspired by a collection of photos taken in Okinawa in 1952-53 by an American Army Captain: Charles Eugene Gail.  ...
          <span style="font-size: medium;">
           faculty, artists and undergraduate students at the University of California, Santa Cruz, are developing a traveling exhibition of Gail's photographs with an accompanying digital archive that is comprised of the photos, key texts and documents, oral histories from both American and Okinawan voices, as well as undergraduate student research and writing."
          </span>
          <a href="https://gailproject.ucsc.edu/">
           https://gailproject.ucsc.edu/
          </a>
         </span>
         <img class="alignnone size-medium" height="200" src="https://gailproject.ucsc.edu/files/square_thumbnails/9c5cec1875e514150223e97f9af51a95.jpg" width="200"/>
        </div>

        <div class="entry-content">
         Isetan Department Store is a major landmark in Shinjuku and
         <a href="http://www.businesswire.com/news/home/20111130005824/en/Asia-Pacific-Top-Department-Stores-Examined-Research-Study">
          known as one of the prime department stores in Japan (if not Asia) in high fashion
         </a>
         . With that said, I look forward to the success of
         <a href="http://www.tilla-earth.com/">
          TILLA-EARTH
         </a>
         , a jewelry brand from Ishigakijima. Shizuka Taira, the owner of the store, will sell his jewelry for a limited time from June 27th ~ July 17th on the first floor of Isetan. He will also set a booth at the well established Ginza Mitsukoshi (July 18th ~31st).
 
 There are many other Okinawan jewelry brands, many simple and charming. Here's a list of some of them (the website may not have English translations):
         <a href="http://www.grandblue-online.jp/index.html">
          Grand Blue
         </a>
         (known for rings with natural star sand in them and minsa motifs)
         <a href="http://www.kaisou.com/jewelry/jewelry.html">
          KAI
         </a>
         (jewelry section of the Okinawan brand,
         <a href="http://www.kaisou.com/index.html">
          Kaiso
         </a>
         )
         <a href="http://www.ruca.cc/index.html">
          Ruca
         </a>
         (jewelry using coral reefs)
         <a href="http://www.cicafu.com/contents.html">
          cicafu
         </a>
         (accessory inspired by Native American jewelry)
         <a href="http://www.grain-okinawa.com/content.aspx?cat=1003">
          Grain
         </a>
         (furniture design, but they sell rings made of Yakushima cedar)
         <a href="http://www.grandblue-online.jp/index.html">
         </a>

         <h1 class="entry-title">
          <a href="http://okinawa.com/event/okinawa-art-in-ny-exhibition" rel="bookmark">
           OKINAWA ART in NY Exhibition
          </a>
         </h1>
         <div class="entry-meta">
          <span class="sep">
           Posted on
          </span>
          <a href="http://okinawa.com/event/okinawa-art-in-ny-exhibition" rel="bookmark" title="4:23 am">
           <time class="entry-date" datetime="2012-06-07T04:23:13+00:00">
            June 7, 2012
           </time>
          </a>
          <span class="by-author">
           <span class="sep">
            by
           </span>
           <span class="author vcard">
            <a class="url fn n" href="http://okinawa.com/author/oceanpears" rel="author" title="View all posts by oceanpears">
             oceanpears
            </a>
           </span>
          </span>
         </div>
         <!-- .entry-meta -->
        </header>
        <!-- .entry-header -->
        <div class="entry-content">
         The Nippon Gallery in New York will hold "Okinawa Art in NY" Exhibition from June 20 - July 27th, 2012. Artists include renowned
         <a href="http://english.ryukyushimpo.jp/2012/01/23/4990/">
          Seikichi Tamanaha
         </a>
         (painting),
         <a href="http://www.museums.pref.okinawa.jp/art/artists/photograph/higa_yoshiharu/index.html">
          Yoshiharu Higa
         </a>
         (photography),
         <a href="http://www.michiko-uehara.jp/">
          Michiko Uehara
         </a>
         (weaving), and other Okinawan artists living abroad. There hasn't been anything like this before in the United States, and Okinawan Art is finally receiving the recognition it deserves.
 
 For more information, please see the following websites:
         <a href="http://www.nipponclub.org/upcomingevents.php">
          http://www.nipponclub.org/upcomingevents.php
         </a>
         <a href=" http://www.compass-art.com/archives/projects/okinawa-art-in-new-york">
          http://www.compass-art.com/archives/projects/okinawa-art-in-new-york
         </a>
        </div>

         <h1 class="entry-title">
          <a href="http://okinawa.com/research/art/tree-project-a-message-about-biodiversity-through-art" rel="bookmark">
           Tree Project – A message about biodiversity through art
          </a>
         </h1>
         <div class="entry-meta">
          <span class="sep">
           Posted on
          </span>
          <a href="http://okinawa.com/research/art/tree-project-a-message-about-biodiversity-through-art" rel="bookmark" title="1:38 am">
           <time class="entry-date" datetime="2012-05-09T01:38:22+00:00">

          <span class="by-author">
           <span class="sep">
            by
           </span>
           <span class="author vcard">

         <!-- .entry-meta -->

        <!-- .entry-header -->
        <div class="entry-content">
         Last year in early November, environmental artists
         <a href="http://collinsandgoto.com/">
          Tim Collins and Reiko Goto
         </a>
         were invited to the Ryukyu University to run a workshop on how art can visually express the importance of biodiversity.
 Traditionally, Okinawans have worshiped nature. Holy grounds known as utaki are usually in forests and by rivers, sometimes  an entire island. Approximately 400 to 500 utakis exist in Okinawa, including the upstream of Sembara pond behind the Ryukyu University's Northern Cafeteria.
 Workshop participants saw the Kyuyo bridge across Sembara pond as a gateway to the biodiveristy of Okinawa and creatively conveyed this message with a performance art using leaves.
 
 source:
         <a href="http://www.u-ryukyu.ac.jp/top_news/hot/student24_2012033002/">
          http://www.u-ryukyu.ac.jp/top_news/hot/student24_2012033002/
         </a>

It's one of my favorite times of the year in Japan, but in Okinawa, hanami starts in January/February since the cherry blossoms bloom earlier.         <div class="entry-content">
         Kin-cho Town has delcared itself as the official "Taco Rice Town."
 
 Taco rice, symbolic of Okinawa's champuru culture, was created in 1984 at Parlor Senri, just outside of Camp Hansen. Popular among American Marines and locals, taco rice became part of Okiawan cuisine. In November 2010, Kincho received the Guinness World Records for making the world's largest taco rice, weighing 746kg.

        <div class="entry-content">
         For those with daring taste buds, toufuyou is a must-eat cuisine only loyal families ate during the Ryukyu Kingdom era. Fermented with jiuqu, moascus purpureus, and awamori, toufuyou is a reddish tofu delicacy, rich like urchin meat (meaning it's creamy and thick). If you've ever tried Chinese fermented bean curd, you have an idea of what it might taste like.
