         <p>
          <img alt="Hoo-Kee-Bak-Zhang" height="1066" src="http://www.sixthseal.com/images/Michelin/Hoo-Kee-Bak-Zhang.jpg" title="Hoo-Kee-Bak-Zhang" width="800"/>

         <p>
          I had a carefully curated list of Michelin 1 Star and Bib Gourmand places to visit in Singapore, with a focus on hawker food. I ended up eating as many as 4 meals in a two hour span for several days, which only sounds fun in concept. I have a huge appetite and a prodigious stomach capacity but I felt stuffed very often and couldn‚Äôt enjoy the food to its fullest potential. This was one of those occasions. I went to Bukit Merah to tackle 4 different dishes and Hoo Kee Bak Chang was my 3rd stop. However, I still very much enjoyed the bak zhang they served.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Hoo-Kee" height="1066" src="http://www.sixthseal.com/images/Michelin/Hoo-Kee.jpg" title="Hoo-Kee" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          I believe this is the only bak zhang in the Michelin list. The stall is in the packed
          <strong>
           Amoy Street Food Center
          </strong>
          . It‚Äôs full of office workers during lunch, it‚Äôs hard to find a seat and the center is blisteringly hot and humid. I‚Äôm not the biggest fan of crowd and I don‚Äôt fare well in the heat, but if you can get past all that, you‚Äôll be rewarded by a beautifully crafted bak zhang. The stall is manned by a husband and wife (?) team and the woman is quite attractive! I found her friendly and approachable too, which is always a plus point.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Michelin-Bak-Zhang" height="600" src="http://www.sixthseal.com/images/Michelin/Michelin-Bak-Zhang.jpg" title="Michelin-Bak-Zhang" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          They only have four items on the menu:
          <strong>
           Chestnut/Original Dumpling
          </strong>
          (SGD 3),
          <strong>
           Salted Egg w/ Chestnut Dumpling
          </strong>
          (SGD 4),
          <strong>
           Mushroom w/ Chestnut Dumpling
          </strong>
          (SGD 4) and
          <strong>
           Deluxe All-in
          </strong>
          (SGD 5). I went for the last option since I could only eat one bak zhang. As the name suggests, it‚Äôs a combination of everything into one dumpling. They‚Äôll cut it up for you and serve it on a disposable cardboard tray so you can eat it right there and then.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Hoo-Kee-Me" height="600" src="http://www.sixthseal.com/images/Michelin/Hoo-Kee-Me.jpg" title="Hoo-Kee-Me" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          I loved the flavors in here. I have a soft spot for bak zhang and I have to freely admit that I like most bak zhang that has passed my lips. The one at Hoo Kee is packed with ingredients ‚Äì there‚Äôs mushrooms, salted egg, chestnuts, marinated pork. It‚Äôs a beautiful medley of comforting flavors and textures and I thoroughly enjoyed it despite being full. Is it better than other bak zhangs? I don‚Äôt know, this was the only bak zhang I‚Äôve eaten in Singapore ever! But it‚Äôs a really good bak zhang, and as I‚Äôm writing this I have the worst craving for them. I could totally smash 4-5 right about now. ü§§
         </p>
         <p>
          But then I see my fat face in the photo above and I know I shouldn‚Äôt. Back to my diet, then.
         </p>

          There was a demonstration on how to open durians and how to tell a Musang King apart from the other cultivars. I already know this but I still picked up a few interesting things. Apparently if you hit a durian, it simulates it falling naturally and starts the ripening process. This is supposed to happen in a matter of seconds, so this is why you see some durian sellers smacking the side of a durian with the flat part of their parang before opening it!
         </p>

          Then came the selection (flight?) of durians. It‚Äôs neatly arranged from mild to intense, so I started out with the Durian Jantung. This is what Chinese call XO durian. It‚Äôs pretty good but I was blown away by the next two ‚Äì one of my favorite cultivars of durian is D2 (Dato Nina) and this was a perfect specimen. Fruity flavors with a fibrous pulp. I also loved the Tekka (what we call chu kiok), it was intensely sweet and creamy. Musang King was the last and the best ‚Äì very bittersweet and strongly flavored to bring a nice end to things. It‚Äôs an excellent way for people to eat a variety of good durian cultivars.

         </h2>
         <div class="entry-meta">
          <span class="posted-on">

         <p>
          <img alt="Pounder" height="600" src="http://www.sixthseal.com/images/cooking/Pounder.jpg" title="Pounder" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          I am currently experimenting with different recipes to make my meal prep taste better and I have found a winner. This is the reigning champion ‚Äì my current favorite! Ayam pongteh is a Nyonya dish made with taucu (fermented soy beans). It has a very distinctive taste that I like. I used the base recipe from
          <a href="http://www.submerryn.com/" target="_blank">
           Merryn
          </a>
          and modified it according to my taste buds. This is for 8 portions:
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           2.5 kg chicken legs
          </li>
          <li>
           0.5 kg chicken breast
          </li>
          <li>
           6 shallots
          </li>
          <li>
           10 garlic cloves
          </li>
          <li>
           1 cup taucu
          </li>
          <li>
           2 tbsp dark soy sauce
          </li>
          <li>
           1 tbsp light soy sauce
          </li>
          <li>
           4 tbsp palm sugar
          </li>
          <li>
           Potatoes
          </li>
          <li>
           6 cups water
          </li>
         </ul>
         <p>
          <img alt="Fry Garlic" height="600" src="http://www.sixthseal.com/images/cooking/Fry-Garlic.jpg" title="Fry Garlic" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          I had to get a pounder for this. You need to slice the garlic and shallots into manageable pieces and pound it to extract the flavor and increase the surface area of the aromatics. You also need a medium to fry them in.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Taucu" height="600" src="http://www.sixthseal.com/images/cooking/Taucu.jpg" title="Taucu" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          I used the chicken skin from the breast meat ‚Äì you can get a small amount of grease from it. The original recipe has oil but I‚Äôm cutting down on excess calories so mine just uses a minimal amount which is skimmed out of the gravy later. You need some agent to fry the minced garlic and shallots.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Pongteh Paste" height="600" src="http://www.sixthseal.com/images/cooking/Pongteh-Paste.jpg" title="Pongteh Paste" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Next you add in the taucu, light + dark soy, and palm sugar. You might need to break it up into smaller pieces first if it comes in block form. You fry this entire mixture for 30 seconds or so until it forms a sticky paste. You can literally see it bind and bubble up when that happens.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Chicken Arrange" height="600" src="http://www.sixthseal.com/images/cooking/Chicken-Arrange.jpg" title="Chicken Arrange" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Next I arrange the chicken legs into my rice cooker (you can use a pot, I just don‚Äôt have one big enough) with the potatoes on top and pour in the mixture. Add in 6 cups of water and you‚Äôre set!
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Chicken Pongteh" height="600" src="http://www.sixthseal.com/images/cooking/Chicken-Pongteh.jpg" title="Chicken Pongteh" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          I didn‚Äôt put in the breast meat coz I don‚Äôt want to overcook them. I wait until the chicken leg is done cooking (up to your standards when that happens) and take them out before dunking in the breast part into the gravy. I don‚Äôt cook my chicken breast long since it goes through a reheating process which dries it up if overcooked. I usually just leave it in for a minute or two and finish up in the microwave when I eat it.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Ayam Pongteh" height="600" src="http://www.sixthseal.com/images/cooking/Ayam-Pongteh.jpg" title="Ayam Pongteh" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          This is really delicious! I‚Äôm a huge fan of it and I can‚Äôt wait to prepare it again coz I was the happiest guy eating this compared to other meal preps. My next prep will be chicken curry, let‚Äôs see if that can topple this one as my favorite meal! I suspect not coz I like sweet gravy in my meals. I made a lot for this but stored it separately, to add to the containers when I heat it up.
         </p>

         <p>
          <img alt="Soy Chicken" height="600" src="http://www.sixthseal.com/images/cooking/Soy-Chicken.jpg" title="Soy Chicken" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          I‚Äôve always been a huge fan of soy chicken. It‚Äôs one of my favorite dishes at the chap fan place. I Googled the recipe for this and settled for one which I thought sounded good. I had all the stuff I needed at home, except for sugar and Shaoxing wine. I‚Äôve completely cut out added sugars from my diet and I never needed Chinese cooking wine. However, I know people put those two in good soy chicken so I used that recipe.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Shaoxing Wine" height="600" src="http://www.sixthseal.com/images/cooking/Shaoxing-Wine.jpg" title="Shaoxing Wine" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          I‚Äôm aiming to eat 204 grams of protein per day so one portion for me is 1 chicken leg + ¬Ω chicken breast. I got 2 kg of chicken legs and there were only 7 inside so one portion is 1 ¬Ω chicken breast only. I marinated the 2 kg of chicken legs and 4 chicken breasts in my huge rice cooker. I knew it was a good idea to get it coz I‚Äôm going to use my rice cooker to cook everything too! I don‚Äôt have a pot large enough to cook everything at once.
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           2 kg chicken legs
          </li>
          <li>
           4 chicken breasts (halved)
          </li>
          <li>
           1 cup light soy sauce
          </li>
          <li>
           ¬Ω cup dark soy sauce
          </li>
          <li>
           ¬Ω cup sugar
          </li>
          <li>
           ¬Ω cup Shaoxing wine
          </li>
          <li>
           1 cup water
          </li>
          <li>
           Coriander (snipped into small pieces)
          </li>
          <li>
           Garlic (to taste)
          </li>
         </ul>
         <p>
          (makes 8 portions)
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Soy Chicken Marinate" height="600" src="http://www.sixthseal.com/images/cooking/Soy-Chicken-Marinate.jpg" title="Soy Chicken Marinate" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          I just chucked everything into the rice cooker and let it sit for 1 hour before turning on the power. This, in hindsight, was a mistake. It takes so much longer to cook chicken legs compared to chicken breasts. I should have cooked the legs first and then added the breasts at the very end. As it turned out, the chicken legs were undercooked and the breasts were overcooked. However, I think if you chop up the chicken legs, they‚Äôll cook around the same time.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Lu Jek" height="600" src="http://www.sixthseal.com/images/cooking/Lu-Jek.jpg" title="Lu Jek" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          It did taste good though. I would even say it‚Äôs delicious! This has topped my favorite meal prep recipe, slightly higher than chicken rice. I kept the sauce separately in the fridge so I can add a spoonful of the soy sauce jelly to the hot microwaved rice. It cools down the rice perfectly so it‚Äôs ready to eat instantly.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Soy Chicken Sauce" height="600" src="http://www.sixthseal.com/images/cooking/Soy-Chicken-Sauce.jpg" title="Soy Chicken Sauce" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          The collagen in the chicken also allows the gravy to solidify into a bottom layer of jelly and a top layer of fat which makes it easy to skim off the hardened fat after it‚Äôs cooled down in the fridge. I pair 1 chicken leg and ¬Ω breast with Basmati rice and broccoli for an awesome meal.
         </p>

           Kok Siong Penang Nasi Kandar, Puchong

         </h2>

         <p>
          <img alt="Nasi Kandar Kok Siong" height="600" src="http://www.sixthseal.com/images/food/Nasi-Kandar-Kok-Siong.jpg" title="Nasi Kandar Kok Siong" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Kok Siong? A Chinese run and operated nasi kandar place? I thought it was pretty unusual too. This is actually a very famous nasi kandar place in Puchong. It‚Äôs always packed with people and there‚Äôs a long queue for the star ‚Äì the nasi kandar stall. The stall is located inside a coffee shop of the same name. I popped by for lunch today coz I‚Äôve been craving nasi kandar for a while now.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Nasi Kandar Puchong" height="600" src="http://www.sixthseal.com/images/food/Nasi-Kandar-Puchong.jpg" title="Nasi Kandar Puchong" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          I hear the dishes to try here are the fried chicken and the lamb curry. I got both and also their omelet with rice and half a salted egg. I‚Äôm not sure if it should be called an omelet coz it‚Äôs fried on the outside and steamed on the inside. It tastes more like a Chinese style steamed egg. There‚Äôs lots of onions inside for flavor and it reminded me of something you could get in a chap fan stall.
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Kok Siong Puchong" height="600" src="http://www.sixthseal.com/images/food/Kok-Siong-Puchong.jpg" title="Kok Siong Puchong" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          This is my plate of nasi kandar. It costs RM 20 for everything. The basic stuff is the rice and gravy ‚Äì you can also add their omelet to this. Sides are fried chicken and lamb curry and also a salted egg. It‚Äôs not an unreasonable charge, I‚Äôve had more expensive nasi kandar for less in Indian places. However, the proof is in the pudding! Is it good?
         </p>
         <p>
          <img alt="Kok Siong" height="600" src="http://www.sixthseal.com/images/food/Kok-Siong.jpg" title="Kok Siong" width="800"/>
         </p>
         <p>
          I‚Äôve read a lot of glowing reviews before this and people have told me via word of mouth that it‚Äôs delicious too so when I ate it I had high expectations. The large crowd here certainly bolstered my opinion. Unfortunately I found everything to be over-salted and I didn‚Äôt really enjoy the food. The fried chicken was quite spiced (as in there are lots of spices, not that it burns your tongue) but nothing spectacular. I much preferred the fried chicken at Wong Soon Kee.
         </p>
         <p>
          I must admit, I questioned myself multiple times. Everyone says it‚Äôs good! Are my taste buds really that out of sync? Is the fact that it‚Äôs too salty mar my experience? But I eat 3 packets of Mi Sedaap all the time! I‚Äôm used to sodium overload. Did I go when I wasn‚Äôt hungry? No, I was famished! I concluded that it‚Äôs probably just not my thing. I won‚Äôt go again but I ate everything on my plate (mostly coz I don‚Äôt like wasting food). However, plenty of people like this so your mileage may vary.
         </p>
