    <h1>
     <a href="/research/projects/tools/">
      <img alt="Captain Nemo" class="i-lhome" height="80" src="/_n/i/nemo.jpg" width="80"/>
      Deep Resources
     </a>
    </h1>
    <p>
     Professionals in the marine sciences are an important part of the MarineBio community. MarineBio's resources provide a convenient clearinghouse of information and links to academic resources including:
     <a href="/research/projects/research/">
      Relevant journals
     </a>
     ~
     <a href="/research/references/">
      Reference books
     </a>
     ~
     <a href="/research/projects/tools/">
      Online research tools (databases etc.)
     </a>
     ~
     <a href="/oceans/conservation/organizations/">
      Worldwide marine conservation organizations
     </a>
     ~
     <a href="/marine-aquariums/">
      Aquariums around the world
     </a>
     ~
     <a href="/news/">
      Marine life hourly news
     </a>
     ...
     <br clear="left"/>
    </p>
    <p>
     <a href="/oceans/">
      The ocean
     </a>
     is our earth's greatest natural resource. It is the place of origin for most life forms. Millions of people rely on the ocean for survival. Twelve million fishermen operate three million vessels landing around
     <em>
      90 million tons
     </em>
     of fish each year, providing work for over 200 million people worldwide.
    </p>
    <p>
     More than 60% of the global population live within 60 km of the coast. The ocean provides the majority of our oxygen and the rain itself. The ocean buffers the weather and helps
     <a href="/oceans/conservation/global-warming/">
      regulate global temperature
     </a>
     and
     <a href="/oceans/ocean-dumping/">
      manages vast amounts of our pollutants
     </a>
     . More than 2 billion tons of carbon dioxide
     <a href="/oceans/conservation/global-warming/">
      are absorbed by the ocean
     </a>
     every year.
    </p>
    <p>
     <img alt="Planet Ocean" class="i-lhome" height="64" src="/_n/i/icons/64x64/Web.png" width="64"/>
     The ocean is home to some of the most
     <a href="/oceans/creatures/">
      amazing creatures
     </a>
     on earth, ~80% of the world's
     <a href="/oceans/conservation/biodiversity/">
      biodiversity
     </a>
     lives in the sea and there is still so much to be discovered. At least 100 million unnamed species live on the ocean floor alone. Thousands of pharmaceutical compounds have been isolated from marine animals and plants. The cures for
     <a href="http://www.scripps.edu/research/fac_int_list.php?int_id=48&amp;interest=HIV/AIDS">
      HIV/AIDs
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="http://www.scripps.edu/research/fac_int_list.php?int_id=41&amp;interest=Cancer">
      cancer
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="http://www.scripps.edu/newsandviews/e_20080519/malaria.html">
      malaria
     </a>
     ,
     <a href="http://www.scripps.edu/news/sr/sr2004/mem04zuraw.html">
      tuberculosis
     </a>
     and
     <a href="http://www.scripps.edu/philanthropy/landing/newsletter/2009_09_othernews.html?tr=y&amp;auid=5349511">
      leukemia
     </a>
     , etc. could lie beneath the waves. The ocean surrounds us all, yet more is known about
     <em>
      the Moon and Mars
     </em>
     . We are just now beginning to understand the ocean and with that understanding comes the increasing realization that the ocean is in
     <em>
      deep
     </em>
     trouble.
     <a href="/oceans/conservation/">
