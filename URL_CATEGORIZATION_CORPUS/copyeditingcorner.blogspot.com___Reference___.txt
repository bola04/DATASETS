                    <meta content="http://bp2.blogger.com/_vk7bFOCX9IE/SBA2YKgQxbI/AAAAAAAAAYw/iEsyw305VVU/s320/AtoZ.jpg" itemprop="image_url"/>

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://copyeditingcorner.blogspot.com/2008/04/basics-of-copy-editing.html">
                      The basics of copy editing
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-7412979241536672879" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     <span style="font-size:100%;">
                      <a href="http://bp2.blogger.com/_vk7bFOCX9IE/SBA2YKgQxbI/AAAAAAAAAYw/iEsyw305VVU/s1600-h/AtoZ.jpg" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}">
                       <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5192710159122613682" src="http://bp2.blogger.com/_vk7bFOCX9IE/SBA2YKgQxbI/AAAAAAAAAYw/iEsyw305VVU/s320/AtoZ.jpg" style="margin: 0pt 10px 10px 0pt; float: left; cursor: pointer;"/>
                      </a>
                     </span>
                     <span style="font-size:100%;">
                      F
                     </span>
                     <span style="font-size:100%;">
                      or many reasons, but mostly because of a query about how a college-level class about copy editing could be structured, I have been thinking about how copy editing can be taught.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      From this pondering, I have decided to start an infrequent series of posts that explore the basics of copy editing.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                     </span>
                     <span style="font-size:100%;">
                      I have learned over time that the first thing a person who is interested in being a copy editor should learn is how to read information and know what it is
                      <span style="font-style: italic; font-weight: bold;">
                       about
                      </span>
                      <span style="font-weight: bold;">
                       .
                      </span>
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      If there is to be any editing beyond simple punctuation and grammar fixes, the copy editor has to grasp what is happening in the article.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Breaking news and gamer sports stories are easy: an event happened. But news analysis and features can be more complex. If the copy editor cannot understand that the analysis has to do with superdelegates in the upcoming presidential election, they will never understand why a quote about Elliot Spitzer being a supporter of candidate Hillary Rodham Clinton is important. Finding any significant, or even many minor, errors in the story really hinges on understanding the larger picture--what it is all about.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Being able to understand what an article is about is especially important for any copy editor who must also write display copy such as headlines and captions. A headline is the news story wrapped up in significantly fewer words, and not knowing what the article is about would leave a copy editor in the dark.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Is it easy to teach a person to read in this way? Perhaps. In really breaking it down, it is Critical Thinking 101.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      At one point in my journalism education, I was taught to jot down key words as I read through and edited stories. Then, when it came to the headline, I had words to work with and build from. In reality, this also made me pin down what was going on in the story.
                      <br/>
                      <br/>
                      Another exercise I remember was writing one-sentence summaries for all of the news stories in a newspaper section each day. (Kind of sounds close to headline writing, huh?) At that time, there was a grade on the line. Now, there are thousands of readers grading my headlines, and I don't want to be the one to fail them.
                     </span>

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://copyeditingcorner.blogspot.com/2008/02/wikipedia-as-source-take-two.html">
                      Wikipedia as a source, take two
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-7604185539781552237" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     The newspaper at which I work had
                     <a href="http://www.examiner.com/a-1247333%7E3_Minute_Interview__Jimmy_Wales.html">
                      an interview with Jimmy Wales
                     </a>
                     , the founder of
                     <span class="blsp-spelling-error" id="SPELLING_ERROR_0">
                      Wikipedia
                     </span>
                     .
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     One part of this question-and-answer interview, however, was edited for space. This part had to do with using
                     <span class="blsp-spelling-error" id="SPELLING_ERROR_1">
                      Wikipedia
                     </span>
                     as a reference. Here is the question and answer:
                     <br/>
                     <blockquote>
                      <span style="font-weight: bold;">
                       Q:
                      </span>
                      What are your thoughts on whether researchers should cite
                      <span class="blsp-spelling-error" id="SPELLING_ERROR_2">
                       Wikipedia
                      </span>
                      pages in their papers?
                      <br/>
                      <span style="font-weight: bold;">
                       A:
                      </span>
                      In general, an encyclopedia is not the type of thing that should be cited in an academic paper, and that goes for Britannica or anything else. When I was in college it would be considered quite silly to cite Britannica unless you were in grade school or something. It's really more about getting that broad background knowledge and that kind of information to get you started. It's a starting point rather than an ending point.
                     </blockquote>
                     I know that this is
                     <span class="blsp-spelling-corrected" id="SPELLING_ERROR_3">
                      talking
                     </span>
                     about academic papers, but I do believe that you could use this to argue why
                     <span class="blsp-spelling-error" id="SPELLING_ERROR_4">
                      Wikipedia
                     </span>
                     should not be used as a source in newspaper articles,
                     <a href="http://copyeditingcorner.blogspot.com/2008/01/wikipedia-as-source.html">
                      which I posted about before
                     </a>
                     .
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     So, there you have it. The founder of
                     <span class="blsp-spelling-error" id="SPELLING_ERROR_5">
                      Wikipedia
                     </span>
                     would not use it as a source.

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://copyeditingcorner.blogspot.com/2008/02/more-from-typos-department.html">
                      More from the typos department
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-4740154654005804148" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     A
                     <a href="https://www.blogger.com/comment.g?blogID=25088553&amp;postID=6561586061372750333">
                      comment
                     </a>
                     was left on
                     <a href="http://copyeditingcorner.blogspot.com/2008/02/blog-post.html">
                      a previous post
                     </a>
                     , and it got me thinking about typos.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Many people may remember a brief flutter of activity in the Barry Bonds steroid scandal that erupted over a typo. But now there is even more because of one small error.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <blockquote>
                      Barry Bonds seized on a pair of typos, complaining in court papers Thursday that the government's mistakes could compromise his chances for a fair trial.
                      <p>
                       The typographical errors showed up in a recent filing by prosecutors wrongly accusing Bonds of flunking a drug test in 2001. They later admitted they instead meant 2000.
                      </p>
                      <p>
                       Baseball's home run king has pleaded not guilty to perjury and obstruction of justice charges alleging he lied when he told a federal grand jury he never knowingly used performance-enhancing drugs. He is asking a judge to dismiss the case, arguing the questions posed to him while under oath were ambiguous and confusing.
                      </p>
                      <p>
                       In a filing last week opposing Bonds' motion for dismissal, prosecutors twice referred to a drug test he failed in November 2001. They later said they meant to reference a November 2000 drug test that had previously been mentioned in the indictment.
                      </p>
                      <p>
                       Still, some media outlets reported that the government had procured new evidence proving Bonds had lied.
                      </p>
                      <p>
                       The mistakes were corrected the next day, but Bonds' lawyers argue in their response to the government's filing that the damage to the case was already done.
                      </p>
                     </blockquote>
                     <p>
                     </p>
                     <br/>
                     Yep, that's Bonds looking to have his case kicked out over typos. Looks like the prosecutor could have used a copy editor.

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://copyeditingcorner.blogspot.com/2008/02/ribbon-cutting-and-groundbreaking.html">
                      Ribbon-cutting and groundbreaking
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-597546050384321636" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     <a href="http://bp3.blogger.com/_vk7bFOCX9IE/R7vl1Y-VcxI/AAAAAAAAAQE/NLGa5XyGH78/s1600-h/redundancies.jpg" onblur="try {parent.deselectBloggerImageGracefully();} catch(e) {}">
                      <span id="formatbar_Buttons" style="display: block;">
                       <span class="on down" id="formatbar_CreateLink" onmousedown="CheckFormatting(event);FormatbarButton('richeditorframe', this, 8);ButtonMouseDown(this);" onmouseout="ButtonHoverOff(this);" onmouseover="ButtonHoverOn(this);" onmouseup="" style="display: block;" title="Link">
                        <img alt="Link" border="0" src="http://www.blogger.com/img/gl.link.gif"/>
                       </span>
                      </span>
                      <img alt="" border="0" id="BLOGGER_PHOTO_ID_5168977702737507090" src="http://bp3.blogger.com/_vk7bFOCX9IE/R7vl1Y-VcxI/AAAAAAAAAQE/NLGa5XyGH78/s320/redundancies.jpg" style="margin: 0pt 10px 10px 0pt; float: left; cursor: pointer;"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     Ceremonies are always great events, right? The city is breaking ground on a new development, or the library is opening a new branch.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     All local papers cover these types of events, and many write the phrases ribbon-cutting ceremony and groundbreaking ceremony.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     This is redundant.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     From the
                     <a href="http://www.amazon.com/Websters-College-Dictionary-Indexed-Fourth/dp/0028631188/ref=pd_bbs_sr_2?ie=UTF8&amp;s=books&amp;qid=1203496912&amp;sr=8-2">
                      Webster's New World College Dictionary, Fourth Edition
                     </a>
                     (the dictionary preferred by The Associated Press):
                     <br/>
                     <blockquote>
                      ribbon- cutting (n.) a ceremony marking the official opening of a site, the commencement of its construction, etc., typically involving the cutting of a ribbon suspended across an entrance
                      <br/>
                     </blockquote>
                     and
                     <br/>
                     <blockquote>
                     </blockquote>
                     <blockquote>
                     </blockquote>
                     <blockquote>
                      groundbreaking (n.) a groundbreaking ceremony for a new building
                     </blockquote>
                     Now, for groundbreaking, I can see more of an argument of using it as an adjective, which is the first use of the word in the dictionary.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Still, I think you would be hard-pressed to find someone who wouldn't understand the word as a noun.

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
                     <a href="http://copyeditingcorner.blogspot.com/2008/02/insure-vs-ensure.html">
                      Insure vs. Ensure
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-3792099363299786993" itemprop="description articleBody">
                     For some reason, I always tend to use
                     <a href="http://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ensure">
                      ensure
                     </a>
                     when writing about guarantees and
                     <a href="http://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/insure">
                      insure
                     </a>
                     when writing about monetary backing. The Associated Press Stylebook says it should be this way.
                     <br/>
                     <blockquote>
                      <br/>
                      <span class="description">
                       Use
                       <span class="example">
                        ensure
                       </span>
                       to mean guarantee:
                       <span class="example">
                        Steps were taken to ensure accuracy.
                       </span>
                       <div class="descriptionmore">
                        Use
                        <span class="example">
                         insure
                        </span>
                        for references to insurance:
                        <span class="example">
                         The policy insures his life.
                        </span>
                       </div>
                      </span>
                     </blockquote>
                     <span class="description">
                      <div class="descriptionmore">
                       <span class="example">
                       </span>
                      </div>
                     </span>
                     <br/>
                     But
                     <a href="http://wsu.edu/%7Ebrians/errors/errors.html#errors">
                      this Web site
                     </a>
                     <a href="http://wsu.edu/%7Ebrians/errors/assure.html">
                      points out
                     </a>
                     that it is not so clear. This is also
                     <a href="http://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/ensure">
                      backed up here
                     </a>
                     (look at bottom of entry).
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Sometimes the right word is difficult to find, and there are not always clear answers. But one that showed up recently in a headline at the newspaper at which I work (though not on my watch) was the use of
                     <a href="http://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/diffuse">
                      diffuse
                     </a>
                     instead of
                     <a href="http://www.merriam-webster.com/dictionary/defuse">
                      defuse
                     </a>
                     .
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Sometimes a few letters off can land you in a place that you can defend (as in ensure, insure). Other times it cannot.
                     <div style="clear: both;">
                     </div>
                    </div>
