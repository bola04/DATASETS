   <div class="sidetitle">
    What we do
   </div>
   <div class="side">
    <a href="http://nielsenhayden.com/makinglight/assets_c/2013/09/awards2013-47.html" onclick="window.open('http://nielsenhayden.com/makinglight/assets_c/2013/09/awards2013-47.html','popup','width=2746,height=2297,scrollbars=no,resizable=no,toolbar=no,directories=no,location=no,menubar=no,status=no,left=0,top=0'); return false">
     <img alt="awards2013.jpg" class="mt-image-right" height="292" src="http://nielsenhayden.com/makinglight/assets_c/2013/09/awards2013-thumb-350x292-47.jpg" style="float: right; margin: 0 0 20px 20px;" width="350"/>
    </a>
    We
    <strong>
     edit books
    </strong>
    , mostly but not exclusively science fiction and fantasy, for
    <a href="http://www.tor-forge.com">
     Tor Books
    </a>
    , where Patrick is Editor-in-Chief and Teresa is a consulting editor. Over the years, we’ve had the privilege of discovering and acquiring the first novels, or in some cases the first adult novels, of some remarkable writers, including but not limited to Maureen F. McHugh, Susan Palwick, Jonathan Lethem, Cory Doctorow, Jo Walton, John Scalzi, Ada Palmer, and Charlie Jane Anders. Among the many books we’re proud to have helped publish are several notable award-winners. Robert Charles Wilson’s
    <a href="http://us.macmillan.com/spin/RobertWilson">
     <em>
      Spin
     </em>
    </a>
    , edited by Teresa, won the Hugo Award in 2006, and Jo Walton’s
    <a href="http://us.macmillan.com/amongothers/JoWalton">
     <em>
      Among Others
     </em>
    </a>
    , edited by Patrick and Teresa, won the Nebula and Hugo Awards in 2012. In 2013, John Scalzi’s
    <a href="http://us.macmillan.com/redshirts/JohnScalzi">
     <em>
      Redshirts
     </em>
    </a>
    , edited by Patrick, won the
    <em>
     Locus
    </em>
    Award and the Hugo Award. In 2014, Charles Stross’s novella “
    <a href="http://www.tor.com/stories/2013/09/equoid">
     Equoid
    </a>
    ,” edited by both of us, won the Hugo for Best Novella, and “
    <a href="http://www.tor.com/stories/2013/10/wakulla-springs">
     Wakulla Springs
    </a>
    ” by Andy Duncan and Ellen Klages, edited by Patrick, won the novella World Fantasy Award. In 2017,
    <a href="http://us.macmillan.com/allthebirdsinthesky/charliejaneanders/9780765379948/">
     <i>
      All the Birds in the Sky
     </i>
    </a>
    by Charlie Jane Anders, co-edited by Patrick and his Tor colleague Miriam Weinberg,
    <a href="http://www.locusmag.com/News/2017/05/12490/">
     won the Nebula Award
    </a>
    . We think all of our authors are awesome.
    <p>
     In 2007, 2010, and 2013, Patrick himself
     <a href="http://www.sfadb.com/Hugo_Awards_Winners_By_Category#editorl">
      won
     </a>
     science fiction’s Hugo Award for Best Editor, Long Form. He thinks there are a bunch of other editors in the field who deserve similar recognition.
    </p>
    <p>
     Patrick also occasionally
     <strong>
      acquires and edits original short fiction
     </strong>
     for
     <a href="http://www.tor.com">
      Tor.com
     </a>
     , Tor/Macmillan’s original-fiction-group-blog-art-gallery-public-forum SF ubersite.
    </p>

     </strong>
     . We’re among the regular instructors at
     <a href="http://www.sff.net/paradise">
      Viable Paradise
     </a>
     on
     <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martha's_Vineyard">
      Martha’s Vineyard
     </a>
     .  Together and separately we’ve taught at a variety of other workshops as well.
    </p>

   <div class="sidetitle">
    A more recent interview
   </div>
   <div class="side">
    <img align="left" height="195" hspace="10" src="http://www.locusmag.com/2013/covers/Issue05_499x646.jpg" vspace="10" width="150"/>
    —with both of us, in the May 2013 issue of
    <em>
     Locus
    </em>
    , the definitive trade magazine of the science fiction and fantasy field. Not available online, but print or electronic copies can be ordered
    <a href="http://www.locusmag.com/Magazine/2013/05/table-of-contents-may-2013/">
     here
    </a>
    . A few excerpts from it can be read
    <a href="http://www.locusmag.com/Perspectives/2013/05/patrick-teresa-nielsen-hayden-the-continuation-of-fanac-by-other-means/">
     on
     <em>
      Locus
     </em>
     ’s site
    </a>
    , and here’s a bit more of it:
    <p>
     <strong>
      PNH
     </strong>
     : “I don’t know how it’s all going to work out, but I’ve always had a sense that the things I am actually good at, which is wading through a lot of stuff and saying, ‘Check this out, this is good’—somebody’s going to be willing to pay for that because I’m pretty good at it. And it cannot be automated.”
     <br/>
     <strong>
      TNH
     </strong>
     : “Actually, there’s one way it can be. I find this very interesting. The fanfic universe is a very inventive place, and worth watching because there’s so much of it and they’re so good at teaching one another to write. The quality of top-level fanfic is now well into the publishable range.”
     <br/>
     <strong>
      PNH
     </strong>
     : “We’re talking about amateur fiction written in someone else’s universe. I clarify this only because the word ‘fanfic’ has meant so many different things historically.”
     <br/>
     <strong>
      TNH
     </strong>
     : “Go to somewhere like Archive Of Our Own, AO3, and what they’ve got are very sophisticated sorting mechanisms. People recommend and bookmark stories, and give each other kudos for good writing, and you can tell the database ‘search for this, this, and that characteristic, and give me the matching stuff with the most hits.’ Personal opinion is variable, but if you get enough people voting for something, the stuff that comes to the top will be good. And since commercial publishers can’t skim off the cream, there’s no limit on how good it can be. There’s an astounding amount of energy and intellectual ferment going on in the fanfic universe.”
     <br/>
     <strong>
      PNH
     </strong>
     : “Our competition at Tor isn’t Ace or Del Rey or Simon &amp; Schuster. They’re part of it, but the real competition is all the other things you can do with your time these days instead of reading books, many of which are really high-quality and terrific things to do. The task is staying interesting in the face of all that. Anybody who’s our age—I’m 53 and she’s 56—we were kids and teenagers in the ’60s and ’70s, so we’re the last or second to last generation who can remember being bored kids with nothing to do.”
    </p>
