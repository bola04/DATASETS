      <h1 class="h-title-medium mbs">
       <a class="a-title" href="/blog/the-long-half-life-of-half-baked-laws">
        The Long Half-Life of Half-Baked Laws
       </a>
      </h1>
      <p>
       So far this year, legislators in at least
       <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2017/03/02/us/when-does-protest-cross-a-line-some-states-aim-to-toughen-laws.html?_r=0">
        16 states
       </a>
       have introduced bills designed to rein in protesters--by, for example, banning the use of masks during protests or by indemnifying drivers who strike activists with their cars.
      </p>
      <p>
       Laws like this jeopardize free speech, needless to say. But they also tend to mutate over time, serving new and unintended purposes long after the protesters they were meant to control have started collecting Social Security. We know this because we have followed this playbook before, almost to the letter.
      </p>
      <h2 class="h-subhead">
       15th Mar 2017
