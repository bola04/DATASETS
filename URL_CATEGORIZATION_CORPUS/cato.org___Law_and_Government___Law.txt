             <div class="text">
              <p>
               President Trump’s new executive order requires the children of border crossers to be detained with their family members. Although a slight improvement over family separation, Trump’s decision raises different questions of whether detaining families together violates the 1997
               <em>
                Flores Settlement
               </em>
               , whereby children have to be released after 20 days, which would necessitate family separation. According to Cato scholar
               <a href="https://www.cato.org/people/alex-nowrasteh">
                Alex Nowrasteh
               </a>
               , the potential Flores problem could be mitigated entirely by Trump if he relied on alternatives to detention (ATD) programs instead of uniform detention of all border crossers.
              </p>

           <div class="text">
            <p>
             Afghans have endured 40 years of uninterrupted war, and there is no plausible argument that war will soon end. In all the debate about troop surges or maintaining the status quo, two critical questions rarely get asked: Why have Afghans been at war for so long, and why can’t the United States and the international community end it? In
             <a href="https://www.cato.org/publications/policy-analysis/war-state-trauma-state-why-afghanistan-remains-stuck-conflict">
              a new paper
             </a>
             , Cato scholar
             <a href="https://www.cato.org/people/erik-goepner">
              Erik Goepner
             </a>
             argues that 40 years of war have fundamentally changed Afghans, and offers recommendations for U.S. efforts in Afghanistan now and for other high-trauma civil war states in the future.
            </p>

            <p>
             The academic and public policy communities generally discuss the effects of government interventions into labor markets piecemeal, teasing out the effects of a particular regulation (such as an increased minimum wage) in isolation. In
             <a href="https://www.cato.org/regulation/summer-2018">
              the new issue of
              <em>
               Regulation
              </em>
             </a>
             , entrepreneur and business owner Warren Meyer surveys the field of labor regulation to demonstrate how the accretion of many different, presumably well-intentioned rules to protect workers can, in total, have exactly the opposite effect, making it difficult for companies to profitably employ unskilled labor and for those workers to develop and advance.

       <h3 class="section-h3">
        Of Special Note
       </h3>
       <div class="content">
        <div class="ui-block-1">
         <h3 class="page-h2">
          <a href="https://store.cato.org/book/frederick-douglass">
           <em>
            Frederick Douglass: Self-Made Man
           </em>
          </a>
         </h3>
         <a href="https://store.cato.org/book/frederick-douglass">
          <img alt="Frederick Douglass: Self-Made Man" src="https://object.cato.org/sites/cato.org/files/download-remote-images/store.cato.org/139907766679/9781944424862_fc.jpg" style="float: left; margin-right: 10px; margin-bottom: 50px" width="270"/>
         </a>
         <p class="text-med">
          Born into slavery in 1818, Frederick Douglass rose to become a preeminent American intellectual and activist who, as statesman, author, lecturer, and scholar, helped lead the fight against slavery and racial oppression. Unlike many other leading abolitionists, Douglass embraced the U.S. Constitution, believing it to be an essentially anti-slavery document guaranteeing that individual rights belonged to all Americans, of all races. This biography from Cato scholar Timothy Sandefur takes a fresh look at the life and inspirational legacy of one of America’s most passionate and dedicated thinkers.
         </p>
