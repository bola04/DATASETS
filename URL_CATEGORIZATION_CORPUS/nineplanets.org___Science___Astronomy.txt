      <h1>
       Nine Planets - Solar System Tour
      </h1>
      <p>
       This website is an overview of the history, mythology and current scientific 
knowledge of the planets, moons and other objects in our solar system. Each page
has our text and NASA's images, some have sounds and movies, most provide 
references to additional related information.
      </p>
      <p class="style1">
       <br/>
       <img alt="Solar System" class="style2" src="images/solar-system-439046_640.jpg" style="height: 213px" width="640"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       Please visit our
       <a href="http://nineplanets.org/news/" target="_blank">
        Astronomy news section
       </a>
       which
gives news, notes and general observations, we also have an interactive
       <a href="http://nineplanets.org/tour/" target="_blank">
        tour of the solar system
       </a>
       (loads in a new window)
      </p>
      <p>
       All
       <a href="http://nineplanets.org" target="_blank">
        planets
       </a>
       can be seen with a small telescope or binoculars and private
       <a href="http://obs.nineplanets.org/obs/obslist.html" target="_blank">
        observatories
       </a>
       continue to provide useful information.
But the possibility of getting up close with
 interplanetary spacecraft has revolutionized planetary science.
Very little of this site would have been possible without the space program.
      </p>
      <p>
       Nevertheless, there's a lot that
       <a href="see.html">
        you can see
       </a>
       with a
       <a href="http://opticsandlab.com/best-telescope-for-kids/">
        telescope for 
kids
       </a>
       or very modest equipment
 or even with just your own eyes.
Past generations of people
found beauty and a sense of wonder contemplating the night sky.
Today's scientific knowledge further enhances and deepens
that experience.  And you can share in it by simply going out in the evening
 and
       <a href="see.html">
        looking up
       </a>
       .
      </p>

         <h1 class="first-header">
          <a href="http://nineplanets.org/sol.html">
           The Sun
          </a>
         </h1>
         <a href="http://nineplanets.org/sol.html">
          <img src="images/basic-pic1.jpg"/>
         </a>
         <p>
          The Sun is by far the largest object in the solar system. It 
			contains more than 99.8% of the total mass of the Solar System 
			(Jupiter contains most of the rest). It is often said that the Sun 
			is an "ordinary" star. That's true in the sense that there are many 
			others similar to it. But there are many more smaller stars than 
			larger ones; the Sun is in the top 10% by mass. The median size of 
			stars in our galaxy is probably less than half the mass of the Sun.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="grid_4">
         <h1 class="first-header">
          <a href="http://nineplanets.org/jupiter.html">
           Jupiter
          </a>
         </h1>
         <a href="http://nineplanets.org/jupiter.html">
          <img src="images/basic-pic2.jpg"/>
         </a>
         <p>
          Jupiter is the fourth brightest object in the sky (after the Sun, 
			the Moon and Venus). It has been known since prehistoric times as a 
			bright "wandering star". But in 1610 when Galileo first pointed a 
			telescope at the sky he discovered Jupiter's four large moons
          <a href="http://nineplanets.org/io.html">
           Io
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="http://nineplanets.org/europa.html">
           Europa
          </a>
          ,
          <a href="http://nineplanets.org/ganymede.html">
           Ganymede
          </a>
          and
          <a href="http://nineplanets.org/callisto.html">
           Callisto
          </a>
          (now known as the Galilean moons) and 
			recorded their motions back and forth around Jupiter.
         </p>
        </div>
        <div class="grid_4">
         <h1 class="first-header">
          <a href="http://nineplanets.org/mercury.html">
           Mercury
          </a>
         </h1>
         <a href="http://nineplanets.org/mercury.html">
          <img src="images/basic-pic3.jpg"/>
         </a>
         <p>
          In Roman mythology Mercury is the god of commerce, travel and 
			thievery, the Roman counterpart of the Greek god Hermes, the 
			messenger of the Gods. The planet probably received this name 
			because it moves so quickly across the sky. Mercury has been known 
			since at least the time of the Sumerians (3rd millennium BC).
         </p>
        </div>
       </div>

       <br/>
       <p>
        The IAU
        <a href="https://www.iau.org/public/themes/pluto/">
         changed the 
	definition
        </a>
        of "planet" so that
        <a href="http://nineplanets.org/pluto.html">
         Pluto
        </a>
        no longer qualifies. There are  
	officially only eight planets in our solar system. Of course this change in 
	terminology does not affect what's actually out there. In the end, it's not 
	very important how we classify the various objects in our solar system. What 
	is important is to learn about their physical nature and their histories.
       </p>
       <p>
        <strong>
         Planet Order from the Sun
        </strong>
        ; Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars, Jupiter, Saturn, 
Uranus and Neptune
       </p>
       <p>
        Our knowledge of our solar system is extensive but it is far from complete. 
Some of the worlds have never even been photographed up close. The 
Nine Planets is an overview of what we know today. We are still exploring, much more is still to come:
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <i>
         We shall not cease from exploration, and the end of all our exploring 
	will be to arrive where we started and know the place for the first time.
        </i>
        <br/>
        -- T. S. Eliot
       </blockquote>
