                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
                     <a href="http://googlemapsmania.blogspot.com/2018/06/is-your-country-ready-for-epidemic.html">
                      Is Your Country Ready for an Epidemic?
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-2990974473448928788">
                     <a href="https://preventepidemics.org/" imageanchor="1" target="_blank">
                      <img border="0" data-original-height="243" data-original-width="523" src="https://1.bp.blogspot.com/-y8O67fPLzrI/Wy0RC31cinI/AAAAAAAA2dw/7lS9iP0p5bk_DzimIhDRewpMHU_9NTr9ACLcBGAs/s523/logo.gif"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     According to Prevent Epidemics more than half the countries in the world are not prepared for the next big epidemic. Many of the most unprepared country are in Africa. Australia and South Korea are the two countries that are best prepared to cope with an epidemic. The United States is also well prepared to cope with a potential health epidemic.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <a href="https://preventepidemics.org/" target="_blank">
                      <span style="color: #cc0000;">
                       <b>
                        Prevent Epidemics
                       </b>
                      </span>
                     </a>
                     has mapped countries around the world based on how well they are prepared to cope with a major health epidemic. Each country has been ranked based on its ability to find, stop and prevent health threats. You can learn more about how prepared each country is on the Prevent Epidemics interactive map. Countries on the map are colored based on their 'Ready Score'. If you click on a country on the map you can view how the country ranks for the different criteria that make up its complete score. These includes individual scores for how prepared a country is for verifying an epidemic outbreak, preventing an epidemic and dealing with an epidemic.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     The Prevent Epidemics ReadyScore is based on data from the Joint External Evaluation (JEE) developed by the World Health Organization. The JEE evaluates a country’s ability to find, stop and prevent disease threats. Every country page on Prevent Epidemics has a 'Take Action' button which provides information about the country's score and suggests simple actions users can take to help their country better prepare for future epidemics.

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
                     <a href="http://googlemapsmania.blogspot.com/2018/06/the-slang-map-of-america.html">
                      The Slang Map of America
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-4789260831773761066">
                     <a href="https://www.playnj.com/slang-map-usa/map.php" imageanchor="1" target="_blank">
                      <img border="0" data-original-height="242" data-original-width="523" src="https://4.bp.blogspot.com/-wemulMeKbl0/Wyzz-GEEAXI/AAAAAAAA2dk/K229Kq7TWzMJZuMWwcoWAIdY5yFb-khvACLcBGAs/s523/mapsmania2.gif"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     You might want to pre-funk before checking out this shucky darn map of United States slang words. The
                     <a href="https://www.playnj.com/slang-map-usa/map.php" target="_blank">
                      <span style="color: #cc0000;">
                       <b>
                        Slang Map of the USA
                       </b>
                      </span>
                     </a>
                     lists the most common slang words in every U.S. state and also includes a little quiz to test your knowledge of America's favorite colloquial phrases.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     PlayNJ carried out a survey to find out the most common slang words in each state. They then compiled the results and released this fun little map. If you select a state on the map you can reveal the two most common slang words in that state. You can also click on the common slang words listed beside the map to view the three states where a slang word is most spoken.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     If you click on the question mark button you can test your knowledge of American slang words by taking the Slang Map of America quiz.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <a href="http://qz.com/862325/the-great-american-word-mapper/" imageanchor="1" target="_blank">
                      <img border="0" src="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-NzuEovm5Q6A/WFTUrlof3kI/AAAAAAAAxE4/zCsoRiREEEAe4gyh5FKodup6PB38cRSiwCLcB/s523/mapsmania2.gif"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     In the evening, when most of the USA is sitting down for dinner, people in the Midwest have their supper instead. This is just one of the many variations in the use and choice of language which is determined by 
where you live in America.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Linguists at Aston University and the University of Manchester have analysed the top 1,000 words used in Twitter messages. They then used users' location data to see how often these words are used in each 
county in the continental United States. The results of this analysis provide an interesting insight into the regional variations in language use across the United States.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Quartz has used this analysis to create an interesting mapped visualization of the use of these top 1,000 words throughout the United States. Type a word into the Quartz
                     <a href="http://qz.com/862325/the-great-american-word-mapper/" style="color: #cc0000;" target="_blank">
                      <b>
                       Great American Word Mapper
                      </b>
                     </a>
                     and you can view a heat-map of its use on Twitter in each county of the USA.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2013/12/20/sunday-review/dialect-quiz-map.html" imageanchor="1" target="_blank">
                      <img border="0" src="https://4.bp.blogspot.com/-9tiGrDoAjmo/UtpbWzgQ01I/AAAAAAAAkTE/PaZk6ye8trY/s523/mapsmania.gif"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     The most popular interactive webpage on the New York Times website in 2013 was
                     <a href="http://www.nytimes.com/interactive/2013/12/20/sunday-review/dialect-quiz-map.html" style="color: #cc0000;" target="_blank">
                      <b>
                       How Y’all, Youse and You Guys Talk
                      </b>
                     </a>
                     . This interactive feature asks a series of questions about your pronunciation and use of certain words.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     From the answers you give to the questions the NYT creates your personal dialect map. This heat-map shows you which areas of the  USA have a dialect similar to your own. You can also view a heat-map for each of 
the individual questions.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     The NYT interactive also asks you whether you call your evening meal 'dinner' or 'supper'. The NYT map shows very similar results to the Quartz map for where these words are most used in the USA.

                     <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/graphics/2018/national/migrant-child-shelters/" imageanchor="1" target="_blank">
                      <img border="0" data-original-height="266" data-original-width="523" src="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-g49LOw9mEIw/Wyy1w0ynimI/AAAAAAAA2dM/qOMM01M2LqsvV3aaO2Nipgj_3OMcCBtzQCLcBGAs/s523/typhoon.gif"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     In
                     <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/graphics/2018/national/migrant-child-shelters/" target="_blank">
                      <span style="color: #cc0000;">
                       <b>
                        Where are the migrant child facilities? Scattered across America
                       </b>
                      </span>
                     </a>
                     The Washington Post is attempting to map where migrant children are being held in the United States. The paper has mapped out  the locations of facilities where it is known migrant children are being held and intends to update the map as more information comes to light.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     At the moment the map is just a simple interactive graphic with markers showing the locations of the known facilities. The result is that the map doesn't work well for states like Texas who have a large number of facilities where children are being held. If you hover over the map markers you can view the name of a facility. Unfortunately the markers in Texas and in some other states are so numerous that it isn't possible to view the name of every facility on the map.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     The Washington Post's map already needs zoom controls so that readers can drill down to all the individual facilities. If the Post does intend to update this map as the story develops a better interactive map will be needed.

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
                     <a href="http://googlemapsmania.blogspot.com/2018/06/refugee-journeys.html">
                      Refugee Journeys
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-1540351703947637251">
                     <a href="http://huelladigital.univisionnoticias.com/crisis-refugiados-javier-bauluz/index-lang=en.html" imageanchor="1" target="_blank">
                      <img border="0" data-original-height="246" data-original-width="523" src="https://4.bp.blogspot.com/-FiSKMqU57oI/Wytx5a_3fZI/AAAAAAAA2cw/gxeKJuqFwu03wZkcPkEZsXztBfUjL841ACLcBGAs/s523/typhoon.gif"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     It is almost impossible to imagine the desperate situations which people must find themselves in to begin the perilous and life-threatening journeys that are the lot of most of the world's refugees. Some politicians like Donald Trump and Italy's Matteo Salvini seem completely incapable of empathy and presumably cannot imagine how desperate the life of a refugee can be. Perhaps they should be forced to read these accounts of refugee journeys.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Spanish photojournalist Javier Bauluz walked with refugees from Greece to Germany in order to document the journey taken by thousands of refugees as they try to make it to the safety of Europe. You can read about this journey on
                     <a href="http://huelladigital.univisionnoticias.com/crisis-refugiados-javier-bauluz/index-lang=en.html" target="_blank">
                      <span style="color: #cc0000;">
                       <b>
                        Seeking Refuge for My Children
                       </b>
                      </span>
                     </a>
                     , a photo report about the long and dangerous trek from Greece, through Macedonia, Serbia, Hungary, Austria and Germany.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Javier's report is broken up into six chapters, each focused on one stage of the journey from Greece to Germany. The route of the journey is displayed on an accompanying interactive map. This map can be used to navigate to any of the six chapters in Seeking Refuge for My Children. Javier's first hand account of the refugees' journeys combined with his powerful photographs of desperate people in a desperate situation combine to provide an evocative and moving insight into the appalling journeys of the refugees seeking entry to Europe.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <a href="https://crossing-the-med-map.warwick.ac.uk/" imageanchor="1" target="_blank">
                      <img border="0" data-original-height="254" data-original-width="523" src="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-pMCEyS-eG44/WgQh1MlWBmI/AAAAAAAAz7M/YEWN-H87dVkGKyin7p2aC8y_F2xh6iDYACLcBGAs/s523/typhoon.gif"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     More than 5,000 refugees died in 2016 trying to get to Europe across the Mediterranean Sea.
                     <a href="https://crossing-the-med-map.warwick.ac.uk/" style="color: #cc0000;" target="_blank">
                      <b>
                       Crossing the Mediterranean Sea by Boat - Mapping and Documenting Migratory Journeys and Experiences
                      </b>
                     </a>
                     is an international research project, led by the University of Warwick, which carried out 257 in-depth qualitative interviews with people who have made this journey.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     The project has released an
                     <a href="https://crossing-the-med-map.warwick.ac.uk/" style="color: #cc0000;" target="_blank">
                      <b>
                       interactive story map
                      </b>
                     </a>
                     which allows you to view some of these migratory journeys on a map, while also learning about the individual experiences of the people forced to make these treacherous journeys.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Each of these individual journeys are mapped to show each stage of the person's migration from Africa to Europe. 'Back' and 'Next' buttons allow the user to follow each stage of the journey made by the 
interviewed refugees. As the map updates the side-panel also updates to provide information about the journey. This information includes the first-hand experiences of the individual refugee and more general 
information about the conditions that refugees experience at each of the mapped locations.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <a href="http://tgm-hussainsjourney.patarmstrong.net.au//" imageanchor="1" target="_blank">
                      <img border="0" src="https://4.bp.blogspot.com/-yAQjzd_tZnw/UnO2SY8H3AI/AAAAAAAAiUs/fhqEqHbBlT8/s523/mapsmania.gif"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     In 2014 the Global Mail created a superb mapped interactive report on one migrant's personal journey. Filmmaker Matt Abbott gave Muhammad Hussain, a Hazara Pakistani about to set out on a journey to seek asylum in Australia, a video camera and asked him to film his experiences. The result is
                     <a href="http://tgm-hussainsjourney.patarmstrong.net.au/" style="color: #cc0000;" target="_blank">
                      <b>
                       Hussain's Journey
                      </b>
                     </a>
                     .
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     The Global Mail's mapped report starts off with Matt Abbot's own recordings of Muhammad Hussain's family
and life in Karachi. When Muhammad set off on his dangerous journey to Australia he took over the filming himself. The mapped report of this journey allows you to view his experiences in safe houses, in smugglers’ homes and, in the final stretch of his journey, across the ocean in a boat that is barely seaworthy.

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
                     <a href="http://googlemapsmania.blogspot.com/2018/06/the-flow-of-human-trafficking.html">
                      The Flow of Human Trafficking
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-6208969700262197404">
                     <a href="blogger: Maps Mania - Create post imageanchor=">
                      <img border="0" data-original-height="246" data-original-width="523" src="https://4.bp.blogspot.com/-QK2Oxguxr4o/WyvLXEICrvI/AAAAAAAA2c8/KVGV43sLEcAfGdJY6svh_LPHA8VQD2H7ACLcBGAs/s523/mapsmania2.gif"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     <a href="https://jhannigan3.github.io/human-trafficking-in-europe/" target="_blank">
                      <span style="color: #cc0000;">
                       <b>
                        Europe &amp; Human Trafficking
                       </b>
                      </span>
                     </a>
                     visualizes the illegal movement of the victims of modern slavery around Europe. There are over 2 million people in Europe living in modern slavery due to human trafficking.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     The map uses flow lines to show the movement of modern slaves around Europe. The map also uses scaled red and blue circles to show the number of modern slaves in each country and the size of each country's GDP. If you click on these red and blue markers the flow lines on the map change to show the countries where the selected country's modern slaves originate.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Europe &amp; Human Trafficking uses the
                     <a href="https://github.com/jwasilgeo/Leaflet.Canvas-Flowmap-Layer" style="color: #cc0000;" target="_blank">
                      <b>
                       Leaflet.Canvas-Flowmap-Layer
                      </b>
                     </a>
                     plug-in for LeafletJS. The plug-in uses Bezier curves to visualize the movement of objects from one location to another (obviously in this case the thing being moved is people). One purpose of using Bezier curves is that you can show the direction of flow by using either a convex or concave curve on your flow line. The direction of flow is also visualized by the library using animated dots which travel along the flow map lines in the direction of flow.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     The Leaflet.Canvas-Flowmap-Layer was inspired by Sarah Bellum's ArcGIS
                     <a href="https://github.com/sarahbellum/Canvas-Flowmap-Layer" style="color: #cc0000;" target="_blank">
                      <b>
                       Canvas Flowmap Layer
                      </b>
                     </a>
                     , a popular plug-in for ArcGIS.

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
                     <a href="http://googlemapsmania.blogspot.com/2018/06/discovering-imaginary-islands.html">
                      Discovering Imaginary Islands
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-6985638191196089727">
                     <a href="http://andrewpekler.com/phantom-islands/" imageanchor="1" target="_blank">
                      <img border="0" data-original-height="242" data-original-width="523" src="https://4.bp.blogspot.com/-JSXzVhsbizA/WyqCCGEhbQI/AAAAAAAA2cY/TlEUkWSVsV0DtTvErx-Qn-0cwVM9HEVYACLcBGAs/s523/typhoon.gif"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     Back in 2012 scientists from the University of Sydney sailed to Sandy Island, an island which Google Maps clearly showed in the Pacific Ocean. Unfortunately when they arrived at the location indicated by Google Maps all they found was a lot more empty sea. Sandy Island didn't exist. In fact it was a phantom island.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Phantom islands are fake islands that have appeared on maps for a period of time, only to be later removed when it is proved that they don't actually exist. Most of the islands come from the reports of sailors exploring previously unknown areas. Presumably they were a mixture of mythical tales and geographical error in positioning real islands on new maps
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <a href="http://andrewpekler.com/phantom-islands/" target="_blank">
                      <span style="color: #cc0000;">
                       <b>
                        Phantom Islands – A Sonic Atlas
                       </b>
                      </span>
                     </a>
                     is an impressive interactive map which takes you on a tour of some of the world's imagined islands. Each of the islands has its own phantom marker on the map. Click on a phantom island and you can learn how the island came to be invented. You can also discover when the island first appeared on an atlas and when it last appeared on any map. Press on the 'Cruise' button and you will be taken on an audio tour of all the Phantom Islands.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <b>
                      Also See
                     </b>
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     <a href="http://googlemapsmania.blogspot.com/2012/11/the-man-responsible-for-googles-non.html" target="_blank">
                      <span style="color: #cc0000;">
                       <b>
                        Sandy Island - the man responsible for Google's non-island
                       </b>

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
                     <a href="http://googlemapsmania.blogspot.com/2018/06/refugee-routes-to-italy.html">
                      Refugee Routes to Italy
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-6969446078940072404">
                     <a href="http://www.storiesbehindaline.com/" imageanchor="1" target="_blank">
                      <img border="0" data-original-height="236" data-original-width="523" src="https://4.bp.blogspot.com/-mSSPZlg5uKM/Wype8gUqK8I/AAAAAAAA2cM/pR-GMVbES8Y_N56_UQpi1l6cTQwrRg62QCLcBGAs/s523/mapsmania2.gif"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     <a href="http://www.storiesbehindaline.com/" target="_blank">
                      <span style="color: #cc0000;">
                       <b>
                        The Stories Behind a Line
                       </b>
                      </span>
                     </a>
                     is a visual presentation of six different refugees' journeys to Italy. This visualization uses simple lines to represent the nightmare journeys undertaken by six asylum seekers from their homes to Europe. These simple lines are used to show the geographical routes and the distances traveled by each refugee. Each journey line also serves as a visual metaphor through which each individual story can be told.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Each of the asylum seeker's journeys in The Stories Behind a Line is represented by its own individual journey line. These lines show the distance traveled, the number of days spent traveling and the methods of transportation of each refugee from their hometown to Italy. Each of the journeys is shown as a simple black line on a white background (although you can reveal the map behind each journey if you want). Red markers are placed along the lines which can be clicked to reveal the personal narrative behind each journey.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     It is possible to switch between the geographically shaped lines to straight lines, with a more analytical view of the distances traveled. In this view the lines are converted to a straight strip map segment. These strip map segments break down the important stages in each refugee's journey and the distances between these stages.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     You can learn more about how and why this visual representation of six refugee journeys was created in a Driven Journalism post by the visualization's authors.
                     <a href="http://datadrivenjournalism.net/featured_projects/the_stories_behind_a_line_a_visual_narrative_of_six_asylum_seekers_routes_t" target="_blank">
                      <span style="color: #cc0000;">
                       <b>
                        The Stories Behind a Line: A Visual Narrative of Six Asylum Seekers' Routes to Italy
                       </b>
                      </span>
                     </a>
                     also explores some of the design and coding choices made in creating the visualization.

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
                     <a href="http://googlemapsmania.blogspot.com/2018/06/the-geography-of-world-cup.html">
                      The Geography of the World Cup
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-3345200002717121800">
                     <a href="https://public.tableau.com/profile/kakuna#!/vizhome/WCRosters/WheredoPlayersplayClubSoccer" imageanchor="1" target="_blank">
                      <img border="0" data-original-height="256" data-original-width="523" src="https://3.bp.blogspot.com/-sabXCX3bVv4/WyomRXXOLyI/AAAAAAAA2cA/wjXRiMOm7VEHmJY5AWpcsffrVelCs0fBwCLcBGAs/s523/typhoon.gif"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     The most expensive football team at this World Cup is France. The combined transfer market value of all the players in the French team is £972.45 million. Spain, who are many people's favorite to win the World Cup, have the second most expensive team, with a combined transfer market value of £930.60 million. Brasil have the third most expensive team.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     You can discover the combined transfer market of every country's team on the
                     <a href="https://notredamecobham.maps.arcgis.com/apps/View/index.html?appid=2a239efb51d94ce3a87795dfe0e9c286&amp;extent=-75.0146,-48.3116,51.5478,77.0495" target="_blank">
                      <span style="color: #cc0000;">
                       <b>
                        FIFA 2018 National Squads
                       </b>
                      </span>
                     </a>
                     interactive map. Teams in the 2018 World Cup are represented by red markers on the map. The country's with blue markers did not qualify for the 2018 Cup. Of these teams Italy stands out. The Italian team has a total transfer market value £598 million, making it the 8th most expensive team in the world. However all those expensive players were unable to help Italy qualify for this year's competition.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Where all those expensive players play their football is another matter. The English Premier League has the most players playing in the 2018 World Cup. 123 players in this World Cup play in the EPL. 81 players in the World Cup play in Spain. 67 play in Germany and 58 play in Italy.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     You can find out more about where this year's World Cup players ply their trade on
                     <a href="https://public.tableau.com/profile/kakuna#!/vizhome/WCRosters/WheredoPlayersplayClubSoccer" target="_blank">
                      <span style="color: #cc0000;">
                       <b>
                        WC Rosters
                       </b>
                      </span>
                     </a>
                     , a tableau visualization of where World Cup players play their club football. Only the England football team in this year's World Cup have all their players playing in their domestic league. Russia comes second in this regard with 21 of the 23 Russian squad playing in the Russian league (Russian league teams must play five Russians, this means Russian players demand high wages in the Russian league and have little financial incentive for playing abroad).
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Manchester City in the English Premier League are the club with the most players in the 2018 World Cup. 16 players in this year's competition are owned by Manchester City. Real Madrid have 15 players and Barcelona have 14 players in the World Cup.

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
                     <a href="http://googlemapsmania.blogspot.com/2018/06/the-american-income-gap.html">
                      The American Income Gap
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-4486432631193419180">
                     <a href="http://www.arcgis.com/home/webmap/viewer.html?webmap=e7e2b47a1382419da4e0b0247d944931&amp;extent=-123.5362,26.1477,-64.0343,45.4232" imageanchor="1" target="_blank">
                      <img border="0" data-original-height="254" data-original-width="523" src="https://4.bp.blogspot.com/-rMfezXZOEk4/WykAtaQSDmI/AAAAAAAA2bo/zeyKlzp74Us6IAdnZcsxq276SA3GrvaIwCLcBGAs/s523/typhoon.gif"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     The
                     <a href="http://www.arcgis.com/home/webmap/viewer.html?webmap=e7e2b47a1382419da4e0b0247d944931&amp;extent=-123.5362,26.1477,-64.0343,45.4232" target="_blank">
                      <span style="color: #cc0000;">
                       <b>
                        Income Extremes for Wealth Divide
                       </b>
                      </span>
                     </a>
                     interactive map shows the richest and poorest households in each census tract area in the United States. The map visualizes two dot map layers showing the number of households earning over $200,000 and the number of households earning under $25,000. The result is a map which clearly shows the income divide in American towns and cities.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     If you select a census tract on the map an information window opens displaying the tract's population and number of households. It also informs you about the number of households in the census tract which have an income greater than $200,000 and the number of households which have an income less than $25,000. The yellow and blue dots don't show the exact addresses of households but are randomized within each census tract area.
                     <br/>
                     <a href="https://esrimedia.maps.arcgis.com/apps/View/index.html?appid=08e4d74ac40a4a25bf852c93bdd442d6" imageanchor="1" target="_blank">
                      <img border="0" data-original-height="243" data-original-width="523" src="https://4.bp.blogspot.com/-anw0I5SHKKs/WnB3oSKRlxI/AAAAAAAA02Y/LYhfPgTLKyYFMV2qSg7nyLNO8_ZZTUgewCLcBGAs/s523/mapsmania2.gif"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     Esri's
                     <a href="https://esrimedia.maps.arcgis.com/apps/View/index.html?appid=08e4d74ac40a4a25bf852c93bdd442d6" style="color: #cc0000;" target="_blank">
                      <b>
                       Predominant Income Range by Households
                      </b>
                     </a>
                     is a similar map, however this map shows the most dominant income range in each census tract in the
 United States. The map uses income data from the 5-year American 
Community Survey in 2016 to show the income range of the most people in 
each tract. Using the map you can explore the reality of income 
inequality in every local neighborhood.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     While exploring the map you might spot patterns which recur in states, 
cities and communities across the country. For example you should be 
able to spot the income divide between many metro and rural areas. In 
college towns you might see low income student-dominated neighborhoods 
surrounded by wealthier neighborhoods.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     The map reveals that a number of cities, such as Philadelphia, Seattle 
and Houston have a thriving downtown core. While cities such as Detroit 
and Cleveland have urban centers which are struggling economically.

                    <h3 class="post-title entry-title">
                     <a href="http://googlemapsmania.blogspot.com/2018/06/apple-maps-in-leafletjs.html">
                      Apple Maps in LeafletJS
                     </a>
                    </h3>
                    <div class="post-header">
                     <div class="post-header-line-1">
                     </div>
                    </div>
                    <div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-76474810779676102">
                     <a href="https://geo.ebp.ch/2018/06/15/a-new-map-in-town-mapkit-js-by-apple/" imageanchor="1" target="_blank">
                      <img border="0" data-original-height="248" data-original-width="523" src="https://2.bp.blogspot.com/-jSu3nFokz1g/WyjIHMMnFFI/AAAAAAAA2bY/nCk8DRV6GKoDCWZMjDUUCrDEdz2jhcnNACLcBGAs/s523/typhoon.gif"/>
                     </a>
                     <br/>
                     Apple launched its desktop mapping API,
                     <a href="https://developer.apple.com/documentation/mapkitjs" style="color: #cc0000;" target="_blank">
                      <b>
                       Mapkit.js
                      </b>
                     </a>
                     , two weeks ago. You can view documentation, demo maps and sample code of Mapkit on Apple's
                     <a href="https://developer.apple.com/maps/mapkitjs/" style="color: #cc0000;" target="_blank">
                      <b>
                       developer page
                      </b>
                     </a>
                     . What Mapkit's developer page doesn't tell you is how to add third party map layers to a Mapkit powered map. It also doesn't tell you how you can use Mapkit's own basemap layers with other popular mapping libraries.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Mapkit's developer page is a little basic at the moment. I've found geo.ebp.ch useful when trying to build a map with Mapkit. Their blog post
                     <a href="https://geo.ebp.ch/2018/06/15/a-new-map-in-town-mapkit-js-by-apple/" target="_blank">
                      <span style="color: #cc0000;">
                       <b>
                        A New Map in Town
                       </b>
                      </span>
                     </a>
                     includes a demo map using Mapkit's driving directions service. This demo map also shows you how to add another map tile layer to a Mapkit powered map. The map loads a WMTS layer from ArcGIS Online. The demo map uses Codepen so it is easy to see how you can use the example to load any other WMTS layer into a Mapkit powered map.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     You can of course also use Mapkit's basemaps in other mapping libraries. For example you could use Apple's map layers in a Leaflet powered map. You can use map tiles from Apple Maps in LeafletJS by using the
                     <a href="https://gitlab.com/IvanSanchez/Leaflet.MapkitMutant" target="_blank">
                      <span style="color: #cc0000;">
                       <b>
                        MapkitMutant
                       </b>
                      </span>
                     </a>
                     plugin for LeafletJS. MapkitMutant is a LeafletJS plugin which allows you to use Apple Map's basemaps. Mapkit's basemaps are a road-map, a satellite (aerial) map and satellite with labels.
                     <br/>
                     <br/>
                     Although LeafletJS is an open-source JavaScript library which is free to use Apple's Mapkit is neither of these things. This means that if you want to use Mapkit's basemaps within a Leaflet powered map you will still need an Apple authorization token and will still need to be aware of Apple's charges. A note at the very bottom of the Mapkit developer page says that "MapKit JS beta provides a free daily limit of 250,000 map initializations and 25,000 service calls."
