       <p align="center">
        <font face="Verdana" size="2">
         On an early autumn day of 1608, Hans 
 		Lipperhey, a spectacle maker from Middelburg, in the Netherlands' 
 		coastal province of Zeeland, applied before the States General of The 
 		Hague for a patent on an "instrument for seeing far". By that time, use 
 		of small rounded glass disks to aid the natural eyesight wasn't new. Those 
 		bulging out on both sides, resembling lentil - or "
         <i>
          lens
         </i>
         " in 
 		Latin - have been used to correct for farsightedness since the mid 13th 
 		century. The idea of a device for magnifying distant objects may have been already 
 		grasped for some time as well. But
         <i>
          this
         </i>
         was the beginning of something else. In the summer of 1609, Galileo, Harriot, 
 		and others, turned the new Dutch invention - the "spyglass" - toward the night sky. The telescope was born.
        </font>
       </p>
       <p align="center">
        <font face="Verdana" size="2">
         This tale grew old, but our fascination 
 		with telescopes has not. Following text is an attempt to give more 
 		of an insight into their inner workings. More specifically, how do they 
 		form images and what 
 		factors determine their quality. Somewhat unusual, the main aspect is 
 		that of the optical wavefront, as opposed to the geometric (ray) 
 		"interface". That deliberate choice, while perhaps somewhat less 
 		convenient, allows for more accurate qualitative assessment, and should throw more light at the underlying physical 
 		fundamentals. Main reference sources are as follows: 
 		(1)
         <i>
          Astronomical Optics
         </i>
         , Daniel J. Schroeder, (2)
         <i>
          Aberration Theory Made 
 		Simple
         </i>
         , Virendra N. Mahajan, (3)
         <i>
          Optical Imaging and Aberrations
         </i>
         I and II, Virendra N. Mahajan, (4)
         <i>
          Optics
         </i>
         , Eugene Hecht, and (5)
         <i>
          Telescope Optics
         </i>
         , 
 		Harrie Rutten and Martin van 
 		Venrooij. 
 		Most of raytracing plots and routine checkups are by
         <a href="http://www.atmos-software.it/Atmos.html" style="color: #0000FF" target="_blank">
          ATMOS
         </a>
         , Massimo Riccardi, 
 		and
         <a href="http://www.sinopt.com/" style="color: #0000FF" target="_blank">
          OSLO
         </a>
         , 
 		Sinclair Optics. Most of diffraction patterns were generated by
         <a href="http://aberrator.astronomy.net/" style="color: #0000FF" target="_blank">
          Aberrator
         </a>
         , Cor Berrevoets.
        </font>
       </p>
