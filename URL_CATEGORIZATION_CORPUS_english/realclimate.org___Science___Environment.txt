       <div class="kcite-section" kcite-section-id="21478">
        <blockquote>
         <p>
          “The greenhouse effect is here.”
          <br/>
          – Jim Hansen, 23rd June 1988,
          <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/1988/06/24/us/global-warming-has-begun-expert-tells-senate.html">
           Senate Testimony
          </a>
         </p>
        </blockquote>
        <p>
         <b>
          The first transient climate projections using GCMs are 30 years old this year, and they have stood up remarkably well.
         </b>
        </p>
        <p>
         We’ve looked at the skill in the
         <span id="cite_ITEM-21478-0" name="citation">
          <a href="#ITEM-21478-0">
           Hansen et al (1988)
          </a>
         </span>
         (
         <a href="https://pubs.giss.nasa.gov/docs/1988/1988_Hansen_ha02700w.pdf">
          pdf
         </a>
         ) simulations before (
         <a href="http://www.realclimate.org/index.php/archives/2007/05/hansens-1988-projections/">
          back in 2008
         </a>
         ), and we said at the time that the simulations were skillful and that differences from observations would be clearer with a decade or two’s more data. Well, another decade has passed!
        </p>
        <p>

      <h3 class="storytitle" id="post-21466">
       <a href="http://www.realclimate.org/index.php/archives/2018/06/will-climate-change-bring-benefits-from-reduced-cold-related-mortality-insights-from-the-latest-epidemiological-research/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Will climate change bring benefits from reduced cold-related mortality? Insights from the latest epidemiological research">
        Will climate change bring benefits from reduced cold-related mortality? Insights from the latest epidemiological research
       </a>
      </h3>
      <div class="meta">
       Filed under:
       <ul class="post-categories">
        <li>
         <a href="http://www.realclimate.org/index.php/archives/category/climate-science/climate-impacts/" rel="category tag">

       — stefan @ 11 June 2018
      </div>
      <div class="entry">
       <div class="at-above-post-homepage addthis_tool" data-url="http://www.realclimate.org/index.php/archives/2018/06/will-climate-change-bring-benefits-from-reduced-cold-related-mortality-insights-from-the-latest-epidemiological-research/">
       </div>
       <div class="kcite-section" kcite-section-id="21466">
        <p>
         <em>
          <img alt="" class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-21470" height="300" src="http://www.realclimate.org/images//Veronika-Huber-240x300.jpg" width="240"/>
          Guest post by Veronika Huber
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         <strong>
          Climate skeptics sometimes like to claim that although global warming will lead to more deaths from heat, it will overall save lives due to fewer deaths from cold. But is this true? Epidemiological studies suggest the opposite.
         </strong>
        </p>
        <p>
         Mortality statistics generally show a distinct seasonality. More people die in the colder winter months than in the warmer summer months. In European countries, for example, the difference between the average number of deaths in winter (December – March) and in the remaining months of the year is
         <a href="http://jech.bmj.com/content/57/10/784">
          10% to 30%
         </a>
         . Only a proportion of these winter excess deaths are directly related to low ambient temperatures (rather than other seasonal factors). Yet, it is reasonable to suspect that fewer people will die from cold as winters are getting milder with climate change. On the other hand, excess mortality from heat may also be high, with, for example, up to
         <a href="https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1631069107003770?via%3Dihub">
          70,000 additional deaths
         </a>
         attributed to the 2003 summer heat wave in Europe. So, will the expected reduction in cold-related mortality be large enough to compensate for the equally anticipated increase in heat-related mortality under climate change?
         <a class="more-link" href="http://www.realclimate.org/index.php/archives/2018/06/will-climate-change-bring-benefits-from-reduced-cold-related-mortality-insights-from-the-latest-epidemiological-research/#more-21466">
          More »
         </a>
        </p>

       <a href="http://www.realclimate.org/index.php/archives/2018/05/does-global-warming-make-tropical-cyclones-stronger/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to Does global warming make tropical cyclones stronger?">
        Does global warming make tropical cyclones stronger?
       </a>
      </h3>
      <div class="meta">
       Filed under:
       <ul class="post-categories">
        <li>
         <a href="http://www.realclimate.org/index.php/archives/category/climate-science/" rel="category tag">

      <div class="entry">
       <div class="at-above-post-homepage addthis_tool" data-url="http://www.realclimate.org/index.php/archives/2018/05/does-global-warming-make-tropical-cyclones-stronger/">
       </div>
       <div class="kcite-section" kcite-section-id="21441">
        <p>
         <em>
          By Stefan Rahmstorf, Kerry Emanuel, Mike Mann and Jim Kossin
         </em>
        </p>
        <p>
         Friday marks the official start of the Atlantic hurricane season, which will be watched with interest after last year’s season broke a number of records and e.g. devastated Puerto Rico’s power grid, causing serious
         <a href="https://blogs.scientificamerican.com/observations/challenges-for-science-in-post-hurricane-puerto-rico/">
          problems that persist today
         </a>
         . One of us (Mike) is part of a team that has issued
         <a href="http://www.essc.psu.edu/essc_web/research/Hurricane2018.html">
          a seasonal forecast
         </a>
         (see Kozar et al 2012) calling for a roughly average season in terms of overall activity (10 +/- 3 named storms), with tropical Atlantic warmth constituting a favorable factor, but predicted El Nino conditions an unfavorable factor.  Meanwhile, the first named storm,
         <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtropical_Storm_Alberto">
          Alberto
         </a>
         , has gone ahead without waiting for the official start of the season.
        </p>
        <p>
         In the long term, whether we will see fewer or more tropical cyclones in the Atlantic or in other basins as a consequence of anthropogenic climate change is still much-debated. There is a mounting consensus, however, that we will see more intense hurricanes. So let us revisit the question of whether global warming is leading to more intense tropical storms. Let’s take a step back and look at this issue globally, not just for the Atlantic.

          More »
         </a>
        </p>

       <div class="kcite-section" kcite-section-id="21423">
        <p>
         <strong>
          A few weeks ago, we’ve argued in a
          <a href="https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-018-0006-5.epdf?author_access_token=d9GwXXnkYQw6itiGny0ZW9RgN0jAjWel9jnR3ZoTv0OdzeJ18XkImxSDnyYEEsE8cCDHkcmVSlMgRd2VzekBpzVfe728uOBU7B1e8unrLGpKyeWhlTvQKe6JHGdYV8iLm4nND7KgW4aTVEUH8xo0AA%3D%3D">
           paper in
           <em>
            Nature
           </em>
          </a>
          that the Atlantic overturning circulation (sometimes popularly dubbed the Gulf Stream System) has weakened significantly since the late 19
          <sup>
           th
          </sup>
          Century, with most of the decline happening since the mid-20
          <sup>
           th
          </sup>
          Century. We have since received much praise for our study from colleagues around the world (thanks for that). But there were also some questions and criticisms in the media, so I’d like to present a forum here for discussing these questions and hope that others (particularly those with a different view) will weigh in in the comments section below.
         </strong>
