            <h2 class="vc_custom_heading" style="text-align: left">
             <a href="http://scriptpipeline.com/about">
              About
             </a>
            </h2>
            <div class="divider-wrap">
             <div class="divider-small-border" data-animate="yes" data-animation-delay="" data-color="accent-color" data-width="20%" style="margin-top: 12px; width: 20%px; height: 4px; margin-bottom: 12px;">
             </div>
            </div>
            <div class="wpb_text_column wpb_content_element ">
             <div class="wpb_wrapper">
              <p>
               Through annual competitions, Script Pipeline discovers and develops writers of all levels for film and television, connecting them to producers, agents, and managers. Since 1999, several produced films and
               <strong>
                over $6 million in screenplay and TV pilot spec sales
               </strong>
               are credited to Script Pipeline’s unique, intensive process of long-term writer-to-industry facilitation. Contest finalists work with Script Pipeline’s executives year-round, getting broader exposure for their work in addition to continuous, one-on-one development assistance.
              </p>
              <p>
               Recent success stories include Screenwriting Competition winner
               <a href="http://scriptpipeline.com/evan-daugherty" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank" title="Evan Daugherty">
                Evan Daugherty
               </a>
               selling
               <a href="http://deadline.com/2010/09/universal-wrapping-snow-white-and-the-huntsman-deal-70975/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank" title="Snow White and the Huntsman - Evan Daugherty Sale">
                <em>
                 Snow White and the Huntsman
                </em>
                to Universal for $3 million
               </a>
               and later taking the lead on studio films
               <em>
                Divergent
               </em>
               ,
               <em>
                Ninja Turtles
               </em>
               , and the upcoming
               <em>
                Rose Red
               </em>
               from Disney. Evan was previously attached to write the limited series
               <em>
                Esmeralda
               </em>
               for ABC Studios,
               <em>
                GI Joe 3
               </em>
               for Paramount, an adaptation of
               <em>
                Myst
               </em>
               for Hulu, and the
               <em>
                Tomb Raider
               </em>
               reboot. His contest-winning script
               <em>
                Killing Season
               </em>
               (formerly
               <em>
                Shrapnel
               </em>
               ) was produced and starred Academy Award-winner Robert De Niro and John Travolta.
              </p>
              <p>
               <a href="http://scriptpipeline.com/tripper-clancy" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank" title="Tripper Clancy">
                Tripper Clancy
               </a>
               , the 2010 Screenwriting Contest winner, sold the road comedy
               <em>
                The Ambassadors
               </em>
               to 20th Century Fox and the pitch
               <em>
                Winter Break
               </em>
               , and was previously on board the comedy
               <em>
                Stranded
               </em>
               for Sony. Tripper is currently writing
               <em>
                Hacker Camp
               </em>
               for Hasbro and an adaptation of the bestselling novel
               <em>
                The Art of Fielding
               </em>
               . His action-comedy
               <em>
                Stuber
               </em>
               sold
               <i>
               </i>
               to Fox for the mid-six figures. Dave Bautista (
               <em>
                Guardians of the Galaxy
               </em>
               ) attached to star.
              </p>
              <p>
               Micah Barnett, whose work was developed through Script Pipeline’s
               <a href="https://scriptpipeline.com/shop/development-notes">
                Workshop
               </a>
               , sold
               <em>
                The Rabbit
               </em>
               to Warner Bros. for six-figures and
               <a href="http://deadline.com/2013/08/nbc-buys-projects-from-hypnotic-one-two-punch-stuber-prods-570972/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">
                a TV pilot,
                <em>
                 Ricochet
                </em>
                , to NBC
               </a>
               . Screenwriter Brian Watanabe had his Script Pipeline “Recommend” action-comedy
               <em>
                Rogue’s Gallery
               </em>
               (later titled
               <em>
                Operation: Endgame
               </em>
               ), also initially developed by Script Pipeline, produced by Script Pipeline’s Chad Clough and Sean McKittrick
               <em>
                (Get Out
               </em>
               )
               <em>
                .
               </em>
               The film starred Zach Galifianakis (
               <em>
                The Hangover
               </em>
               ), Adam Scott (
               <em>
                Parks and Rec
               </em>
               ), Maggie Q, Ellen Barkin, Bob Odenkirk (
               <em>
                Better Call Saul
               </em>
               ), and an ensemble cast.
              </p>
              <p>
               In 2018, production wrapped on the Script Pipeline contest-winning screenplay
               <em>
                The Incident at Sparrow Creek Lumber
               </em>
               (formerly
               <em>
                Militia
               </em>
               ) written by Henry Dunham. Henry will make his directorial debut with the crime-thriller that stars James Badge Dale (
               <em>
                Iron Man
               </em>
               ,
               <em>
                Rubicon
               </em>
               ). Madhouse Entertainment signed Henry a few weeks after he was announced as the winner of the competition, with UTA following suit.
              </p>
              <p>
               Screenwriting Contest finalist Jen Goldson saw her romantic comedy
               <em>
                Off the Menu
               </em>
               produced and released in 2018, starring Santino Fontana (
               <em>
                Crazy Ex-Girlfriend
               </em>
               ) and Dania Ramirez (
               <em>
                Devious Maids
               </em>
               ). Jen was introduced to director Jay Silverman at a Script Pipeline event—the screenplay went into production in less than a year. She has two other features in production, including her contest-placing dramedy
               <em>
                Everything’s Gonna Be Okay
               </em>
               , Andy Tennant
               <em>
                (Hitch
               </em>
               ) set to direct.
              </p>
              <p>
               <em>
                The Devil in Evelyn
               </em>
               , winner of the  First Look Project (Teleplay), was picked up for development by Mandalay Pictures in September 2017. Script Pipeline set up the co-writers, Ben and Tyler Soper, with meetings after extensive circulation to industry. Also in 2017: Howard Jordan Jr., runner-up in the Script Pipeline TV Writing Competition with the comedy
               <em>
                Family Be Like
               </em>
               , was staffed on the CBS series
               <em>
                Superior Donuts
               </em>
               . His first episode aired in January 2018.
              </p>
              <p>
               Outside of its own writer successes,
               <em>
                The Living Wake
               </em>
               ,
               <a href="http://www.hulu.com/watch/209740" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank" title="The Living Wake movie - watch on Hulu">
                Script Pipeline’s first produced film
               </a>
               starring Academy Award-nominee Jesse Eisenberg
               <em>
                (The Social Network
               </em>
               ) and comedian Mike O’Connell (
               <em>
                Dr. Ken
               </em>
               ), received
               <a href="http://variety.com/2007/film/reviews/the-living-wake-1200558577/" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">
                high praise
               </a>
               when it made its festival debut in 2010. In conjunction with the newly launched Film Pipeline, Script Pipeline plans on producing more work in the future, both short-form content and feature films.
              </p>
              <p>
               A number of original feature and TV projects are in various stages of development, and over 100 writers have signed with representation or had their scripts optioned as a result of facilitation. With execs actively expanding the Script Pipeline industry network on a weekly basis, Script Pipeline is continuously on the hunt for quality material. In 2017,
               <strong>
                13,000 screenplays, pilots, and original pitches
               </strong>
               were submitted, making Script Pipeline the leading review outlet for writers worldwide.

                      <a href="https://scriptpipeline.com/may-2018-script-sales">
                       May 2018 Script Sales
                      </a>
                     </h2>
                     <span class="meta-author">
                      <span>
                       By
                      </span>
                      <a href="https://scriptpipeline.com/author/scriptpipeline" rel="author" title="Posts by Script Pipeline">
                       Script Pipeline

                     </span>

                    <!--/post-header-->
                    <p style="text-align: center">
                     <a href="https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/Benedict-cumbershs.jpeg">
                      <img alt="" class="alignnone wp-image-67431" height="250" src="https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/Benedict-cumbershs.jpeg" width="187"/>
                     </a>
                     <a href="https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/Bill-and-Ted.jpeg">
                      <img alt="" class="alignnone wp-image-67432" height="250" sizes="(max-width: 167px) 100vw, 167px" src="https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/Bill-and-Ted.jpeg" srcset="https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/Bill-and-Ted.jpeg 532w, https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/Bill-and-Ted-200x300.jpeg 200w" width="167"/>
                     </a>
                     <a href="https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/151_v9_ba.jpg">
                      <img alt="" class="wp-image-67433" height="250" sizes="(max-width: 188px) 100vw, 188px" src="https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/151_v9_ba.jpg" srcset="https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/151_v9_ba.jpg 1080w, https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/151_v9_ba-225x300.jpg 225w, https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/151_v9_ba-768x1024.jpg 768w, https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/151_v9_ba-600x800.jpg 600w" width="188"/>
                     </a>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Tom O’Connor’s Cold War spy spec
                     <em>
                      Ironbark
                     </em>
                     has found a home with FilmNation Entertainment. The true story follows Greville Wynne, a British businessman, as he helps the CIA end the Cuban Missile Crisis and avert disaster. Benedict Cumberbatch is set to executive produce and star. Another historical spec landed at Gran Via Productions. This one: Alex Cramer’s
                     <em>
                      Rawhide Down
                     </em>
                     . The screenplay follows the immediate aftermath of John Hinkley’s assassination attempt on Ronald Reagan in real time. Paramount Pictures picked up Cory Goodman’s horror spec
                     <em>
                      The Oberline Incident
                     </em>
                     . The plot is being kept under wraps, but it’s been described as high-concept with a female lead. Sam Raimi’s Ghost House Pictures has picked up
                     <em>
                      Crawl
                     </em>
                     , written by Alexandre Aja, Shawn Rasmussen, and Michael Rasmussen. The story follows a young woman trapped in a flooding house with fierce predators during a Category 5 hurricane. Aja will also direct.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Other script sales:
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     – Dueling Leonard Bernstein projects are a go. First up is Michael Mitnick’s adaption of Leonard Bernstein’s biography written by Humphrey Burton. Cary Fukunaga to direct, Jake Gyllenhaal to star. Next up is Josh Singer’s script
                     <em>
                      Bernstein
                     </em>
                     . Bradley Cooper, Steven Spielberg and Martin Scorsese will produce, Cooper will direct and star. Life rights were acquired for this one.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     – Bill and Ted are finally getting a sequel. Excellent! Chris Matheson and Ed Solomon are back to write, Keanu Reeves and Alex Winter are back to star, and Dean Parisot is set to direct. Party on, dudes.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     – J.J. Abrams and his company Bad Robot picked up Daniel Casey’s subversive superhero script
                     <em>
                      The Heavy
                     </em>
                     . Julius Avery will direct.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     – Guy Ritchie to direct
                     <em>
                      Toff Guys
                     </em>
                     from a script he co-wrote with Marn Davies and Ivan Atkinson. The story follows an English drug lord as he tries to sell his business to Oklahoman billionaires.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     – Gabriel Sherman is writing a Trump movie for Gidden Media. It’ll be called
                     <em>
                      The Apprentice
                     </em>
                     .
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     – Nicol Paone’s
                     <em>
                      Friendsgiving
                     </em>
                     has not only found a home at Red Hour Films but has also found an amazing cast: Malin Akerman, Kat Dennings, Jane Seymour, Aisha Tyler, Deon Cole, Ryan Hansen, Chelsea Peretti, Christine Taylor, Wanda Sykes and Margaret Cho are set to star. Ben Stiller and Akerman will produce, Paone will also direct.
                    </p>

                     <h2 class="title">
                      <a href="https://scriptpipeline.com/tripper-clancy-part-2">
                       Tripper Clancy (Part 2)
                      </a>
                     </h2>
                     <span class="meta-author">
                      <span>
                       By
                      </span>
                      <a href="https://scriptpipeline.com/author/spmjm" rel="author" title="Posts by Matt Joseph Misetich">
                       Matt Joseph Misetich
                      </a>
                     </span>
                     <span class="meta-category">
                      |
                      <a href="https://scriptpipeline.com/category/interviews">
                       Exclusive Interviews
                      </a>
                     </span>
                    </div>
                    <!--/post-header-->
                    <p>
                     <i>
                      <a href="https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/05/Tripper-Clancy-PR-Pic.jpg">
                       <img alt="" class=" wp-image-65668 aligncenter" height="324" sizes="(max-width: 264px) 100vw, 264px" src="https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/05/Tripper-Clancy-PR-Pic.jpg" srcset="https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/05/Tripper-Clancy-PR-Pic.jpg 1271w, https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/05/Tripper-Clancy-PR-Pic-244x300.jpg 244w, https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/05/Tripper-Clancy-PR-Pic-768x943.jpg 768w, https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/05/Tripper-Clancy-PR-Pic-834x1024.jpg 834w, https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/05/Tripper-Clancy-PR-Pic-600x737.jpg 600w" width="264"/>
                      </a>
                     </i>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <i>
                      Tripper won the 2009 Screenwriting Competition with his comedy
                     </i>
                     Henry the Second
                     <em>
                      . Soon after, he signed with manager Jake Wagner, leading to several studios projects sold and writing assignments with major companies. His action-comedy
                     </em>
                     Stuber
                     <em>
                      stars Kumail Nanjiani, Dave Bautista, and Iko Uwais. As his career continues to burn a path through Hollywood
                     </em>
                     <em>
                      , he’s juggling multiple projects in both film and TV.
                     </em>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <strong>
                      It’s been almost 10 years (…I know—that went fast) since you won the 2009 Screenwriting Competition with what was, and still is, one of the best grounded comedies we’ve read in
                      <em>
                       Henry the Second
                      </em>
                      . A lot has happened over that span, but everything seemed to get rolling when you signed with manager Jake Wagner.
                     </strong>
                     <strong>
                      What was it that clicked? What do writers, of all levels, need to keep in mind when considering representation?
                     </strong>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     The goal with representation—and this applies to a manager or an agent—is finding someone who actually wants to represent you as a writer and not just one piece of material you’ve written that might have a chance of selling. Unfortunately, when you’re starting out in the industry, you don’t have much choice in who reps you. You take anyone you can get, and oftentimes that person doesn’t share your vision for what your career path should look like.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     I’ve enjoyed working with Jake because we have a very candid relationship, so even when I disagree with his opinion, we can have a healthy debate about it. The cold truth about representation is that when you book projects and generate income, your reps work harder for you. So the trick is finding reps that will work their ass off for you even if you’re in a slump because they know you can write your way out of it.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <strong>
                      After winning the contest, you took part in the Fox writing program.
                      <em>
                       The Ambassadors
                      </em>
                      and
                      <em>
                       Winter Break
                      </em>
                      followed. Both were picked up. How did the program help push you forward, both in your development as a writer and your knowledge of the industry?
                     </strong>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     The Fox Writers Studio was an unbelievable experience, and I’m still friends with (and even working with) several people from that program. I’d say the most educational part of that job was working directly with studio execs at Fox, pitching them feature ideas, developing the script with them, getting their insights on a weekly basis. . . . When you’re writing a spec, you’re alone on an island, and you have no idea what producers or studio execs will think of your work. So at Fox, getting a constant window into their thought process was invaluable and definitely changed the way I think about movie concepts from a macro level.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <strong>
                      Regardless of the fact you’re able to make a living off of writing, there’s surely a bit of frustration when a project is sold but goes unproduced, even if that’s a reality every writer recognizes. Is it easy to brush it off and move on?
                     </strong>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     No. It’s never easy to brush off a project that dies on the vine. You spent an extraordinary amount of time writing and rewriting it. A lot of times it can be really heartbreaking because there are a million ways for a feature project to fail and it’s almost never for the reason you might think. The only thing I can do is remind myself how fortunate I am to get to do this for a living, so if/when a project gets a green light, I can consider it a huge bonus. I also find it helpful not to dwell on projects after you hit send on the email and turn it into the studio. At that point, it’s out of your hands and up to the movie gods, so all you can do is move on and focus on the next thing.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <strong>
                      <em>
                       Henry the Second
                      </em>
                      has had some veterans (Shawn Levy and 21 Laps) shepherding it for years. What have been the diversions in getting it made?
                     </strong>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     21 Laps is still on board. It’s been a long, strange trip and I’m still hopeful that it’ll get made one day. I can’t tell you exactly why it hasn’t been made yet. We’ve come very close several times, but I think it has a lot to do with the tetris game of finding the right piece of talent for the right price who’s available at the right time and that has the right potential upside for the marketplace. Any original project that’s not based on IP has a tough road ahead, so the obstacles we’ve faced on Henry are not that uncommon. Some pretty amazing films took forever to get made, so who knows?
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <strong>
                      You sold the feature action-comedy
                      <em>
                       Stuber
                      </em>
                      to Fox, starring Kumail Nanjiani, Dave Bautista, and Iko Uwais. What was the process like from idea, to finished script, to getting such stellar talent attached?
                     </strong>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Around December of 2015 my manager, Jake, sent me an email with a title (
                     <em>
                      Stuber
                     </em>
                     ) and all he knew was maybe there was a comedy version of Collateral about an Uber driver named Stu. I have a deep love of 80s action comedies, and the characters, storyline and structure hit me immediately.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     The next morning, I put together a three-page treatment and then a month or so later I had a draft of the script. It took a minute to find the right producers, and then in April of 2016, the script went out to every buyer in town, which led to Fox buying the script. After that, I did a couple of passes for the studio, and then our director, Michael Dowse, came on board, had some more notes, and then it was a matter of finding the right cast for it, which is its own rollercoaster.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     I give all the credit to Dowse, the producers, and our exec at Fox for believing in the project and helping Kumail, Dave, and the rest of the cast see how much fun this movie could be. Most things in development at studios will never make it into production, which really makes you beyond grateful when it actually comes together.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <strong>
                      Adapting a book to a screenplay with
                      <em>
                       The Art of Fielding
                      </em>
                      : is it a different beast, or not necessarily? How closely do you work with the author of the material, if at all?
                     </strong>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Adapting a book is a slightly different process than working on an original idea. For starters, you have to determine how much the producers/studio execs love the material. Sometimes a place might own the rights to a book, but they’ll tell the writer: “All we really like is the basic concept, so feel free to use creative license for the rest.”
                     <em>
                      The Art of Fielding
                     </em>
                     is one of my favorite books of all time—I read it several years before it even became a potential job—so the producers and director and I all agreed that we’d try to stay as true to the novel as possible.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Novels don’t always have a traditional three-act structure, which is more common in features, so the first major decision is figuring out how to structure it as a movie. I spoke with the author a few times during the outline phase, which was super helpful, but then with each draft, I found myself needing to distance myself from the source material. At some point you have to ask yourself: “Am I writing the best adaptation or the best movie?” And that might mean cutting things you adore from the book or creating an extra scene here or there to bridge a storyline.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     With a novel like
                     <em>
                      The Art of Fielding
                     </em>
                     , the characters are so rich and so layered that the most challenging obstacle for me was figuring out how to keep the thing under 125 pages.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <strong>
                      There have been a number of other scripts—
                      <em>
                       Hacker Camp
                      </em>
                      with Hasbro,
                      <em>
                       Stranded
                      </em>
                      for Sony—all features. All comedies or a variation thereof. Is TV on the horizon? Directing?
                     </strong>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     I think every screenwriter hopes to direct one day. I will likely cross that bridge down the line, but director jobs don’t grow on trees, so I will have to wait for the right opportunity. I actually feel like producing is a kind of a parallel skillset to screenwriting, since you’re almost always wearing a producer hat, asking yourself if a particular assignment is a good fit for you, or if a spec idea has a strong enough concept to find a studio home, having to interface with studio execs, managing expectations, etc.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     TV is definitely something I’d like to get into and I have one project now that’s in its early stages, but just like features, it’s not easy to get a project off the ground, so we’ll just have to see how it goes.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <strong>
                      Dream project to write. . . .
                     </strong>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     I love writing comedies, but I always find myself gravitating toward more serious subject matter. I’m currently writing a spec that’s pretty much a dream project. It’s based on a true story and it’s not remotely funny. Will it be any good? I’d like to hope so, but who the hell knows? It’s pushing me out of my comfort zone, which is something all writers should try from time to time. Be willing to suck every once in a while!
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     I guess for me a “dream project” is defined as anything that I’d be pumped to see on screen, so it’s a bit of a moving target as my tastes change.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <strong>
                      Million dollar question—and we probably know the answer. There’s no real secret to all this, right? Part timing, part skill, part luck? Should be noted, too, that you’ve always been the most gracious and modest writer we’ve come across, and that truly goes a long way in maintaining and growing a flawless reputation in Hollywood.
                     </strong>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <strong>
                      However, beyond the intangibles, how does a writer get noticed and
                      <em>
                       stay
                      </em>
                      noticed? Meaning, we always hear about the ways writers can break in, whether it’s through a discovery platform like Script Pipeline or by catching the attention of someone in an influential position, but how do you keep the fire going once you have that spark?
                     </strong>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     No simple answer here. Work ethic is a huge part of it. There’s that famous quote: “I hate writing, but I love having written.” I’ve seen that with a lot of aspiring screenwriters. They fall in love with the romantic notion of being a screenwriter more than actually being a screenwriter. It’s a job. It’s a grind. And if you aren’t willing to generate new ideas and write new pages, you’re susceptible to having a short career.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Another part of the equation that helps is if you can avoid being an asshole. I know that sounds obvious, but the notes process on a script can be daunting and exhausting, and you’ll feel like people are trying to tear down all the great work you’ve done, but you have to keep your composure and keep the debate constructive. Remind yourself that everyone involved wants the best version of this script, so if there’s a disagreement, find a polite way to get to the heart of it without making enemies and burning bridges.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Lastly, I’d say you have to find a way to put yourself in a mental state where you’re willing to absorb the ups and the downs of the job. Don’t celebrate too hard on the highs, and don’t get too depressed with the lows. Be humble and recognize that anyone who’s willing to cut you a check is helping you extend this absurd fantasy of being a screenwriter.
                    </p>
                    <hr/>
                    <p>
                     <a href="https://www.imdb.com/name/nm2945690" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
                      <b>
                       Tripper Clancy
                      </b>
                     </a>
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     After working in the Fox Writers Studio in 2011, Tripper has gone on to write comedies and dramas of all shapes and sizes for Columbia Pictures, 20th Century Fox, Fox Animation, Paramount Animation, Hasbro, Amazon, and studios abroad. His original spec script,
                     <em>
                      Stuber
                     </em>
                     , sold to Fox in April of 2016 and stars Kumail Nanjiani and Dave Bautista. He is also adapting the New York Times’ bestselling novel,
                     <em>
                      The Art of Fielding
                     </em>
                     , for producer Mike Tollin and Mandalay Sports Media, with Craig Johnson directing. Tripper has written two foreign language films, including Wolfgang Petersen’s German bank heist comedy,
                     <em>
                      Four Against the Bank
                     </em>
                     , which released in 2016.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Tripper lives in Los Angeles with his wife, Maggie, and their daughters, Olive and Ruby.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <em>
                      Follow Tripper:
                     </em>
                     <a href="http://twitter.com/TripperClancy" rel="noopener" target="_blank">
                      Twitter
                     </a>
                    </p>

                     <h2 class="title">
                      <a href="https://scriptpipeline.com/pride-screenplay">
                       Pride – Screenplay
                      </a>
                     </h2>
                     <span class="meta-author">
                      <span>
                       By
                      </span>
                      <a href="https://scriptpipeline.com/author/scriptpipeline" rel="author" title="Posts by Script Pipeline">
                       Script Pipeline
                      </a>
                     </span>
                     <span class="meta-category">
                      |
                      <a href="https://scriptpipeline.com/category/screenplays-and-pilots">
                       Essential Reading - Screenplays and Pilots
                      </a>
                     </span>
                    </div>
                    <!--/post-header-->
                    <p>
                     <a href="https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/pride.jpg">
                      <img alt="" class="size-full wp-image-67427 aligncenter" height="304" sizes="(max-width: 638px) 100vw, 638px" src="https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/pride.jpg" srcset="https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/pride.jpg 638w, https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/pride-300x143.jpg 300w, https://scriptpipeline.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/pride-600x286.jpg 600w" width="638"/>
                     </a>
                     We’re deep in June, which is officially Pride Month, so why not catch up on a recent LGBT flick that may have flown under your radar?
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     <em>
                      Pride
                     </em>
                     , written by Stephen Beresford and directed by Matthew Warchus, didn’t receive much attention here in the States despite receiving almost universally positive reviews, and that’s a shame because this movie has it all—comedy, romance, drama, tragedy, and even history. Yes,
                     <em>
                      Pride
                     </em>
                     is based on a true story, a seemingly unlikely one at that. Set in the early ‘80s, the film follows gay activists from London as they raise awareness for striking coal miners in South Wales. The group (the aptly named Lesbians and Gays Support the Miners) reasoned that, since both the miners and the LGBT community faced oppression from the British government and police forces, they could form an alliance of sorts. The enemy of my enemy is my friend, so to speak. However odd the pairing may sound, what resulted was a strong friendship between two communities linked by similar experiences of marginalization.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     Like the creative team behind
                     <a href="https://scriptpipeline.com/hidden-figures-screenplay">
                      <em>
                       Hidden Figures
                      </em>
                     </a>
                     , Beresford found a compelling real-life event that few had heard of, one that seemed almost too unusual or anachronistic to be true, making it the perfect story for a film adaptation. Working-class coal miners and gay rights activists aren’t exactly the most natural of allies, and that serves as an amazing source of tension. Since the mineworkers’ union is initially reluctant to accept the LGSM’s support, the LGSM limits their activism to one small town desperate for help. The men of the village aren’t the most welcoming (the women and children are more inviting), and some are outright homophobic. With the men on strike, the women are supporting their families, and having gays help as well was yet another source of emasculation. At first. Charity and kindness in times of crisis go a long way.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     The screenplay juggles a large ensemble, effortlessly blending historical figures with characters who feel just as real. Some notable real-life characters include Mark Ashton, gay rights activist and founder of LGSM who ultimately died from AIDS shortly after the events of this movie; Jonathan Blake, one of the first men in London diagnosed with HIV and who is still alive; and Hefina Headon and Siân James, members of the Women’s Support Group for the striking miners (the latter of whom eventually became a Member of Parliament, the first female MP to serve her constituency). And although the characters deal with the bleak realities of the time and their circumstances—homophobia, HIV/AIDS, poverty, police harassment—the screenplay and the movie never lose their sense of humor. Both are hilarious throughout.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     “Crowd-pleasing” gets tossed around a lot, almost to the point that it’s an empty advertising buzzword, but that descriptor certainly applies here. The movie works towards those “crowd-pleasing” moments and earns each of them, with the final scene feeling legitimately triumphant. Not to give too much away (though, I mean, this
                     <em>
                      is
                     </em>
                     based on a true story—the rules against spoilers in movies shouldn’t apply to history, but whatever), the National Union of Mineworkers ends up unequivocally supporting gay rights in the United Kingdom, thanks in no small part to the work of LGSM. It’s a story of two disparate communities coming together to forge an alliance and fight for their rights. A story that’s still relevant today.
                    </p>
                    <p>
                     (And if for some reason none of that sold you, Imelda Staunton and Bill Nighy are in it. Everyone loves them, right?)
                    </p>
                    <p style="text-align: center">
                     <a href="http://downloads.bbc.co.uk/writersroom/scripts/Pride-Shooting-Script.pdf">
                      Read the
                      <em>
                       Pride
                      </em>
                      Screenplay
                     </a>
                    </p>
