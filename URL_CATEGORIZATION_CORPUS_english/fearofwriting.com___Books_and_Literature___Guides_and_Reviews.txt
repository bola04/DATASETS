         <p>
          <a href="http://www.fearofwritingonlinecourse.com" target="new">
           <img alt="Fear of Writing Online Course: 8 weeks of fun writing!" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-754" height="94" sizes="(max-width: 600px) 100vw, 600px" src="https://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/FoWOC-header-yellow-banner-8-wks-600.jpg" srcset="https://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/FoWOC-header-yellow-banner-8-wks-600.jpg 600w, https://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/FoWOC-header-yellow-banner-8-wks-600-300x47.jpg 300w" width="600"/>
          </a>
         </p>

         <center>
          ~.~.~.~.~.~
         </center>
         <h3 align="center">
          <font color="blue">
           IF YOUR CREATIVE DREAMS are still in the closet . . . or you feel you’ve lost your spontaneity in an effort to fit into the publishing industry . . . or you just want to put some fun back into your creativity . . .
          </font>
         </h3>
         <h3 align="center">
          <font color="blue">
           . . . then find out how writers and CLOSET WRITERS have ignited
           <br/>
           (or re-ignited) their passion for creativity using this little-known method.
          </font>
         </h3>
         <p>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_25" style="width: 160px">
          <a href="http://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/Milli-Thornton-150px.jpg">
           <img alt="Milli Thornton, writing coach and author of Fear of Writing" class="size-full wp-image-25" height="176" src="https://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/Milli-Thornton-150px.jpg" width="150"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Milli Thornton
          </p>
         </div>
         <strong>
          <br/>
          From the Desk of Milli Thornton
         </strong>
         <p>
          Dear Writer or Closet Writer,
         </p>
         <p align="left">
          Since you’ve ended up here at my site, chances are, you’ve searched the Internet for something a little different. Maybe you weren’t even really sure what that “something different” was.
         </p>
         <p align="left">
          As one visitor put it:
         </p>
         <p align="left">
          <em>
           I found the information on your site quite intriguing and I began to wonder if I qualified as someone who actually had a ‘Fear Of Writing.’ Even now, I am not sure. In fact, I’ve been trying to determine if I have a Fear of Writing, or if I actually have a Greater Fear of Not Writing.
          </em>
         </p>
         <p>
          Regardless of how my current blocked situation is viewed, I know for a fact that I am tired of NOT being a writer. I am frustrated with my reluctance to pick up pen and paper or sit down at my keyboard. I am angry with myself for finding that I currently prefer to ‘have written’ than ‘to write.’
         </p>
         <p>
          Can you help free me from these demons? Can your book and course unlock these chains?
         </p>
         <p>
          It turns out that this visitor, Walt Nickell, does feel that Fear of Writing has helped him. You can use the 60-second survey below to find out if it might also help you.
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_80" style="width: 141px">
          <a href="http://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/Sandy-Fleming.jpg">
           <img alt="Sandy Fleming, reader, Fear of Writing" class="size-full wp-image-80" height="163" src="https://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/Sandy-Fleming.jpg" width="131"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Sandy Fleming
          </p>
         </div>
         <strong>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Milli, you cannot imagine how much positive impact you have had on me, my writing, and I’m sure, tons of other people!
         </strong>
         <p>
          <strong>
           —Sandy Fleming, Pipe Creek, Texas
          </strong>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <br/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <font color="brown">
           <strong>
            60 SECONDS TO FIND OUT IF THIS BOOK IS FOR YOU
           </strong>
          </font>
         </p>
         <p>
          My goal in this letter is to let you know how my book can help you. You can know right away whether you’re in the right place by using these questions.
         </p>
         <p align="left">
          <strong>
           Have you ever . . .
          </strong>
         </p>
         <ul>
          <li>
           <p>
            longed to write a story but felt you had nothing to write about?
           </p>
          </li>
         </ul>
         <ul>
          <li>
           <p>
            heard a voice inside your head telling you your writing sucks?
           </p>
          </li>
          <li>
           <p>
            felt your creative writing was a chore (“I’d rather clean the oven or mow the lawn”) instead of fun?
           </p>
          </li>
          <li>
           <p>
            had your writing painfully critiqued by a well-meaning person and felt the passion go out of your creativity?
           </p>
          </li>
          <li>
           <p>
            studied lots of books on writing and publishing—but the more you do, the more the fun goes out of it?
           </p>
          </li>
          <li>
           <p>
            felt overshadowed by the achievements of other writers?
           </p>
          </li>
          <li>
           <p>
            felt haunted by this question: “There are millions of short stories and books in the world—who cares if I write anything”?
           </p>
           <p>
            Or, if you’re out searching on behalf of a loved one . . .
           </p>
          </li>
          <li>
           <p>
            Do you know someone who would dearly love to take up writing but he or she has been too intimidated to try?
           </p>
          </li>
         </ul>
         <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_12" style="width: 98px">
          <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1494495848/ref=as_li_qf_sp_asin_il_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=1494495848&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=fearofwriting" target="new">
           <img alt="Fear of Writing book cover" class="size-full wp-image-12" height="135" src="https://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/01/2nd-edition-med-e1325903910876.jpg" width="88"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Fear of Writing
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          If even a single question on the above list speaks to you, then my book can help you. The name of the book is
          <strong>
           Fear of Writing . . . putting the fun back into writing!
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
          <font color="brown">
           <strong>
            BUT THE PROMISE IS NOT WHAT YOU’D NORMALLY EXPECT
           </strong>
          </font>
         </p>
         <p>
          Fear of Writing does not promise to show you how to write a novel, find an agent or get published. The Fear of Writing method is designed to take you back to
          <span style="background-color:yellow">
           <strong>
            something more primal
           </strong>
          </span>
          . . . joy in your creativity.
         </p>
         <p>
          We all secretly wish we hadn’t forgotten how to play. We watch kids playing and wonder how we ever got so far from something that real. Fear of Writing DOES PROMISE to show you how to recapture that kind of joy and fun.
         </p>
         <p>
          This method is active, not passive—and that makes all the difference to you.
         </p>
         <p>
          Wait. To be more specific, part of it IS passive, because you get to relax and read entertaining and emotionally liberating stuff (more on that in a moment).
         </p>
         <p>
          But this won’t be a helpful book unless you actually write something yourself.
          <em>
           Fear of Writing
          </em>
          provides
          <strong>
           112 ways for you to
          </strong>
          <strong>
           quickly, easily and painlessly start writing
          </strong>
          —and you can also use these 112 ways more than once for different results.
         </p>
         <p>
          The sky’s the limit! Now you can use that enticing new notebook you’ve been too busy (or too nervous) to crack open.
         </p>
         <p>
          112 easy ways to start writing . . . and that can easily translate into a year’s worth of material for your creativity. A group in Santa Fe has used this method on a weekly basis for almost three years and still never exhausted the possibilities.
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_90" style="width: 155px">
          <a href="http://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/Sandy-Schairer.jpg">
           <img alt="Sandy Schairer, reader, Fear of Writing" class="size-full wp-image-90" height="160" src="https://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/Sandy-Schairer.jpg" width="145"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Sandy Schairer
          </p>
         </div>
         Or the fun.
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <br/>
         <strong>
          I’m still so amazed at how the Fear of Writing method WORKS for me. I can write creatively and leave my stuffy old left brain out of it!
         </strong>
         <br/>
         <strong>
          <br/>
          —Sandy Schairer, Tijeras, New Mexico
         </strong>
         <p>
          <br/>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_86" style="width: 155px">
          <a href="http://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/Jenn-Martin.jpg">
           <img alt="Jenn Martin, reader, Fear of Writing" class="size-full wp-image-86" height="163" src="https://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/Jenn-Martin.jpg" width="145"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Jenn Martin
          </p>
         </div>
         <br/>
         <strong>
          Just read your first chapter last night, and you were speaking directly to me! If my fiancee hadn’t been sleeping I would have shouted,
          <span style="background-color:yellow">
           “That is SO me!”
          </span>
          I’m sure that is SO a lot of people, and I’m not the first to tell you that, but I just had to say thanks!
         </strong>
         <p>
          <strong>
           —Jenn Martin, Hawai’i
          </strong>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <br/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <font color="brown">
           <strong>
            I’M A TOUGH CASE. HOW DO I KNOW THE FEAR OF WRITING METHOD WILL WORK FOR ME?
           </strong>
          </font>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <em>
           Fear of Writing
          </em>
          was first published in January 2000. In April 2014 the third edition was released. In almost 14 years of running writing workshops, weekly writing circles and the online course, I’ve never seen this method not work.
         </p>
         <p>
          One time, a 79-year-old man came to the Fear of Writing Clinic with no previous creative writing experience. All he’d ever written since his school days amounted to some office memos. He came to the clinic purely because he was bored with retirement. After trying the
          <a href="/storyteller-writing-prompts" target="_blank">
           Storyteller Writing Prompts
          </a>
          and sharing his newly-written stories with the group, the other participants LOVED his writing. The feedback ranged from “Was that a true story?” (it wasn’t, he was just using the vivid imagination we were all born with) to
          <span style="background-color:yellow">
           <strong>
            “Jim, if you don’t keep writing, you’re crazy!”
           </strong>
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          But you don’t need the Fear of Writing Clinic to show you how to use this method. The book gives all the ground support you’ll need to unleash yourself into the stratosphere. One woman from Texas described it this way:
          <br/>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_88" style="width: 139px">
          <a href="http://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/Marlena-Damme.jpg">
           <img alt="Marlena Damme, reader, Fear of Writing" class="size-full wp-image-88" height="155" src="https://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/Marlena-Damme.jpg" width="129"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Marlena Damme
          </p>
         </div>
         <strong>
          <br/>
          These writing prompts motivated a creative area in my brain that was lying dormant. One minute I was staring at the title of the exercise—the next thing I know I was shaking the pain out of my hand from writing five pages.
         </strong>
         <p>
          <strong>
           —Marlena Dammé, San Antonio, Texas
          </strong>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_83" style="width: 151px">
          <a href="http://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/Carolyn-Howard-Johnson.jpg">
           <img alt="Carolyn Howard Johnson recommends Fear of Writing" class="size-full wp-image-83" height="179" src="https://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/Carolyn-Howard-Johnson.jpg" width="141"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Carolyn Howard-Johnson
          </p>
         </div>
         <strong>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          Everyone suffers from the occasional onset of fear if not full-blown writers’ block.
          <br/>
          I offer FEAR OF WRITING as a remedy every time.
         </strong>
         <p>
          —
          <strong>
           Carolyn Howard-Johnson, instructor for UCLA Extension’s renowned Writers’ Program
          </strong>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <br/>
         </p>
         <p>
          <font color="brown">
           <strong>
            THE ACTIVE ZONE AND THE PASSIVE ZONE
           </strong>
          </font>
         </p>
         <p>
          The book is designed to deliver both active fun (112 irresistible writing prompts) and invisible emotional healing—for any writing scars you may have accumulated along the way. You’ll find that a certain rhythm comes with it. This is to strike a balance between absorbing the “good medicine” (without having to do anything but read), and actually getting some writing done yourself so the healing is complete.
         </p>
         <p>
          Part of the “Passive Zone” includes four short stories that have been known to subconsciously work healing magic on the reader.
         </p>
         <p>
          Based on my own intimate knowledge of fear of writing, I cooked up four memorable characters and threw them in the fear of writing cauldron. These characters first suffer deeply in rather creative ways, and then—just when you think they’re completely doomed—they burn through to writing freedom.
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_81" style="width: 107px">
          <a href="http://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/doggy-pawprint.gif">
           <img alt="Fear of Writing symbol of the Inner Doggie" class="size-full wp-image-81" height="65" src="https://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/doggy-pawprint.gif" width="97"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           The Fear of Writing symbol of the
           <br/>
           imagination
          </p>
         </div>
         <strong>
          <br/>
          The story “Worm Medicine,” with images I will never forget, made the point real—personal—alive and breathing. Afterwards, I went through and in a few hours fixed the corner I’d painted myself into with my novel. Woohoo!
         </strong>
         <p>
          <strong>
           —BJ Apostol, Santee, California
          </strong>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <br/>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_85" style="width: 154px">
          <a href="http://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/J_R_Turner.jpg">
           <img alt="Jennifer Turner" class="size-full wp-image-85" height="188" src="https://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/J_R_Turner.jpg" width="144"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Jennifer Turner
          </p>
         </div>
         <strong>
          I’d been battling a case of writer’s block ever since the terrorist attack on September 11. Each word needed to be squeezed out of me like lemon juice, and when it hit the page it was just as sour. And then I read “Jack’s House.” Every obstacle Helen encountered, every fear she had, every thought she had, paralleled my own troubles so completely I was stunned. When I finished reading “Jack’s House” I understood what I needed to do.
          <span style="background-color:yellow">
           This was the miracle I’d been searching for. Thank you!
          </span>
          <p>
           <strong>
            —J.R. Turner, Wisconsin, now author of
            <em>
             Stark Knight, Silent Knight, My Biker Bodyguard,
            </em>
            and 5 other titles
           </strong>
           <br/>
           <br/>
          </p>
         </strong>
         <p>
          <font color="brown">
           <strong>
            JUMP IN AND TASTE THE ADVENTURE!
           </strong>
          </font>
          <br/>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_12" style="width: 98px">
          <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1494495848/ref=as_li_qf_sp_asin_il_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=1494495848&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=fearofwriting" target="new">
           <img alt="Fear of Writing book cover" class="size-full wp-image-12" height="135" src="https://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2012/01/2nd-edition-med-e1325903910876.jpg" width="88"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Fear of Writing
          </p>
         </div>
         <p>
          Many of the writers and closet writers I’ve worked with over the  years have felt slowed down by one kind of fear or another about their writing: fear of getting started, fear of finishing, fear of failure, even fear of success—the list goes on.
         </p>
         <p>
          <span class="image-right">
          </span>
          That’s why the Fear of Writing method uses a completely “irrational” approach (fun and spontaneity) that works on you before your logical mind has a chance to ruin things. And then, with this new energy injected into your creativity, you can go on to learn (or continue) the kind of writing you most want to do.
         </p>
         <p>
          But that will never happen unless you get started. Every one of the people who’ve used this method—including the ones who’ve gone on to get published—took the plunge, read the book, and used the exercises. And they found it so much fun that it took the boogey-man out of writing and made it something attainable.
         </p>
         <p>
          That can be true for you also,
          <span style="background-color:yellow">
           but only if you act on your creative desires.
          </span>
         </p>
         <p>
          So, what are you waiting for? Jump in and taste the adventure!
         </p>
         <p>
          Sincerely,
          <br/>
          <a href="http://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/Milli-Signature.jpg">
           <img alt="Milli-Signature" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-87" height="85" src="https://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/Milli-Signature.jpg" width="124"/>
          </a>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          <br/>
         </p>
         <p>
          Milli Thornton
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           P.S.
          </strong>
          Using this method, you don’t need any previous experience as a writer to get started. The emphasis is on fun and creativity—
          <em>
           not
          </em>
          good grammar or literary plums-in-the-mouth.
         </p>
         <p>
          <strong>
           P.P.S.
          </strong>
          On the other hand, if you’re an experienced writer you can still gain all the benefits of the freshness and fun of this approach. As J.R. Turner, author of
          <em>
           Stark Knight
          </em>
          , said: “One great thing about the writing prompts is that I can take a break from a longer piece (such as one of my novels) and have some real fun! But the best part about the exercises is that each story can be saved and worked on again later—either as a novel or as a story that might be worth submitting to a magazine or writing contest.”
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignleft" id="attachment_84" style="width: 123px">
          <a href="http://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/DArcy-Mayo.jpg">
           <img alt="D'Arcy Mayo, reader of Fear of Writing" class="size-full wp-image-84" height="160" src="https://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/DArcy-Mayo.jpg" width="113"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           D’Arcy Mayo
          </p>
         </div>
         <strong>
          <br/>
          <br/>
          I’ve studied hundreds of books on writing. This book has proven to be the best investment ever.
         </strong>
         <p>
          <strong>
           —D’Arcy Mayo, professional writer, Sydney, Australia
          </strong>
          <br/>
          <br/>
         </p>
         <p>
         </p>
         <div class="wp-caption alignright" id="attachment_89" style="width: 131px">
          <a href="http://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/Susan-Smith.jpg">
           <img alt="Susan Smith, reader of Fear of Writing" class="size-full wp-image-89" height="160" src="https://fearofwriting.com/wp-content/uploads/2013/11/Susan-Smith.jpg" width="121"/>
          </a>
          <p class="wp-caption-text">
           Susan Smith
          </p>
         </div>
         <strong>
          <br/>
          Milli’s techniques are so friendly and non-frightening. The book is very laid-back, conversational, and downright funny! Even the cover picture of the dog holding a match to his manuscript makes me laugh every time I look at it. The exercises are fantastic—they wind up your imagination and let it go and then wonderful things happen.
         </strong>
         <p>
          <strong>
           —Susan Smith, San Antonio, Texas
          </strong>
         </p>
