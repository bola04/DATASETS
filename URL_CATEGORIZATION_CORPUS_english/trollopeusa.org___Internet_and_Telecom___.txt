       Anthony Trollope and “The Salisbury Conundrum”
      </a>
     </h2>
     <div class="meta">
      <p>
       <em>
        Posted on: March 14th, 2018	by Douglas Gerlach
       </em>
      </p>
      <!-- <a href="http://www.trollopeusa.org/anthony-trollope-and-the-salisbury-conundrum/#respond" class="comments-link" >No Comments</a> -->
     </div>
     <div class="entry">
      <p>
       <img alt="" class="alignright size-thumbnail wp-image-2506" height="47" sizes="(max-width: 150px) 100vw, 150px" src="http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/03/2018-03-14_11-55-57-150x47.png" srcset="http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/03/2018-03-14_11-55-57-150x47.png 150w, http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/03/2018-03-14_11-55-57.png 186w" width="150"/>
       On
       <em>
        The New Yorker’s
       </em>
       website on 13 March 2018, Anthony Lane comments on the recent Russian spy poisoning incident and its setting in a town familiar to Trollopians, Salisbury:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
        Did he do it? Why would he do it? Such are the questions that Russia-watchers are asking of Vladimir Putin. Needless to say, he is in no mood to answer. What people want to know about is a remarkable saga that began on March 4th, though its roots lie deeper in the past, and which has grown more complicated since. It has spread from a bench in provincial England to the doors of the Kremlin. Were Robert Ludlum to have cooked it up, it would be called “The Salisbury Conundrum.”
       </p>
       <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
        Salisbury is a comely cathedral town in the southwest of England, the towering church being the hub of its existence and, over many centuries, a place of pilgrimage for worshippers and travellers alike. The spire was painted by John Constable and became the focus of a novel by William Golding. The Barsetshire novels, by Anthony Trollope, are widely assumed to be based on Salisbury and its surrounding county. Stroll through the Close—the peaceful area around the cathedral, rich in desirable properties—and you can easily picture yourself in the company of Trollope’s ecclesiastical meddlers. Walk a few minutes north and you arrive at the Maltings, a modern shopping mall that Constable, alas, never got to paint.
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       <a href="https://www.newyorker.com/news/daily-comment/sergei-skripal-russia-and-the-salisbury-conundrum">

     <h2>
      <a href="http://www.trollopeusa.org/wsj-5bestbooksonconmen/">
       Wall Street Journal: Five Best Books on Con Men
      </a>
     </h2>
     <div class="meta">
      <p>
       <em>
        Posted on: October 25th, 2017	by Douglas Gerlach
       </em>
      </p>
      <!-- <a href="http://www.trollopeusa.org/wsj-5bestbooksonconmen/#respond" class="comments-link" >No Comments</a> -->
     </div>
     <div class="entry">
      <p>
       In
       <em>
        The Wall Street Journal
       </em>
       on October 20, 2017, author and columnist Anne Applebaum lists the “five best books on con men.” Number one on her  list is
       <strong>
        The Way We Live Now
       </strong>
       (1875) by Anthony Trollope:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
        <em>
         1. ’No one pretends to think that he is a gentleman. . . . But because he has learned the art of making money, we not only put up with him, but settle upon his carcass as so many birds of prey.” Thus does the upright Roger Carbury dismiss the notion of a marriage between his feckless cousin and the unimaginably wealthy daughter of Melmotte, the mountebank around whom this novel circulates. But no one listens to him: Larger than life, vaguely foreign, with horses, carriages, palatial residences and too much money to count, Melmotte is a man of the “City,” London’s financial markets, and thus a figure of envy and scorn among the harder-up members of the British aristocracy. He’s also one of the first truly modern fictional con men: An outsider who outsmarts the insiders, a “new man” who runs rings round the old elite. This is still the Victorian era so Melmotte ends badly, but not before demonstrating that much of the London establishment is just as hypocritical and false as himself.
        </em>
       </p>
      </blockquote>

     <h2>
      <a href="http://www.trollopeusa.org/new-york-times-should-critics-aim-to-be-open-minded/">
       New York Times: Should Critics Aim to Be Open-Minded?
      </a>
     </h2>
     <div class="meta">
      <p>
       <em>
        Posted on: September 1st, 2017	by Douglas Gerlach
       </em>
      </p>
      <!-- <a href="http://www.trollopeusa.org/new-york-times-should-critics-aim-to-be-open-minded/#respond" class="comments-link" >No Comments</a> -->
     </div>
     <div class="entry">
      <p>
       In the Bookends column of the
       <em>
        New York Times Book Review
       </em>
       , writers Thomas Mallon and Liesl Schillinger take on the effects of criticism. Schillinger points to some observations on the topic made by Anthony Trollope:
      </p>
      <blockquote>
       <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
        <em>
         In his 1875 novel
         <strong>
          The Way We Live Now
         </strong>
         , one of my favorite authors, Anthony Trollope (several generations older than Maugham and infinitely more indulgent of his fellow man), pointed out the hazard of a charitable critical approach: “Eulogy is invariably dull.’ There’s a distinct line between eulogy and fairness, but every critic knows you make more of a splash when you wield a bludgeon than when you bestow a bouquet.
        </em>
       </p>
       <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
        <em>
         Yet Trollope also recognized that brickbats too readily brandished lose their power to stun. If you’re a writer known for dispensing venom, he explained, your targets grow immune to your poison: ‘Censure from those who are always finding fault is regarded so much as a matter of course that it ceases to be objectionable,’ making the dependably whip-cracking critic a ‘caricaturist.’ Whereas an open-minded critic makes an enemy every time he or she lays down a harsh verdict: ‘Abuse from those who occasionally praise is considered to be personally offensive.’
        </em>
       </p>
      </blockquote>
      <p>
       (
       <a href="https://www.nytimes.com/2017/08/29/books/review/criticism-objectivity-judgement.html">
        Read the full article.
       </a>
       )
      </p>

     <h2>
      <a href="http://www.trollopeusa.org/forbes-what-anthony-trollope-can-teach-us-about-finance/">
       Forbes: What Anthony Trollope Can Teach Us About Finance
      </a>
     </h2>
     <div class="meta">
      <p>
       <em>
        Posted on: July 28th, 2017	by Douglas Gerlach
       </em>
      </p>
      <!-- <a href="http://www.trollopeusa.org/forbes-what-anthony-trollope-can-teach-us-about-finance/#respond" class="comments-link" >No Comments</a> -->
     </div>
     <div class="entry">
      <p>
       <img alt="Forbes" class="alignright size-thumbnail wp-image-2361" height="90" sizes="(max-width: 150px) 100vw, 150px" src="http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/Forbes-150x90.png" srcset="http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/Forbes-150x90.png 150w, http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/Forbes-300x180.png 300w, http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/Forbes-768x461.png 768w, http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/Forbes.png 1000w" width="150"/>
       Mihir Desai’s new book,
       <strong>
        The Wisdom of Finance: Discovering Humanity in the World of Risk and Return,
       </strong>
       links the fundamentals of finance to several centuries of literature, history, philosophy, music, visual arts, theater, and comedy in order to make the subject seem less mystifying — more humanizing — to non-financiers. One of the examples in the book is how Anthony Trollope’s novel
       <em>
        <strong>
         Phineas Finn
        </strong>
       </em>
       offers useful lessons on risk management. Carmen Nobel recently interviewed in Desai, the Mizuho Financial Group Professor of Finance at Harvard Business School, f0r
       <em>
        Forbes
       </em>
       magazine. In the article, Desai discusses his approach to interpreting financial concepts.
      </p>
      <p>
       (
       <a href="https://www.forbes.com/sites/hbsworkingknowledge/2017/07/12/what-jane-austen-and-mel-brooks-can-teach-us-about-finance/#67c24d2c72e8">
        Read the article.
       </a>
       )
      </p>

     <h2>
      <a href="http://www.trollopeusa.org/commemorating-the-200th-anniversary-of-jane-austens-death/">
       Commemorating the 200th Anniversary of Jane Austen’s Death
      </a>
     </h2>
     <div class="meta">
      <p>
       <em>
        Posted on: July 18th, 2017	by Douglas Gerlach
       </em>
      </p>
      <!-- <a href="http://www.trollopeusa.org/commemorating-the-200th-anniversary-of-jane-austens-death/#respond" class="comments-link" >No Comments</a> -->
     </div>
     <div class="entry">
      <p>
       <img alt="jane-austen" class="alignright size-thumbnail wp-image-2347" height="150" sizes="(max-width: 103px) 100vw, 103px" src="http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/jane-austen-103x150.jpg" srcset="http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/jane-austen-103x150.jpg 103w, http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/jane-austen-207x300.jpg 207w, http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/07/jane-austen.jpg 260w" width="103"/>
       Jane Austen passed away on July 18, 1817. Anthony Trollope was but two years old at the time, though by the age of 19 years, as he wrote in his
       <strong>
        <em>
         An Autobiography
        </em>
       </strong>
       , he had
       <em>
        “
       </em>
       already made up my mind that
       <em>
        Pride and Prejudice
       </em>
       was the best novel in the English language.” This belief held steady until Trollope encountered Thackeray, at which point
       <em>
        Henry Esmond
       </em>
       became his favorite novel.
      </p>
      <p>
       Trollope still held Austen in high esteem for the rest of his life. In the late 1860s as he lectured across England, one of his presentations was a survey course in English literature entitled
       <em>
        “
       </em>
       On English Prose Fiction as Rational Amusement.” Published privately in 1870 in a volume entitled
       <em>
        <strong>
         Four Lectures
        </strong>
       </em>
       , the lecture traces the history of the novel from its very beginnings with Sir Philip Sidney’s 1580 proto-novels through Thackeray’s Victorian classics. Though Austen was not widely read by the Victorians until the first posthumous republication of her novels in 1870, Trollope was an admirer of Austen as a “surely great novelist.” Following is an excerpt from Trollope’s essay extolling Austen’s virtues.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a class="more-link" href="http://www.trollopeusa.org/commemorating-the-200th-anniversary-of-jane-austens-death/#more-2345">
        <span aria-label="Continue reading Commemorating the 200th Anniversary of Jane Austen’s Death">
         (more…)
        </span>
       </a>
      </p>

      <p>
       <a href="https://www.amazon.com/gp/product/B01I3LSCCU/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=B01I3LSCCU&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=trsous-20&amp;linkId=ce3ccd0006f21d4d2d36c01184ccbdb5">

       <strong>
        The Way We Live Now
       </strong>

       </strong>

     <h2>
      <a href="http://www.trollopeusa.org/bibliophilia-by-n-john-hall/">
       “Bibliophilia” by N. John Hall
      </a>
     </h2>
     <div class="meta">
      <p>
       <em>
        Posted on: February 18th, 2017	by Douglas Gerlach
       </em>
      </p>
      <!-- <a href="http://www.trollopeusa.org/bibliophilia-by-n-john-hall/#respond" class="comments-link" >No Comments</a> -->
     </div>
     <div class="entry">
      <p>
       <a href="https://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1567925618/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=1567925618&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=trsous-20&amp;linkId=c1c86b9a2a31b97d643f905c677ba9b0">
        <img alt="Bibliophilia" class="alignright size-medium wp-image-2306" height="300" sizes="(max-width: 215px) 100vw, 215px" src="http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/Bibliophilia-215x300.png" srcset="http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/Bibliophilia-215x300.png 215w, http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/Bibliophilia-108x150.png 108w, http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/Bibliophilia.png 405w" width="215"/>
       </a>
       From noted Trollopian scholar and biographer N. John Hall comes a sequel to his 2011 epistolatory novel
       <a href="https://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1567924123/ref=as_li_qf_sp_asin_il_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=trsous-20&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;creativeASIN=1567924123&amp;linkId=c90478df194db494e7a79eacc31ccb56">
        Correspondence: An Adventure in Letters
       </a>
       entitled
       <a href="https://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1567925618/ref=as_li_qf_sp_asin_il_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=trsous-20&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;creativeASIN=1567925618&amp;linkId=ff03f86932d871812667c77d53f14d1e">
        Bibliophilia: An Epistolary Novel of One Man’s Obsession with Book Collecting
       </a>
       . Flush with $400,000 dollars from selling his great great grandfather’s correspondence with Victorian authors, Larry Dickerson, retired bank clerk, amateur editor, and literary neophyte, needs a creative outlet for his newly acquired funds and quickly escalating love of all things bookish.
      </p>
      <p>
       In
       <a href="https://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1567925618/ref=as_li_qf_sp_asin_il_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=trsous-20&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;creativeASIN=1567925618&amp;linkId=ff03f86932d871812667c77d53f14d1e">
        Bibliophilia
       </a>
       , Dickerson turns his attention to
       <em>
        New Yorker
       </em>
       authors, with his determination to acquire growing with each new purchase. James Thurber, E.B. White, Vladimir Nabokov, J.D. Salinger, and Dorothy Parker all find their way onto Larry’s shortlist, standing along previously acquired greats Trollope, Dickens, Thackeray, and Hardy. Being sensible, he approaches the biggest names in the rare book field with childish glee, and with refreshing brashness, finds himself handling copies of authors’ most coveted titles, touring the New Yorker offices, and then involved in one of the biggest scandals book collecting has ever seen. Will the thrill of the chase overwhelm Larry’s ability to see reason? Will his appetite outpace his resources? Join him on this journey as he discovers just how far he’s willing to take his obsession.
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="https://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1567925618/ref=as_li_tl?ie=UTF8&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=9325&amp;creativeASIN=1567925618&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;tag=trsous-20&amp;linkId=4a2de5b822d0569dbd0ae7f8fcb3b186" target="_blank">

       <em>
        Posted on: February 1st, 2017	by Douglas Gerlach
       </em>
      </p>
      <!-- <a href="http://www.trollopeusa.org/presidents-letter-february-2017/#respond" class="comments-link" >No Comments</a> -->

     <div class="entry">
      <p>
       <img alt="Doug Gerlach" class="alignright size-thumbnail wp-image-1924" height="150" sizes="(max-width: 150px) 100vw, 150px" src="http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/DougGerlachTrollopeSociety-150x150.jpg" srcset="http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/DougGerlachTrollopeSociety-150x150.jpg 150w, http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/DougGerlachTrollopeSociety-300x300.jpg 300w, http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/DougGerlachTrollopeSociety-90x90.jpg 90w, http://www.trollopeusa.org/wp-content/uploads/2015/08/DougGerlachTrollopeSociety.jpg 411w" width="150"/>

      </p>
      <p>
       We will welcome Benjamin Parker of Brown University as the speaker for our Winter Reception on February 20, 2017 at St. Bartholomew’s Church. Professor Parker’s talk is entitled “Does Trollope’s Psychology Follow Newton’s Laws?” You may
       <a href="http://www.trollopeusa.org/event/2016-winter-reception/">
        reserve tickets on the TrollopeUSA.org website
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       A number of other activities are on the 2017 calendar. The Center for Fiction in New York will host a reading group of William Makepeace Thackeray’s
       <strong>
        Vanity Fair
       </strong>
       , led by scholar and Trollope Society director N. John “Jack” Hall. Thackeray was Trollope’s idol and mentor, and this is sure to be an invigorating excursion for Trollopians. The group will meet from February through May 2017, and is $120 for members of The Trollope Society.
       <a href="http://www.trollopeusa.org/event/new-york-reading-group-vanity-fair/">
        Tickets are available here
       </a>
       .
