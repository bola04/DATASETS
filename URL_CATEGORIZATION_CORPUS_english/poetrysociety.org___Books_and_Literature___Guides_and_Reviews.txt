          <h2>
           <a href="http://www.poetrysociety.org/psa/poetry/crossroads/own_words/ponge/">
            Jonathan Larson on Francis Ponge's Nioque of the Early-Spring
           </a>
          </h2>
          <div class="content">
           <div class="left">
            <a href="http://www.poetrysociety.org/psa/poetry/crossroads/own_words/ponge/">
             <img src="http://www.poetrysociety.org/psa/set/page_193/icon:en-us.jpg?v=1529331705"/>
            </a>
           </div>
           <p>
            I decided to translate Francis Ponge's
            <em>
             Nioque of the Early-Spring
            </em>
            , in part, for its bridge from the earlier Ponge of
            <em>
             Things
            </em>
            (playful paeans to objects) and the later Ponge of
            <em>
             Making of the Pré
            </em>
            and
            <em>
             The Table
            </em>
            , etc.(serial investigations into the changing thing, prairie, commodity…), but also because the time spanned by the document gains a distinct charge due to their circumstantial nature.

          <h2>
           <a href="http://www.poetrysociety.org/psa/poetry/crossroads/tributes/page/">
            From the Archive: Those Sunglasses
           </a>
          </h2>
          <h4 class="byline">
           by
           <a class="author" href="http://www.poetrysociety.org/psa/poetry/poets/peggy-fox/">
            Peggy Fox
           </a>
          </h4>
          <div class="content">
           <div class="left">
            <a href="http://www.poetrysociety.org/psa/poetry/crossroads/tributes/page/">
             <img src="http://www.poetrysociety.org/psa/set/page_187/icon:en-us.jpg?v=1529331705"/>
            </a>
           </div>
           <p>
            James Laughlin's delightful poem "Those Sunglasses" about his good friend, Tennessee Williams, was almost completely lost to the world through my own over-organization. Having finally finished (with my co-editor Thomas Keith)
            <em>
             The Luck of Friendship: The Letters of Tennessee Williams and James Laughlin

            (which my boss, James Laughlin had asked me to do almost twenty-five years ago, as part of a series of correspondence volumes with his most famous New Directions authors, published by W. W. Norton &amp; Co.), I was tidying up my files and decided to go through the JL/TW-related material that I had brought home from New Directions as James Laughlin's literary co-executor when I retired in 2011.

          <h2>
           <a href="http://www.poetrysociety.org/psa/poetry/crossroads/own_words/xie/">
            Jenny Xie on “Melancholia”
           </a>
          </h2>
          <div class="content">
           <div class="left">
            <a href="http://www.poetrysociety.org/psa/poetry/crossroads/own_words/xie/">
             <img src="http://www.poetrysociety.org/psa/set/page_185/icon:en-us.jpg?v=1529331705"/>
            </a>
           </div>
           <p>
            In "Mourning and Melancholia" (1917), Freud traces the distinction between the psychological state of mourning, a normal response to loss that is finite, and melancholia, a pathological mourning whose labor is endless. Whereas in mourning, the object of loss is clear and can be released by the mourner with time, in melancholia, what has been lost can remain hidden and becomes internalized—"devoured" by the ego, as Freud writes.

            "To Body What's Around Me" is a love poem with a problem: it does not clarify who the You of its address is. At moments it feels like a beloved other: "These are the days in which you come to me…" But at other moments that You is not quite human, not quite beloved.

             <img src="http://www.poetrysociety.org/psa/set/page_11/icon:en-us.jpg?v=1529331707"/>
            </a>
           </div>
           <p>
            This poem started when a friend challenged me to write something "elegant." On G-chat, I followed his suggestion with a "lol." Elegance is something my poems never aspire to. I write about disappointing one night stands, peeing on street corners at night, getting too drunk to hide how I feel. As a woman, I almost cringe at the idea of being elegant, weary from men on the street telling me to smile and averse to anything that insists I "behave" or be "lady-like."
