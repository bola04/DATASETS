        <a href="https://www.fordbarn.com/2017/09/13/how-about-an-f1/" rel="bookmark" title="Permanent Link to How About An F1?">
         How About An F1?
        </a>
       </h2>
       <div class="post-info">
        Posted on September 13th, 2017 by Ryan
        <br/>
        Filed under:
        <a href="https://www.fordbarn.com/category/early-v8/" rel="category tag">
         Early V8
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="https://www.fordbarn.com/category/late-v8/" rel="category tag">
         Late V8
        </a>
       </div>
       <div class="post-text entry">
        <p>
         <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-931" height="404" sizes="(max-width: 569px) 100vw, 569px" src="https://www.fordbarn.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/f1.jpg" srcset="https://www.fordbarn.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/f1.jpg 569w, https://www.fordbarn.com/wp-content/uploads/2017/09/f1-300x213.jpg 300w" width="569"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         So I’ve given up on my hunt for a 40/41 Ford Pickup for a couple of reasons. First, the prices on these haulers have gotten so high that it doesn’t really make sense for a guy like me to use one as it was intended – a truck. Secondly, I’m doing a lot of wood working lately and the 40/41 bed isn’t wide enough for a standard sheet of plywood.
        </p>
        <p>
         So, what now? Actually, I’ve started looking for an F1… or maybe even an early F100. Anyone have a line on a nice solid driver with no rust?
         <a href="https://www.fordbarn.com/contact-us/general-inquiries/">
          Look me up if so!
         </a>
        </p>

         The 1940 Ford Cutaway
        </a>
       </h2>
       <div class="post-info">
        Posted on January 21st, 2015 by Ryan
        <br/>
        Filed under:
        <a href="https://www.fordbarn.com/category/early-v8/" rel="category tag">
         Early V8
        </a>
       </div>
       <div class="post-text entry">
        <p>
         <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-850" height="282" sizes="(max-width: 569px) 100vw, 569px" src="https://www.fordbarn.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/TFB.jpg" srcset="https://www.fordbarn.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/TFB.jpg 569w, https://www.fordbarn.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/01/TFB-300x148.jpg 300w" width="569"/>
        </p>
        <p>
         What you see before you is something so incredibly rare that there doesn’t seem to be anyone alive that can actually tell us why it was made or for whom. It’s a 1940 Ford cutaway chassis and running gear that is owned and was restored by Rick Rennebohm of Whidbey Island, WA. Rick bought the enigmatic chassis on eBay in 2009 without knowing much about its history. He just thought it was cool… and had to have it.
