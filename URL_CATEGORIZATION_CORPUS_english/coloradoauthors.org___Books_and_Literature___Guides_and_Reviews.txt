       <h1 class="post-title">
        <a href="https://coloradoauthors.org/2018/06/03/beyond-the-camps-by-denny-dressman-and-book-events/">
         Beyond the Camps, by Denny Dressman (and book events)
        </a>
       </h1>
       <div class="c_link">
        <span class="w100 fleft pad_bottom20">
         Posted On June 3, 2018 by
         <a href="https://coloradoauthors.org/author/michelecookingwithmichele-com/" rel="author" title="Posts by CAL Website Administrator">
          CAL Website Administrator
         </a>
        </span>
       </div>
       <div class="description">
        <p>
         <a href="https://coloradoauthors.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/beyond-the-camps.jpg">
          <img alt="" class="alignleft size-medium wp-image-11057" height="300" src="https://coloradoauthors.org/wp-content/uploads/2018/06/beyond-the-camps-203x300.jpg" width="203"/>
         </a>
         Outgoing CAL President Denny Dressman has a new book, his ninth (and second written in collaboration with Dr. John Elliff). Published in April,
         <em>
          Beyond The Camps – From Japanese-American Internment Nightmare to ‘American Dream’
         </em>
         looks at the lives led by Japanese Americans who went through internment in the U.S. during WWII, or their children. As the jacket copy states, most books and articles about internment focus on how bad or how wrong it was. This looks at what internees made of their lives after that ordeal. Jim Kroll, manager of the Denver Public Library’s Western History Department and recipient of the 2017 Author Advocate Award, calls
         <em>
          Beyond The Camps
         </em>
         “an achievement in narrative history.”
        </p>
        <p>
         An interesting note about the book is that all of the internment subjects featured in the book have some Colorado tie–even though they were in nine of the 10 camps built in seven states. This is in large part because of Colorado Gov. Ralph Carr and the position he took regarding internees compared to governors of other states.
        </p>
        <p>
         <em>
          Beyond The Camps
         </em>
         will be featured at a program at The Denver Press Club Wednesday, June 13, starting at 6 p.m. (open to CAL members and their guests); also at a book-signing at Barnes and Noble on Colorado Boulevard Saturday, June 16, from 11 a.m. to 1 p.m.; and will be on sale at the 46th annual Cherry Blossom Festival at Sakura Square downtown June 23-24, starting at 11 a.m. each day. Available at
         <a href="http://www.comservbooks.com">
          www.comservbooks.com
         </a>
         . Hardback, $29.95
        </p>

       <h1 class="post-title">
        <a href="https://coloradoauthors.org/2018/05/30/grief-memoir-by-cal-author-wins-nautilus-silver-book-award/">
         Grief Memoir by CAL Author Wins Nautilus Silver Book Award
        </a>
       </h1>
       <div class="c_link">
        <span class="w100 fleft pad_bottom20">
         Posted On May 30, 2018 by
         <a href="https://coloradoauthors.org/author/michelecookingwithmichele-com/" rel="author" title="Posts by CAL Website Administrator">
          CAL Website Administrator
         </a>
        </span>
       </div>
       <div class="description">
        <p>
         <a href="https://coloradoauthors.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/the-secret-life-of-grief.jpg">
          <img alt="" class="alignleft size-full wp-image-9003" height="214" src="https://coloradoauthors.org/wp-content/uploads/2017/02/the-secret-life-of-grief.jpg" width="138"/>
         </a>
         <em>
          The Secret Life of Grief: A Memoir
         </em>
         by Tanja Pajevic has been named a silver winner in the Nautilus Book Awards, an award celebrating books that inspire and connect people’s lives as individuals, communities and global citizens. After her mother’s death, Tanja Pajevic explored what it meant to grieve consciously in a society that barely acknowledges grief. As the weeks passed, Pajevic—a first-generation, Serbian-American woman—delved into her anger, rage and sorrow, as well as explored the complicated issue of forgiveness. Along the way, she explored the rise and fall of communal mourning in the United States, as well as its roots in the current medical model. Pajevic also examined ingrained family patterns she was ready to release—all while raising two young children and dealing with a husband facing his own challenges. Throughout, she reassessed and reconfigured her life, reconnected with her true self and found redemption and healing, as well as joy.
        </p>
        <p>
         Additional Nautilus winners include Denverite Helen Thorpe, Brene Brown, Tara Brach, Thich Nhat Hanh, J.D. Vance and Peter Wohlleben. Now completing its 20th year, Nautilus Book Awards “recognizes books that transcend barriers of culture, gender, race, and class, and promote conscious living and green values, spiritual growth, wellness and vitality, and positive social change.” Winners were determined by a unique three-tier system of judging books against a carefully prepared list of notable characteristics, by three teams of highly qualified, experienced reviewers including editors, writers, librarians, schoolteachers, psychologists and bookstore owners.
        </p>
