       <h2>
        <a href="http://jilltxt.net/?p=4727" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to The god trick and the idea of infinite, technological vision">
         The god trick and the idea of infinite, technological vision
        </a>
       </h2>
       <p>
        When I was at the
        <a href="https://indvil.org">
         INDVIL
        </a>
        workshop about data visualisation on Lesbos a couple of weeks ago, everybody kept citing Donna Haraway. “It’s the ‘god trick’ again,” they’d say, referring to Haraway’s 1988 paper on
        <a href="https://www.jstor.org/stable/3178066">
         Situated Knowledges
        </a>
        . In it, she uses vision as a metaphor for the way science has tended to imagine knowledge about the world.
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         The eyes have been used to signify a perverse capacity–honed to perfection in the history of science tied to militarism, capitalism, colonialism, and male supremacy–to distance the knowing subject from everybody and everything in the interests of unfettered power. (p. 581)
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        Haraway connects this to what I would call machine vision (“..satellite surveillance systems, home and office video display terminals, cameras for every purpose from filming the mucous membrane lining the gut cavity of a marine worm living in the vent gases on a fault between continental plates to mapping a plantery hemisphere elsewhere in the solar system..”) and states that these technologies don’t just pretend to be all-seeing, objective and complete, they also make this seem ordinary, part of everyday life:
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         Vision in this technological feast becomes unregulated gluttony; all seems not just mythically about the god trick of seeing everything from nowhere, but to have put the myth into ordinary practice. (p. 581)
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        Of course, “that view of infinite vision is an illusion, a god trick” (p. 582). But it’s not an illusion that we seem to have escaped since 1988. Google’s satellite maps, for instance, have that lovely feel of “seeing everything from nowhere.” I heard
        <a href="https://pureportal.coventry.ac.uk/en/persons/rob-tovey">
         Rob Tovey
        </a>
        present a fascinating paper about this at the Post Screen Festival in Lisbon a couple of years ago (“
        <a href="http://www.academia.edu/30257928/God_s_Eye_View_The_Satellite_Photography_of_Google">
         God’s Eye View: The Satellite Photography of Google
        </a>
        “, 2016), noting not only the “god trick,” but also the
        <a href="http://journals.sagepub.com/doi/full/10.1177/1470357217746028">
         mechanics
        </a>
        of how these images are created from multiple photographs using specific projection techniques rather than others. A map is far from objective.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="Section of a satellite map image of part of the city of Bergen, showing streets, trees, buildings, parked cars.        </p>
       <noscript>
        <img alt="Section of a satellite map image of part of the city of Bergen, showing streets, trees, buildings, parked cars. " class="alignnone size-full wp-image-4732" data-attachment-id="4732" data-comments-opened="1" data-image-description="" data-image-meta='{"aperture":"0","credit":"","camera":"","caption":"","created_timestamp":"0","copyright":"","focal_length":"0","iso":"0","shutter_speed":"0","title":"","orientation":"0"}' data-image-title="Google-Satellite-map-bergen" data-large-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/Google-Satellite-map-bergen-1024x548.png" data-medium-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/Google-Satellite-map-bergen-300x160.png" data-orig-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/Google-Satellite-map-bergen.png" data-orig-size="2476,1324" data-permalink="http://jilltxt.net/?attachment_id=4732" height="1324" sizes="(max-width: 2476px) 100vw, 2476px" src="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/Google-Satellite-map-bergen.png" srcset="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/Google-Satellite-map-bergen.png 2476w, http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/Google-Satellite-map-bergen-300x160.png 300w, http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/Google-Satellite-map-bergen-768x411.png 768w, http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/Google-Satellite-map-bergen-1024x548.png 1024w" width="2476"/>
       </noscript>
       <p>
        Haraway’s conclusion is that the only way of achieving any kind of objectivity in science, and for her this is a feminist point, though valid for all science, is by admitting that knowledge is partial and situated. Perhaps something like a 360 degree photosphere, taken by an individual like myself using Google’s Street View app on my phone, could be classified as an example of a visual position that is partial?
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="https://goo.gl/maps/jQthwiGCZzN2">
         <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-4728" data-attachment-id="4728" data-comments-opened="1" data-image-description="" data-image-meta='{"aperture":"0","credit":"","camera":"","caption":"","created_timestamp":"0","copyright":"","focal_length":"0","iso":"0","shutter_speed":"0","title":"","orientation":"0"}' data-image-title="GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-1" data-large-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-1-1024x642.png" data-lazy-sizes="(max-width: 2190px) 100vw, 2190px" data-lazy-src="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-1.png" data-lazy-srcset="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-1.png 2190w, http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-1-300x188.png 300w, http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-1-768x481.png 768w, http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-1-1024x642.png 1024w" data-medium-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-1-300x188.png" data-orig-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-1.png" data-orig-size="2190,1372" data-permalink="http://jilltxt.net/?attachment_id=4728" height="1372" src="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/plugins/jetpack/modules/lazy-images/images/1x1.trans.gif" width="2190"/>
         <noscript>
          <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-4728" data-attachment-id="4728" data-comments-opened="1" data-image-description="" data-image-meta='{"aperture":"0","credit":"","camera":"","caption":"","created_timestamp":"0","copyright":"","focal_length":"0","iso":"0","shutter_speed":"0","title":"","orientation":"0"}' data-image-title="GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-1" data-large-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-1-1024x642.png" data-medium-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-1-300x188.png" data-orig-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-1.png" data-orig-size="2190,1372" data-permalink="http://jilltxt.net/?attachment_id=4728" height="1372" sizes="(max-width: 2190px) 100vw, 2190px" src="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-1.png" srcset="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-1.png 2190w, http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-1-300x188.png 300w, http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-1-768x481.png 768w, http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-1-1024x642.png 1024w" width="2190"/>
         </noscript>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        If you click through that screenshot to see the way Google displays my photo, you’ll see you can drag it around to see everything I saw in every direction.
       </p>
       <p>
        Well, almost everything I saw. If you look down, you won’t see my feet.
       </p>
       <p>
        <a href="https://goo.gl/maps/jQthwiGCZzN2">
         <img alt="A photograph of a puddle in a muddy path. A narrow google map below it shows where the photo was taken." class="alignnone wp-image-4730 size-full" data-attachment-id="4730" data-comments-opened="1" data-image-description="" data-image-meta='{"aperture":"0","credit":"","camera":"","caption":"","created_timestamp":"0","copyright":"","focal_length":"0","iso":"0","shutter_speed":"0","title":"","orientation":"0"}' data-image-title="GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-3" data-large-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-3-1024x638.png" data-lazy-sizes="(max-width: 2194px) 100vw, 2194px" data-lazy-src="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-3.png" data-lazy-srcset="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-3.png 2194w, http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-3-300x187.png 300w, http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-3-768x478.png 768w, http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-3-1024x638.png 1024w" data-medium-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-3-300x187.png" data-orig-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/GoogleStreetViewPhotosphereofSolheimsvannet-3.png" data-orig-size="2194,1366" data-permalink="http://jilltxt.net/?attachment_id=4730" height="1366" src="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/plugins/jetpack/modules/lazy-images/images/1x1.trans.gif" width="2194"/>
         <noscript>
          <img alt="A photograph of a puddle in a muddy path.          </noscript>
        </a>
       </p>
       <p>
        Google edited them out.
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         The knowing self is partial in all its guises, never finished, whole, simply there and original; it is always constructed and stitched together imperfectly, and
         <em>
          therefore
         </em>
         able to join with another, to see together without claiming to be another. Here is the promise of objectivity: a scientific knower seeks the subject position, not of identity, but of objectivity, that is, partial connection.
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        Those 360 images are certainly constructed and stitched together, and have a more specific standpoint or position, maybe even an implicit subject position from which you see. The glitches in the stitching together of the images remind us that they are imperfect, partial representations.
       </p>
       <p>
        And yet the human is edited out.
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         Knowledge from the point of view of the unmarked is truly fantastic, distorted, and irrational. The only position from which objectivity could not possibly be practiced and honoured is the standpoint of the master, the Man, the One God, whose Eye produces, appropriates, and orders all difference. (..) Positioning is, therefore, the key practice in grounding knowledge organised around the imagery of vision. (p. 587)
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        I wonder whether today it is Google and technology, rather than the patriarchal male master, whose “Eye produces, appropriates, and orders all difference.”
       </p>
       <p>
        And of course, as the scholars at our workshop about data visualisation pointed out, data visualisations are another way in which information is presented as objective, as seen from a disembodied, neutral viewpoint. The kind of viewpoint that doesn’t exist.
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         Above all, rational knowledge does not pretend to disengagement: to be from everywhere and so nowhere, to be free from interpretation, from being represented, to be fully self-contained or fully formalisable. (p. 590)
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        And of course, data visualisations tend to show the big picture. It’s nicely organised, you can see the patterns, and there are no “troubling details,” as Johanna Drucker puts it in
        <em>
         Graphesis
        </em>
        (2014).

       <h2>
        <a href="http://jilltxt.net/?p=4717" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to Skal samfunnet styres av algoritmer? To foredrag og syv bøker">
         Skal samfunnet styres av algoritmer? To foredrag og syv bøker
        </a>
       </h2>
       <p>
        <em>
         [English summary: info about two recent talks I gave about algorithmic bias in society]
        </em>
       </p>
       <p>
        Algoritmer, stordata og maskinlæring får mer og mer å si for samfunnet vårt, og brukes snart i alle samfunnsområder: i skolen, rettsstaten, politiet, helsevesenet og mer. Vi trenger mer kunnskap og offentlig debatt om dette temaet, og jeg har vært glad for å kunne holde to foredrag om det den siste måneden, en lang og en kort – og her kan du se videoene om du vil!
       </p>
       <p>
        Sist onsdag holdt jeg et innlegg på Bergen offentlige bibliotek med fullsatt sal og en av de beste påfølgende debattene jeg har vært med på. Ikke bare IT-folk og studenter og Facebookbrukere, men også helsearbeidere, barnehagelærere og psykiatere fortalte om hvordan algoritmer brukes i deres yrker, og hva slags tvil og bekymringer de og deres kollegaer har. Innlegget ble streamet og du kan se hele her:
       </p>
       <p>
        <span class="embed-youtube" style="text-align:center; display: block;">
         <iframe allowfullscreen="true" class="youtube-player" height="284" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/uFsjcnf5x_A?version=3&amp;rel=1&amp;fs=1&amp;autohide=2&amp;showsearch=0&amp;showinfo=1&amp;iv_load_policy=1&amp;wmode=transparent" style="border:0;" type="text/html" width="450">
         </iframe>
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        I mars var jeg invitert til å holde et 10-minutters innlegg for 600 kommunepolitikere på
        <a href="http://www.ks.no/arrangementer/kommunalpolitisk-toppmote-2018/">
         Kommunalpolitisk toppmøte
        </a>
        , som hadde digitalt utenforskap som tema. Jeg argumenterte for at digital utenforskap handler om mer enn bare tilgang til nettet, og at vi også må tenke på hvordan samfunnsgrupper og individer kan ekskluderes eller diskrimineres gjennom algoritmisk styring.
       </p>
       <p>
        <span class="embed-youtube" style="text-align:center; display: block;">
         <iframe allowfullscreen="true" class="youtube-player" height="284" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/f9KUedc6j3M?version=3&amp;rel=1&amp;fs=1&amp;autohide=2&amp;showsearch=0&amp;showinfo=1&amp;iv_load_policy=1&amp;start=265&amp;wmode=transparent" style="border:0;" type="text/html" width="450">
         </iframe>
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        Det er kommet ut en rekke gode bøker om dette temaet de siste  månedene – flest fra USA, hvor utviklingen er kommet lenger enn her. Om du kjenner til flere bøker, særlig norske eller europeiske, så håper jeg du legger igjen tips i kommentarfeltet!
       </p>
       <h3>
        Norske tekster:
       </h3>
       <h4>
        Bår Stenvik:
        <a href="http://www.tiden.no/skjonnlitteratur/informasjonen">
         Informasjonen
        </a>
        (roman). Tiden, 2018
       </h4>
       <p>
        Denne romanen skal jeg lese straks jeg er ferdig med Ada Palmers
        <a href="https://www.goodreads.com/book/show/33517544-the-will-to-battle">
         fremtidssamfunn
        </a>
        : “
        <em>
         Informasjonen
        </em>
        er et kjærlighetsdrama mellom en mann, en kvinne og et dataprogram.”
       </p>
       <h4>
        Datatilsynets rapport
        <a href="https://www.datatilsynet.no/aktuelt/aktuelle-nyheter-2018/hva-vet-de-om-deg/">
         Hva vet de om deg
        </a>
        ? 2018.
       </h4>
       <p>
        En rapport som viser hva fire vanlige, norske virksomheter lagrer om deg som kunde.
       </p>
       <h3>
        Amerikanske bøker:
       </h3>
       <h4>
        Virginia Eubanks:
        <a href="https://virginia-eubanks.com/books/">
         <em>
          Automating Inequality – How High-Tech Tools Profile, Police, and Punish the Poor
         </em>
         .
        </a>
        Macmillan, 2018.
       </h4>
       <p>
        Denne boken gjenforteller tre historier som viser hvordan automatisering av tildeling av velferdstjenester kan slå veldig feil. Argumentet er at algoritmisk styring slik den har vært brukt gjenskaper forskjeller. Lytt til et
        <a href="https://www.npr.org/sections/alltechconsidered/2018/02/19/586387119/automating-inequality-algorithms-in-public-services-often-fail-the-most-vulnerab">
         radio-intervju om boken
        </a>
        eller
        <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Avxm7JYjk8M">
         se henne presentere den selv
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <h4>
        Safiya Umoja Noble:
        <a href="https://nyupress.org/books/9781479837243/">
         Algorithms of Oppression: How Search Engines Reinforce Racism
        </a>
        . NYU Press, 2018.
       </h4>
       <p>
        Første gangen Noble googlet “black girls” for å finne aktiviteter til ti-åringen sin, fikk hun bare porno som treff. Boken starter med dette eksempelet, men går mye lenger i å vise hvordan google og andre søkemotorer har dyptgående problemer med rasisme. Se et kort
        <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iRVZozEEWlE">
         foredrag
        </a>
        hvor Noble presenterer boken sin.
       </p>
       <h4>
        Meredith Broussard:
        <em>
         <a href="https://mitpress.mit.edu/books/artificial-unintelligence">
          Artificial Unintelligence – How Computers Misunderstand the World
         </a>
        </em>
        . MIT Press, 2018.
       </h4>
       <p>
        Broussard er IT-utvikler og journalist, og i denne boken viser hun hvordan teknologi definitivt ikke løser alle problemer.
       </p>
       <h4>
        Andrew Guthrie Ferguson :
        <a href="https://nyupress.org/books/9781479892822/">
         The Rise of Big Data Policing – Surveillance, Race, and the Future of Law Enforcement
        </a>
        . NYU Press, 2017.
       </h4>
       <p>

        <a href="https://www.datatilsynet.no/aktuelt/aktuelle-nyheter-2018/datatilsynet-vil-mote-tolletaten-om-palantir/">

        </a>
        . I Danmark bruker politiet “predictive policing”. Bruk av stordata og algoritmer kan endre politiarbeid også i Norge – og da er det viktig å forstå hva det vil innebære.
       </p>
       <h4>
        Cathy O’Neil:
        <a href="https://www.penguin.co.uk/books/304513/weapons-of-math-destruction/">
         Weapons of Math Destruction: How Big Data Increases Inequality and Threatens Democracy
        </a>
        . Penguin, 2016.
       </h4>
       <p>
        Jeg var på et foredrag av O’Neil i fjor, og hun er en rasende god taler. Her kan du bl.a. se en kortere
        <a href="https://www.ted.com/talks/cathy_o_neil_the_era_of_blind_faith_in_big_data_must_end">
         TED-talk
        </a>
        hun har gjort om temaet.

        </a>
       </h2>
       <figure class="graf graf--figure graf-after--h4" id="c62e">
        <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder is-locked">
         <div class="aspectRatioPlaceholder-fill">

          <noscript>
           <img class="progressiveMedia-image js-progressiveMedia-image" data-src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1600/1*HIf8YAejTa8OXNvuPr0f2A.png" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1600/1*HIf8YAejTa8OXNvuPr0f2A.png"/>
          </noscript>

        </div>

       <p class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure" id="ffbf">
        Did you know the iPhone will search your photos for brassieres and breasts, but not for shoulders, knees and toes? Or boxers and underpants either for that matter. “Brassiere” seems to be a codeword for cleavage and tits.
       </p>
       <p class="graf graf--p graf-after--p" id="ea84">
        I discovered this not by suddenly wanting to see photos of bras, but because I did a reverse image search for a sketch of a pregnant woman’s belly selfie to see if the sketch had anonymised it sufficently that Google wouldn’t find the original. Lo and behold, all the “related images” were of porn stars with huge tits and ads of busty women trying to sell bras, which surprised me, given that the original was of a woman with a singlet pulled up to show her pregnant belly. I would have expected person, woman, pregnant, selfie, belly, bare arms to show up as descriptions, but brassiere? Was that really the most salient feature of the image? Apparently so, to Google.
       </p>
       <p class="graf graf--p graf-after--p" id="201c">
        Usefully, one of the text hits for the image was to
        <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="https://www.theverge.com/2017/10/30/16575600/apple-iphone-photos-brassiere-machine-learning" href="https://www.theverge.com/2017/10/30/16575600/apple-iphone-photos-brassiere-machine-learning" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">
         this article explaining with horror that Apple has “brassiere” as a search term for your photos
        </a>
        . Well, clearly Google does too.
       </p>
       <p class="graf graf--p graf-after--p" id="3ee3">
        I promptly did a search in the photos on my iPhone, and was appalled to see a selfie I took one morning show up?—?I wasn’t wearing a bra, but a singlet, and the image is mostly of my face, neck and upper chest.
        <canvas class="progressiveMedia-canvas js-progressiveMedia-canvas" height="38" width="75">
        </canvas>
        <img class="progressiveMedia-image js-progressiveMedia-image" data-lazy-src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1600/1*yxUAT44u2qGdLs6MEI61bQ.jpeg" data-src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1600/1*yxUAT44u2qGdLs6MEI61bQ.jpeg" src="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/plugins/jetpack/modules/lazy-images/images/1x1.trans.gif"/>
       </p>
       <noscript>
        <img class="progressiveMedia-image js-progressiveMedia-image" data-src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1600/1*yxUAT44u2qGdLs6MEI61bQ.jpeg" src="https://cdn-images-1.medium.com/max/1600/1*yxUAT44u2qGdLs6MEI61bQ.jpeg"/>
       </noscript>
       <p class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure" id="ea21">
        Seriously? I suppose you might think that the main point of that image was the triangle of my singlet that could have been a bra, but really?
       </p>
       <p class="graf graf--p graf-after--p" id="2b95" style="text-align: left;">
        The other images are a screenshot of some Kim Kardashian thing I saved for some reason I don’t remember, and fittingly enough, in the middle there, is a video of part of
        <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://www.ericascourti.com" href="http://www.ericascourti.com/" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">
         Erica Scourti
        </a>
        ’s excellent video poem,
        <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
         Body Scan
        </em>
        , which is precisely about how commercial image recognition commodifies the human body. (Here is a paper I wrote
        <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="https://www.researchgate.net/publication/308791894_Machine_Vision_as_Viewed_Through_Art_Hostile_Other_or_Part_of_Ourselves" href="https://www.researchgate.net/publication/308791894_Machine_Vision_as_Viewed_Through_Art_Hostile_Other_or_Part_of_Ourselves" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">
         about
         <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">
          Body Scan
         </em>
        </a>
        )
        <canvas class="progressiveMedia-canvas js-progressiveMedia-canvas" height="43" width="75">
        </canvas>
       </p>

        <iframe allowfullscreen="" frameborder="0" height="253" mozallowfullscreen="" src="https://player.vimeo.com/video/111503640" webkitallowfullscreen="" width="450">
        </iframe>
       </div>
       <p class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure" id="02e7">
        The app Erica Scourti was using,
        <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="https://camfindapp.com" href="https://camfindapp.com/" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">
         CamFind
        </a>
        , is in some ways more nuanced than the iPhone’s image recognition, which has no idea how to look for a human hand or a knee or a foot. That’s because those words weren’t among the images the system was trained to recognise.
       </p>
       <p class="graf graf--p graf-after--p" id="2091">
        Yeah. Somebody decided to program the systems to look for breasts and bras, but not for knees or toes or backs. I wonder why.
       </p>

There’d be a precedent for it: the Lena image, which is the most commonly used test image for image compression algorithms, is a scan of a Playboy centrefold, so that naked shoulder actually leads to a naked body in a fake Wild West setting. (This image is one of the main cases discussed in
        <a class="markup--anchor markup--p-anchor" data-href="http://dylanmulvin.com" href="http://dylanmulvin.com/" rel="nofollow noopener" target="_blank">

        <em class="markup--em markup--p-em">

       <p class="graf graf--p graf-after--figure" id="3835">
        So why does this matter? It matters because these algorithms are organising our personal photos, memories we have captured of ourselves and of our loved ones. Those algorithms that create those cute video compilations of my photos, showing the kids’ growing up over the years, or all the smiley photos from our family holiday?—?they are also scanning my private photos for breasts and cleavage.
       </p>
       <p class="graf graf--p graf-after--p graf--trailing" id="378e">
        I really don’t like that my phone thinks the best way to describe my selfie is “brassiere”. I hate that. Image recognition needs to do something more than simply replicate a commercialised version of the male gaze.
       </p>

       <h2>
        <a href="http://jilltxt.net/?p=4693" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to My project on machine vision will be funded by the ERC!">
         My project on machine vision will be funded by the ERC!
        </a>
       </h2>
       <p>
        Amazing news today: my ERC Consolidator project is going to be funded! This is huge news: it’s a €2 million grant that will allow me to build a research team to work for five years to understand how machine vision affects our everyday understanding of ourselves and our world.
       </p>

       <p>
        Here is the short summary of what the project will do:
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         In the last decade, machine vision has become part of the everyday life of ordinary people. Smartphones have advanced image manipulation capabilities, social media use image recognition algorithms to sort and filter visual content, and games, narratives and art increasingly represent and use machine vision techniques such as facial recognition algorithms, eye-tracking and virtual reality.
        </p>
        <p>
         The ubiquity of machine vision in ordinary peoples’ lives marks a qualitative shift where once theoretical questions are now immediately relevant to the lived experience of ordinary people.
        </p>
        <p>
         MACHINE VISION will develop a theory of how everyday machine vision affects the way ordinary people understand themselves and their world through 1) analyses of digital art, games and narratives that use machine vision as theme or interface, and 2) ethnographic studies of users of consumer-grade machine vision apps in social media and personal communication. Three main research questions address 1) new kinds of agency and subjectivity; 2) visual data as malleable; 3) values and biases.
        </p>
        <p>
         MACHINE VISION fills a research gap on the cultural, aesthetic and ethical effects of machine vision. Current research on machine vision is skewed, with extensive computer science research and rapid development and adaptation of new technologies. Cultural research primarily focuses on systemic issues (e.g. surveillance) and professional use (e.g. scientific imaging). Aesthetic theories (e.g. in cinema theory) are valuable but mostly address 20th century technologies. Analyses of current technologies are fragmented and lack a cohesive theory or model.
        </p>
        <p>
         MACHINE VISION challenges existing research and develops new empirical analyses and a cohesive theory of everyday machine vision. This project is a needed leap in visual aesthetic research. MACHINE VISION will also impact technical R&amp;D on machine vision, enabling the design of technologies that are ethical, just and democratic.
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        The project is planned to begin in the second half of 2018, and will run until the middle of 2023. I’ll obviously post more as I find out more! For now, here’s a very succinct overview of the project, or you can take a look at this
        <a href="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/MachineVision-B1-Jill-Walker_Rettberg-ERC-CoG-2017.pdf">
         five-page summary of the project
        </a>
        , which was part of what I sent the ERC when I applied for the funding.
       </p>

       <h2>
        <a href="http://jilltxt.net/?p=4681" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to Hand signs on musical.ly = emoji for video">
         Hand signs on musical.ly = emoji for video
        </a>
       </h2>
       <a href="http://jilltxt.net/?p=4681">
        <img alt="drawings of a musical.ly user using hand signs" class="attachment-thumbnail size-thumbnail wp-post-image" data-attachment-id="4683" data-comments-opened="1" data-image-description="" data-image-meta='{"aperture":"0","credit":"","camera":"","caption":"","created_timestamp":"0","copyright":"","focal_length":"0","iso":"0","shutter_speed":"0","title":"","orientation":"0"}' data-image-title="10.1177_2056305117735751-fig2" data-large-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/10.1177_2056305117735751-fig2.gif" data-lazy-src="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/10.1177_2056305117735751-fig2-150x150.gif" data-medium-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/10.1177_2056305117735751-fig2-300x143.gif" data-orig-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/10.1177_2056305117735751-fig2.gif" data-orig-size="500,238" data-permalink="http://jilltxt.net/?attachment_id=4683" height="150" src="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/plugins/jetpack/modules/lazy-images/images/1x1.trans.gif" width="150"/>
       </a>
       <p>
        You know how we add emoji to texts?  In a face-to-face conversation, we don’t communicate simply with words, we also use facial expressions, tone of voice, gestures and body language, and sometimes touch. Emojis are pictograms that let us express some of these things in a textual medium. I think that as social media are becoming more video-based, we’re going to be seeing new kinds of pictograms that do the same work as emoji do in text, but that will work for video.
       </p>
       <p>
        I wrote a paper about this that was just published in
        <em>
         Social Media and Society
        </em>
        , which is an open access journal that has published some
        <em>
         really
        </em>
        fabulous papers in social media and internet studies. It’s called
        <a href="http://journals.sagepub.com/eprint/H5pkZ6a3xnbhREI4pa69/full">
         Hand Signs for Lip-syncing: The Emergence of a Gestural Language on Musical.ly as a Video-Based Equivalent to Emoji
        </a>
        . As you might have guessed, it argues that the hand signs lip-syncs on musical.ly use are doing what emoji do for text – but in video.
       </p>
       <p>
        Musical.ly is super popular with tweens and teens, but for those of you not in the know, here is an example of how the hand signs work on musical.ly.
       </p>
       <p>
        <span class="embed-youtube" style="text-align:center; display: block;">
         <iframe allowfullscreen="true" class="youtube-player" height="284" src="http://www.youtube.com/embed/tN_8tMWRufg?version=3&amp;rel=1&amp;fs=1&amp;autohide=2&amp;showsearch=0&amp;showinfo=1&amp;iv_load_policy=1&amp;wmode=transparent" style="border:0;" type="text/html" width="450">
         </iframe>
        </span>
       </p>
       <p>
        Musical.ly has become a pretty diverse video-sharing app, but it started as a lip-syncing app, and lip-syncing is still a major part of musical.ly. You record 15 second videos of yourself singing to a tune that you picked from the app’s library. You can add filters and special effects, but you can’t add text or your own voice.
       </p>
       <p>
        I think the fact that the
        <em>
         modalities are limited
        </em>
        – you can have video but no voice or text – leads to the development of a pictogram to make up for that limitation. That’s exactly what happened with text-based communication. Emoticons came early, and were standardised as emoji 🙂 after a while.
       </p>
       <p>
        Hand signs on musical.ly are pretty well defined. Looking at the videos or the tutorials on YouTube you’ll see that there are many that are quite standard. They’re usually made with just one hand, since the camera is held in the other hand, and often camera movements are important too, but more as a dance beat than as a unit of meaning. Here are the hand signs used by one lip-syncer to perform a 15 second sample from the song “Too Good” by Drake and Rihanna. First, she performs the words “I’m way too good to you,” using individual signs for “too”, “good”, “to” and “you”.
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="drawings of a musical.ly user using hand signs" class="wp-image-4682 size-full" data-attachment-id="4682" data-comments-opened="1" data-image-description="" data-image-meta='{"aperture":"0","credit":"","camera":"","caption":"","created_timestamp":"0","copyright":"","focal_length":"0","iso":"0","shutter_speed":"0","title":"","orientation":"0"}' data-image-title="10.1177_2056305117735751-fig1" data-large-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/10.1177_2056305117735751-fig1.gif" data-lazy-src="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/10.1177_2056305117735751-fig1.gif" data-medium-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/10.1177_2056305117735751-fig1-300x116.gif" data-orig-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/10.1177_2056305117735751-fig1.gif" data-orig-size="500,194" data-permalink="http://jilltxt.net/?attachment_id=4682" height="194" src="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/plugins/jetpack/modules/lazy-images/images/1x1.trans.gif" width="500"/>
       </p>

       <p>
        The next words are “You take my love for granted/I just don’t understand it.” This is harder to translate into signs word for word, so the lip-syncer interprets it in just three signs, pointing to indicate “you”, shaping her fingers into half of a heart for “love”, and pointing to her head for “understand”.
       </p>

       <p>
        In my paper I talk about how these hand signs are similar to the codified gestures used in early oratory and in theatre. These are called chironomics, and there are 17th and 18th century books explaining them in detail. The drawings are fascinating:
       </p>
       <p>
        <img alt="" class="alignnone size-full wp-image-4685" data-attachment-id="4685" data-comments-opened="1" data-image-description="" data-image-meta='{"aperture":"0","credit":"","camera":"","caption":"","created_timestamp":"0","copyright":"","focal_length":"0","iso":"0","shutter_speed":"0","title":"","orientation":"0"}' data-image-title="Fig6-Jbulwer-Chirologia.1644" data-large-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/Fig6-Jbulwer-Chirologia.1644-591x1024.jpg" data-lazy-sizes="(max-width: 800px) 100vw, 800px" data-lazy-src="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/Fig6-Jbulwer-Chirologia.1644.jpg" data-lazy-srcset="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/Fig6-Jbulwer-Chirologia.1644.jpg 800w, http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/Fig6-Jbulwer-Chirologia.1644-173x300.jpg 173w, http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/Fig6-Jbulwer-Chirologia.1644-768x1332.jpg 768w, http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/Fig6-Jbulwer-Chirologia.1644-591x1024.jpg 591w" data-medium-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/Fig6-Jbulwer-Chirologia.1644-173x300.jpg" data-orig-file="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/uploads/Fig6-Jbulwer-Chirologia.1644.jpg" data-orig-size="800,1387" data-permalink="http://jilltxt.net/?attachment_id=4685" height="1387" src="http://jilltxt.net/wp-content/plugins/jetpack/modules/lazy-images/images/1x1.trans.gif" width="800"/>
       </p>

       </noscript>
       <p>
        I think it’s important to think of the hand signs as
        <em>
         performance
        </em>
        , and in the theatrical or musical sense, not in the more generalised sense that Goffman used for a metaphor, where all social interaction is “performative”. No, these are literal performances, interpretations of a script for an audience. That’s important, because without realising that, we might think the hand signs are just redundant. After all, they’re just repeating the same things that are said in the lyrics of the song, but using signs. When we think of the signs as part of a performance, though, we realise that they’re an interpretation, not simply a repetition. Each muser uses hand signs slightly differently.
       </p>
       <p>
        And those hand signs aren’t easy. Just look at Baby Ariel, who is very popular on musical.ly, trying to teach her mother to  lip-sync. Or look at me in my Snapchat Research story trying to explain hand gestures on musical.ly just as I was starting to write the paper that was published this week:

         free to read for anyone
        </a>
        .

You can’t search for eating disorder-related hashtags, but there are other ways to find it, and if you look at that kind of content, the platforms offer you more, in quite jarring ways.

There are some ethical issues to work out here – but certainly the methodology of using the platform’ recommendation systems to find content is powerful.
       </p>

        Yesterday I dropped by the
        <a href="http://www.4sonline.org/meeting">
         4S conference: Society for Social Studies of Science
        </a>
        . It’s my first time at one of these conferences, but it’s big, with lots of parallel sessions and lots of people. I could only attend one day, but it’s great to get a taste of it.
        <a href="snapchat.com/add/jilltxt">
         I snapchatt
        </a>
        <a href="snapchat.com/add/jilltxt">
         ed bits of
        </a>
        <a href="snapchat.com/add/jilltxt">
         the sessions
        </a>
        I attended if you’re interested.

       <p>
        Going abroad on a sabbatical means dealing with a lot of practical details, and we’ve spent a lot of time just getting things organised. We’re actually living in Providence, which is an hour’s train ride away. Scott is affiliated with Brown, and we thought Providence might be a more livable place to be. It was pretty complicated just getting the kids registered for school – they needed extra vaccinations, since Norway has a different schedule, and they had to have a language test and then they weren’t assigned to the school three blocks from our house but will be bussed to a school across town. School doesn’t even start until September 5, so Scott and I are still taking turns spending time with the kids and doing work. We’re also trying to figure out how to organize child care for the late afternoon and early evening seminars and talks that seem to be standard in the US. Why does so little happen during normal work hours? Or, to be more precise, during the hours of the day when kids are in school? I’m very happy that Microsoft Research at least seems to schedule their meetings for the day time, and a few events at MIT are during the day. I suppose it allows people who are working elsewhere to attend, which is good, but it makes it hard for parents.

        Notes of interest about visiting the US embassy:
       </p>
       <ol>
        <li>
         They’ll store your phone and other small items in a box at the gate, but no large items or laptops.
        </li>
        <li>
         There are no clocks on the walls of the waiting room. Rows of chairs face the counters where the embassy employees take your paperwork and then call you up for your interview.

         They only let you bring your paperwork with you, nothing else. It was a two hour wait. There is no reading material provided except some children’s books.        </noscript>
       <p>
        The award has been given to a long and impressive list of academic bloggers. This is the first year it has been opened up to other forms of social media knowledge sharing, and I am honored to be the first award-winner to win for something other than blogging. Yay!
       </p>
       <blockquote>
        <p>
         The John Lovas Award is sponsored by Kairos in recognition and remembrance of John Lovas’s contributions to the legitimation of academic knowledge­sharing using the emerging tools of Web publishing, from blogging, to newsletters, to social media. Each year the award underscores the valuable contributions that such knowledge-creation and community-building have made to the discipline by recognizing a person or project whose active, sustained engagement with topics in rhetoric, composition, or computers and writing using emerging communication tools best exemplifies John’s model of a public intellectual.
        </p>
       </blockquote>
       <p>
        John Lovas was an influential early scholarly blogger, especially important within the fields of composition and rhetoric. I’ve been rereading some of his blog posts, and note that he experimented with visual argumentation in his blog, something that was quite unusual at the time, because it was more complicated to get images off cameras and onto the web than now, and bandwidth was limited too so images had to be carefully compressed in a photo editor so they would load before viewers got bored. So I like to think that John Lovas would have appreciated the combination of visual and textual communication about research that and other academics on Snapchat are exploring.
       </p>
       <p>
        Here is an archive of some of my Snapchat Research Stories – they are better on snapchat, add me on Snapchat to see them live – I’m jilltxt. Thank you so much for this recognition – I really wish I could have been at the conference.

       <h2>
        <a href="http://jilltxt.net/?p=4593" rel="bookmark" title="Permalink to Snapchat erases protests and diversity in its White Australia version of Australia Day">
         Snapchat erases protests and diversity in its White Australia version of Australia Day
        </a>
       </h2>
       <p>
        Snapchat’s live stories usually present the world in a way that emphasises diversity, tolerance and respect for different races, religions and sexualities. But sometimes they fail miserably – like in the Live Story about yesterday’s Australia Day, which is now available globally.
       </p>
       <p>
        Australia Day is celebrated on January 26, the day the first fleet arrived in Australia from Britain, and there is a strong movement to
        <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/australia-news/2017/jan/26/australia-day-attended-by-growing-controversy-and-calls-for-date-change">
         #changethedate
        </a>
        so that it celebrates Australia, and not the European invasion of indigenous Australian land. That movement is actually so strong that
        <a href="http://www.theage.com.au/victoria/invasion-day-melbourne-rally-draws-tens-of-thousands-of-protesters-20170126-gtz3ce.html">
         yesterday 50,000 people marched in Melbourne,
        </a>
        and thousands more around in other cities all over Australia. Here is a photo of the rally in Melbourne yesterday:

       <p>
        The first seven snaps are all of young, white people partying or at the pool. The last three are of fireworks.
       </p>
       <p>
        It’s a short Live Story – the Live Story from the Women’s March last weekend had 71 snaps, so was obviously of a different scope altogether. But what an unbelievably skewed version of Australia Day this shows. What a skewed and stereotypical version of the Australian population it shows. Especially seen in contrast to the coverage of the inauguration and the Women’s March last weekend, this is pretty astounding.
       </p>
       <p>
        I’ve previously noted that the Norwegian national day as seen on Snapchat appears to be nothing but young people partying in national costumes, which is not how the day looks to me. No doubt most of Snapchat’s portrayals of national, “exotic” festivals (at least exotic to young Americans) leave out a lot, or present things in a skewed manner. But at least Norway doesn’t have 50,000 people protesting the day that Snapchat somehow forgets to include in their story.
       </p>
       <p>
        It looks as though Triple J may have sponsored this Live Story, based on the emphasis on their Hottest 100 in the first snaps. Triple J has been the radio channel for youth and music for decades, but
        <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/music/2017/jan/27/triple-j-hottest-100-does-australias-youth-broadcaster-have-an-identity-crisis">
         their emphasis on a music countdown on what more and more people are calling Invasion Day rather than Australia Day may be ripe for change
        </a>
        .
       </p>
       <p>
        Another way in which Snapchat spreads very partial information about the politics of Australia Day is with their selfie filters and geofilters. I couldn’t access them in Norway, but the Live Story seems to have a couple of non-branded Australia Day geofilters, and some sponsored by Triple J. I imagine that Triple J actually sponsored the Live Story, or at least had significant influence on it, based on the number of geofilters they seem to have for the day, and their emphasis on the Hottest 100 on Australia Day. If that’s so, perhaps Snapchat’s US team, which seems to be pretty savvy about diversity in their own country, simply didn’t pay much attention. That would also explain why the narrative arc of the Live Story is pretty flat compared to many of the US Live Stories, which are more skilfully put together.
       </p>
