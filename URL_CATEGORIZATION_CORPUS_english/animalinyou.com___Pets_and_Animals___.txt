      <h2>
       The Four Fs of Animal Personalities
      </h2>
      <p>
       The fundamental aspects of animal personalities can be summarized with the four Fs:
       <strong>
        Feeding
       </strong>
       ,
       <strong>
        Fighting
       </strong>
       ,
       <strong>
        Fleeing
       </strong>
       and
       <strong>
        Sex
       </strong>
       .
      </p>
      <div style="padding-bottom:0px;">
       <p>
        <img alt="Bird feeding on a peanut" src="img/feeding.png" style="float:left;padding-right:10px;" title="The way animals find food is equivalent to the way human personalities earn a living."/>
        <strong>
         Feeding
        </strong>
        techniques translate into the careers that corresponding human personalities would choose. Bird personalities, for example, would prefer jobs that provide a great deal of freedom, while sheep personalities might flourish under the direction of a strong dog personality. Canine personalities like the
        <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/wolf">
         wolf
        </a>
        ,
        <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/dog">
         dog
        </a>
        and
        <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/fox">
         fox
        </a>
        instinctively work well with others while
        <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/bear">
         bear
        </a>
        personas chafe under the direction of authority.
       </p>
      </div>
      <div style="padding-bottom:10px;">
       <p>
        <img alt="Mountain lion growling" src="img/fighting.png" style="float:left;padding-right:10px;" title="Animals fight to control their environment."/>
        <strong>
         Fighting
        </strong>
        is equivalent to the way in which a person controls his or her environment. Carnivorous personalities are assertive and adventurous, while
        <a href="http://animalinyou.com/herbivore-personalities">
         herbivore personalities
        </a>
        tend to be passive and cautious.
       </p>
      </div>
      <div style="padding-bottom:13px;">
       <p>
        <img alt="Deer fleeing quickly" src="img/fleeing.png" style="float:left;padding-right:10px;" title="Some animals are more likely to flee from danger than others"/>
        <strong>
         Fleeing
        </strong>
        is how people protect themselves from each other. Herd animal personalities find refuge in the company of friends and family,
        <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/wolf">
         wolves
        </a>
        prefer tightly knit social groups, and mice personalities prefer to keep low profiles.
       </p>
      </div>
      <p>
       <img alt="Lions mating" src="img/sex.png" style="float:left;padding-right:10px;" title="Different animal personalities seek partners in different ways."/>
       <strong>

Young girls walk by pretending not to notice the watching boys displaying their own mating behavior, some of whom adopt masculine stances lounging around with their legs apart, or calling aggressively to the females, while others feign disinterest and use subtle body language to stake their claims.

      <h2>
       Why Do People's Personalities Resemble Animals?
      </h2>
      <img alt="Why Do People's Personalities Resemble Animals?" class="content_image" src="img/resemble.jpg"/>
      <style type="text/css">
       @media (min-width: 601px){.large_image{float:left;box-shadow: 0 4px 8px 0 rgba(0, 0, 0, 0.2), 0 6px 20px 0 rgba(0, 0, 0, 0.19); box-sizing:border-box;-moz-box-sizing:border-box;-webkit-box-sizing:border-box; margin-right:15px;margin-bottom:5px;}}
 @media (max-width: 600px){.large_image{float:left; width:50%; height:50%; box-shadow: 0 4px 8px 0 rgba(0, 0, 0, 0.2), 0 6px 20px 0 rgba(0, 0, 0, 0.19); box-sizing:border-box;-moz-box-sizing:border-box;-webkit-box-sizing:border-box; margin-right:15px;margin-bottom:5px;}}
      </style>
      <p class="quote">
       <em>
        Humans are an unusual and highly successful species that spends a great deal of time examining its higher motives and an equal amount of time ignoring its fundamental ones.
       </em>
       <span class="attrib" itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/Person">
        —
        <span itemprop="name">
         Desmond Morris
        </span>
       </span>
      </p>
      <p>
       Have you ever noticed how people tend to assume
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/about/animal-personalities">
        animal personalities
       </a>
       ? We talk of someone being a
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/bear">
        bear
       </a>
       of a man or someone acting like a
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/dog">
        dog
       </a>
       . People we don't care for are
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/weasel">
        weasels
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/sloth">
        sloths
       </a>
       , or sometimes
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/vulture">
        vultures
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       Why is there such a strong correlation between human and animal behavior? Are these connections coincidental, or is there a more prosaic explanation? A possible clue lies in nature's need for diversity.
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="Chimpanzee with human qualities" class="large_image" src="img/personality.jpg" title="Our closest relatives, the chimpanzees, offer insights into human behavior."/>
       Life consists of an astonishing variety of animal species, each with its own distinct behavior and physical properties. This diversity appears to be an essential component of life, for without it, an ecosystem cannot successfully maintain itself.
      </p>
      <p>
       At first glance, it would seem that nature would find the most efficient structure for an animal species and duplicate it en masse. However, an ecosystem with a single species cannot survive, and it takes a range of animal species to ensure a healthy habitat. The food chain — or, more accurately, the food web — requires the interaction of predators, prey, burrowing creatures, arboreal animals, and insects to remain stable. It is the interplay Introduction among these divergent species that gives rise to enduring and healthy populations.
      </p>
      <p>
       In a process known as parallel evolution, unrelated animal species separated by vast distances often evolve corresponding behavioral and physical characteristics in order to take advantage of available niches. For example, isolated from the mainland for thousands of years, the marsupial Tasmanian wolf, or thylacine, evolved numerous features similar to the North American
       <a href="wolf">
        wolf
       </a>
       . Although almost extinct from being hunted, its doglike body, coughing bark, and canine hunting behavior closely parallel that of wolf society, even though they have markedly different ancestries.
      </p>
      <p>
       A similar process seems to have taken place in human society. Our human species dominates the earth and is essentially a microcosm of nature. The attributes that provide stability in the animal world — aggression, passivity, stealth, skittishness, and so on — serve the same function in our own society. It is no coincidence, therefore, that we mimic these animal behaviors to better survive in a complex and competitive world.
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="Pig smiling, being true to itself" src="img/true.png" style="float:left;margin-right:10px;" title="A pig cannot help but live up to its pig nature."/>
      </p>
      <h3>
       To Thine Own Self Be True
      </h3>
      <p>
       Animals are themselves at all times. A pig always exhibits pure pig behavior and does nothing un-piglike. It has found a niche in the scheme of things and, when being a pig, is supremely happy. We humans are not always this true to our own natures. We struggle to find our niche in this multifaceted society and accept jobs or relationships ill-suited to our personalities. The resulting tensions contribute to our stress and unhappiness. The goal of this website is to identify your true animal spirit and give you a deeper insight into your authentic nature.
      </p>

      <h2>
       Animal Personalities in Culture
      </h2>
      <img alt="Animal Personalities in Culture" class="content_image" src="img/animal-farm.jpg"/>
      <p>
       <img alt="Chinese Zodiac" src="img/zodiac.png" style="float:left;margin-right:10px;" title="The Chinese Zodiac assigns different qualities to animals."/>
      </p>
      <h3>
       Chinese Zodiac
      </h3>
      <p>
       Recognition of the intimate connections between animals and humans dates back tens of thousands of years. The ancient Chinese developed a distinctive calendar system that described twelve animal personalities: Rat,
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/tiger">
        Tiger
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/rabbit">
        Rabbit
       </a>
       , Ox, Dragon,
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/rooster">
        Rooster
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/snake">
        Snake
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/horse">
        Horse
       </a>
       , Ram, Pig,
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/dog">
        Dog
       </a>
       , and Monkey. While the precise origins of these animals are unknown, Chinese astrologers considered them to be a reflection of the universe itself.
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="Native American Headdress on Horns" src="img/native.png" style="float:left;margin-right:10px;" title="Native American beliefs were heavily tied to concepts like spirit animals"/>
      </p>
      <h3>
       Native American Beliefs
      </h3>
      <p>
       Native Americans also recognized the intractable bond between humans and animals. For many young men, trekking into the forest to find their spirit animal was a rite of passage. Reverence for animals was almost universal among the Plains Indians, who dedicated a great deal of energy to paying tribute to their companions of the prairies. Spiritual beliefs were formed in large part by their close survival bonds with North American wildlife.
       <a href="bison">
        Bison
       </a>
       were not viewed simply as a food source but were recognized to be an essential element in the grand scheme.
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="Contemporary Literature book Animal Farm" src="img/book.png" style="float:left;margin-right:10px;" title="George Orwell's Animal Farm is one of the most famous animal allegories for human society."/>
      </p>
      <h3>
       Contemporary Literature
      </h3>
      <p>
       There is a great deal of writing found in contemporary sources that reflects our connections to animals with rich references in language. We refer to people as being bitchy,
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/fox">
        foxy
       </a>
       , slothful, and
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/wild-cat">
        catty
       </a>
       . People work like
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/horse">
        horses
       </a>
       , eat like pigs, and are as stubborn as mules.
       <span itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/Book">
        <span itemprop="author">
         George Orwell
        </span>
        's classic
        <span itemprop="name">
         Animal Farm
        </span>
       </span>
       explored this idea to its limits, and animal characters dominate comic pages.
       <span itemscope="" itemtype="http://schema.org/Book">
        The
        <span itemprop="award">
         Pulitzer Prize
        </span>
        winning
        <span itemprop="name">
         Maus
        </span>
        , by
        <span itemprop="author">
         Art Spiegelman
        </span>
       </span>
       , detailed his father's concentration camp experiences and used animal personalities to depict the drama of the Nazi atrocities; mice were used to portray victims, cats to represent the Germans, frogs the French, and pigs to describe the Poles.
      </p>
     </div>
     <div class="quiz_content clearfix">
      <h2>
       Relationships Between Animal Personalities
      </h2>
      <img alt="Relationships Between Animal Personalities" class="content_image" src="img/family350.jpg"/>
      <p>
       <img alt="Different insects on display" src="img/ecosystem.png" style="float:left;margin-right:10px;" title="Healthy ecosystems must contain many different species."/>
      </p>
      <p>
       For an ecosystem to remain stable, it must contain a wide diversity of species. It is also important that the ratio of these species is balanced, since an overabundance of predators could wreak havoc on the ecosystem. If predators were not present at all, then prey animals would overpopulate the environment causing overgrazing and disease. Interestingly, the ratio between predators and prey in nature seems to be mirrored in our own society. Larger animal personalities like
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/elephant">
        elephants
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/giraffe">
        giraffes
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/gorilla">
        gorillas
       </a>
       cannot be supported in large numbers since their bulky personalities put a disproportional stress on the social environment. Conversely, smaller personalities like
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/mouse">
        mice
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/otter">
        otters
       </a>
       ,
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/beaver">
        beavers
       </a>
       , and
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/sheep">
        sheep
       </a>
       are found in great numbers throughout the concrete jungle.
      </p>
      <p>
       The ratio of predators to prey in human society is maintained through a process of social pressure. Consider the artificial environment of prisons. In these overcrowded inhospitable conditions, someone who was previously a combative
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/warthog">
        warthog
       </a>
       might be unable to survive in a society dominated by
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/crocodile">
        crocodiles
       </a>
       and
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/lion">
        lions
       </a>
       . By backing away from his assertive stance and manifesting the more gregarious personality of a herbivore, this prisoner can seek the protection of the herd in order to survive. Carnivorous personalities are territorial and require more personal space than their herbivorous counterparts.
      </p>
      <p>
       <img src="img/kissing.png" style="float:left;margin-right:10px;margin-bottom:-2px;"/>
      </p>
      <h3>
       Courtship Rituals
      </h3>
      <p>
       From the subtle and coy techniques of the mouse and cottontail personalities, to the aggressive displays of the
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/lion">
        lion
       </a>
       and
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/wolf">
        wolf
       </a>
       , every species employs a unique mating strategy. These sorts of behaviors come naturally to us and a visit to a public park quickly reveals our animal personalities in action.
 Young girls walk by, often arm in arm, pretending not to notice the watching boys displaying their own mating behavior. Some boys adopt masculine stances, lounging around with their legs apart, calling aggressively to the females. Others will feign disinterest and use subtle body language and eye contact to stake their claims.
      </p>
      <p>
       A male
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/wolf">
        wolf
       </a>
       personality might pursue a female
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/sable">
        sable
       </a>
       by first surrounding himself with friends for moral support and then carefully and indirectly approaching the female. If comfortable with these advances, the female will display her interest by moving slowly away from the pack—taking care not to withdraw too far. As the male continues his hunt, she will turn and cautiously engage the group.
      </p>
      <p>
       This stalking approach is not for the male
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/weasel">
        weasel
       </a>
       . To seduce a female
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/warthog">
        warthog
       </a>
       personality he must first gain the trust of this cantankerous lady by hiding his true intentions with a small gift or an offer of friendship. If successfully swayed by these advances, the female warthog soon finds herself lured into an uncomfortably unbalanced relationship with the wily weasel.
      </p>
      <p>
       The rules that govern our mating behaviors are instinctive and deeply rooted. In a number of mammalian species, when males reach middle age they respond to a biological realization that they are no longer in their prime. Aging silverback
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/gorilla">
        gorillas
       </a>
       can no longer compete physically or sexually with the upcoming group of younger males, and in a biological panic, their reproductive urges trigger them to make one last fling at mating with younger, more fertile females. In humans, this manifests itself when a middle-aged man suddenly feels the urge to display his wealth by buying a fancy sports car, begins ignoring his wife, and starts a workout routine. This middle-life crisis is simply one example of our response to animal programming.
      </p>
      <p>
       <img alt="Attractive dogs playing" src="img/magnetism.png" style="float:left;margin-right:10px;margin-bottom:-2px;"/>
      </p>
      <h3>
       Animal Magnetism
      </h3>
      <p>
       Fortunately, we humans have the ability to control our own behaviors and are not complete slaves to these drives. Still, it is useful to understand our passions in the light of these powerful animal urges.
      </p>
      <p>
       When a
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/wild-cat">
        wildcat
       </a>
       and a
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/fox">
        fox
       </a>
       get together, they have superficial connections with a common range and nocturnal spirit. However, as a canine, the fox is a natural competitor of the cat and its natural friendliness grates against the cat's tendency to maintain its distance. Over time these tensions conspire to destroy the relationship. Likewise, if a
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/mouse">
        mouse
       </a>
       personality married a cat, power conflicts or spousal abuse would quickly destroy the union. So, all
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/about/animal-personalities">
        animal personalities
       </a>
       should avoid forming close relationships with their species' natural predator.
      </p>
      <p>
       However, this does not mean that all herbivorous personalities must avoid
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/carnivores">
        predators
       </a>
       . The meek
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/rabbit">
        cottontail rabbit
       </a>
       might even strike up a friendship with a powerful
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/lion">
        lion
       </a>
       , since lions are disinclined to waste energy chasing elusive, low-calorie
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/rabbit">
        rabbits
       </a>
       . Although marriage is out of the question, these friendships can be quite enduring. In exchange for companionship and loyalty, the predator provides resources and protection for the cottontail.
      </p>
      <p>
       Animal personalities tend to relate to species that share their ranges. The water personality of the
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/dolphin">
        dolphin
       </a>
       has much in common with the aquatic
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/sea-lion">
        sea lion
       </a>
       and the pastoral nature of the
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/sheep">
        sheep
       </a>
       makes for a compatible mate with the grazing
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/deef">
        deer
       </a>
       . Conversely, animal personalities that live in markedly different environments tend to avoid each other.
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/birds">
        Birds
       </a>
       choose to remain out of reach of the land mammal personalities and the unencumbered lives of the sea dwellers make them awkward mates for complex land creatures. On the other hand, the semi-aquatic
       <a href="http://animalinyou.com/animals/beaver">
        beaver
       </a>
       is capable of forming relationships with both water-going and land-based animal personalities.
      </p>
     </div>
     <div class="quiz_content clearfix">
      <h2>
       Why Ask What Animal Am I?
      </h2>
      <img alt="Why Ask What Animal Am I?" class="content_image" src="img/why-ask.jpg"/>
      <p>
       <img alt="The Question What Animal Am I" src="img/whatanimalami.png" style="float:left;margin-right:10px;"/>
      </p>
      <p>
       Personality quizzes have been around since the 1920s and were originally designed to help businesses select personnel – particularly in the US military.   The grandaddy of personality quizzes is the Myers Briggs Type Indicator Test, which requires the questionnaire to be sent to the company for analysis. Their tests are primarily purchased by large corporations.
      </p>
      <p>
       In 1995, Roy Feinson published the bestselling book, The Animal in You, based on the hypothesis that humans adopt a set of behavioral and personality traits to allow them to function in their particular environments. The book featured a nine question quiz and spawned a sequel, Animal Attraction (St. Martin’s Press).
      </p>
      <p>
       It turns out that people really want to know the answer to the question
       <strong>
        <a href="http://animalinyou.com/quiz">
         What Animal am I?
        </a>
       </strong>
       But why?
 The fact is, humans are just like animals – albeit with much larger brains -- and animals are just like people in that they exhibit virtually the same range of emotions. Animals can be happy, sad, depressed, excited, and afraid. They are capable of forming loving relationships, alliances and even go to war.
      </p>
      <p>
       So asking “what animal am I” is a natural extension of asking yourself “who am I?”...  the most basic question known to man.
      </p>
