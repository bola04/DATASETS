          <h2 class="item-title" itemprop="headline">
           <a href="/125-uncategorised/4342-share-it" itemprop="url">
            Share It On Social Media
           </a>
          </h2>
          <p>
           This poem will be short, of course, maybe a quick observation, a moment preserved, as in this
           <a href="https://www.poetryexpress.org/15-poems/6:-write-a-no-sentence-poem" title="no-sentence poem">
            no-sentence poem
           </a>
           by
           <a href="https://www.poetryfoundation.org/poets/ezra-pound" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank" title="Ezra Pound">
            Ezra Pound
           </a>
           :
          </p>
          <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
           In a Station of the Metro
          </p>
          <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
           The apparition of these faces in the crowd;
           <br/>
           Petals on a wet, black bough.
          </p>
          <p>
           Or maybe an
           <a href="https://www.poetryexpress.org/15-poems/1:-talk-to-animals-(and-stars)" title="apostrophe">
            apostrophe
           </a>
           like
           <a href="https://www.poetryfoundation.org/search?query=H.D." rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank" title="H.D.">
            H. D.
           </a>
           's "The Pool":
          </p>
          <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
           The Pool
          </p>
          <p style="padding-left: 30px;">
           Are you alive?
           <br/>
           I touch you.
           <br/>
           You quiver like a sea-fish.
           <br/>
           I cover you with my net.
           <br/>
           What are you—banded one?
          </p>

           </a>
          </p>

          <h2 class="item-title" itemprop="headline">
           <a href="/139-first-feature/125-it-must-be-spring" itemprop="url">
            It might as well be spring . . .
           </a>
          </h2>
          <p>
           or maybe it is, so many love poems keep appearing in the blogs. Love poems are some of the hardest poems to write, or so claimed W. H. Auden. Here are a few classics that have inspired poets and lovers over the years:
          </p>
          <ul>
           <li>
            <a href="http://poets.org/viewmedia.php/prmMID/16408" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">
             The Passionate Shepherd to His Love
            </a>
            by Christopher Marlowe
           </li>
           <li>
            <a href="http://poets.org/viewmedia.php/prmMID/16407" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">
             The Nymph's Reply to the Shepherd
            </a>
            by
            <span style="font-family: Bookman Old Style;">
            </span>
            Sir Walter Ralegh
           </li>
           <li>
            <span class="TITLE">
             <a href="http://poets.org/viewmedia.php/prmMID/15401" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">
              somewhere i have never travelled, gladly beyond
             </a>
            </span>
            by e. e. cummings
           </li>
           <li>
            <a href="http://poets.org/viewmedia.php/prmMID/19398" target="_top">
             Let me not to the marriage of true minds
            </a>
            by
            <span style="font-family: Bookman Old Style;">
            </span>
            William Shakespeare
           </li>
           <li>
            <a href="http://poets.org/viewmedia.php/prmMID/16162" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">
             The Definition of Love
            </a>
            by
            <span style="font-family: Bookman Old Style;">
            </span>
            Andrew Marvell
           </li>
           <li>
            <span class="TITLE">
             <a href="http://poets.org/viewmedia.php/prmMID/15420" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">
              What lips my lips have kissed, and where, and why
             </a>
            </span>
            by
            <span style="font-family: Bookman Old Style;">
            </span>
            <span class="TITLE">
             Edna St. Vincent Millay
            </span>
           </li>
           <li>
            <a href="http://poets.org/viewmedia.php/prmMID/19425" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">
             The Buried Life
            </a>
            by
            <span style="font-family: Bookman Old Style;">
            </span>
            Matthew Arnold
           </li>
           <li>
            <a href="http://poets.org/viewmedia.php/prmMID/19440" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">
             A Birthday
            </a>
            by
            <span style="font-family: Bookman Old Style;">
            </span>
            Christina Rossetti
           </li>
           <li>
            <a href="http://poets.org/viewmedia.php/prmMID/15384" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">
             How Do I Love Thee?
            </a>
            by
            <span style="font-family: Bookman Old Style;">
            </span>
            Elizabeth Barrett Browning
           </li>
           <li>
            <a href="http://poets.org/viewmedia.php/prmMID/19039" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">
             Wild Nights — Wild Nights!
            </a>
            by
            <span style="font-family: Bookman Old Style;">
            </span>
            Emily Dickinson
           </li>
           <li>
            <span class="TITLE">
             <a href="http://poets.org/viewmedia.php/prmMID/15526" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">
              When You Are Old
             </a>
            </span>
            by
            <span style="font-family: Bookman Old Style;">
            </span>
            W. B. Yeats
           </li>
           <li>
            <a href="http://poets.org/viewmedia.php/prmMID/18998" rel="noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">
             Credo
            </a>
            by
            <span style="font-family: Bookman Old Style;">
            </span>
            Matthew Roher
           </li>
          </ul>
          <p>
           A few suggestions: use concrete, specific images to
           <em>
            show
           </em>
           your feelings; avoid sing-song "roses are red" type rhyming; try to say something fresh and new that gives readers a new insight
           <span style="font-family: Bookman Old Style;">
            —
           </span>
           or even a laugh.
          </p>

           </h4>
           <p>
            <a href="/index.php/glossaryl#alliteration">
             Alliteration
            </a>
            refers to the repetition of initial sounds of words,
            <a href="/index.php/glossary#assonance">
             assonance
            </a>
            to the repetition of vowel sounds within a word,
            <a href="/index.php/glossary#consonance">
             consonance
            </a>
            to the repetition of consonant sounds within a word, and
            <a href="/index.php/glossary#rhyme">
             rhyme
            </a>
            to the repetition of both vowel and consonant sounds, especially, but not necessarily, at the end of a line.
