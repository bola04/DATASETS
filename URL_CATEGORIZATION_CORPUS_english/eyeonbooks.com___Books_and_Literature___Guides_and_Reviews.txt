      <p>
       Next spring marks thirty years of interviewing authors. I’ve done over 10,000 author interviews over that time, and I’ve collected a few signed books along the way.
      </p>
      <p>
       But now I find that it’s time to begin paring the collection down just a tad. In coming days, I’ll be offering a few selected books from
       <em>
        my
       </em>
       collection to add to
       <em>
        yours
       </em>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       <a href="http://www.eyeonbooks.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/ellroycover.jpg">
        <img alignleft="" alt="ellroycover" height="190" src="http://www.eyeonbooks.com/wp-content/uploads/2014/11/ellroycover-243x300.jpg" style="float: left; padding: 0 20px 10px 0; border: none;" width="154" wp-image-4879=""/>
       </a>
       The first book I’m parting with is an inscribed first edition of James Ellroy’s 1990 classic “L.A. Confidential.”
       <a href="http://www.ebay.com/itm/261655123028?ssPageName=STRK:MESCX:IT&amp;_trksid=p3984.m1554.l2649" target="_blank" title='eBay listing for Ellroy "LA Confidential"'>
        See the eBay listing here
       </a>
       .
      </p>
      <p>
       In the days ahead, I’ll be offering signed first editions by:
      </p>

      <p>
       She feels, she says, “a little bit vulnerable.”  For the first time, actress
       <a href="/janine-turner">
        Janine Turner
       </a>
       opens up about her private life in a book she calls “A Little Bit Vulnerable.”
      </p>
      <div style="float: left; padding: 0 10px 10px 0; border: none;">
       <img alt="" src="/graphics/turnerjanine.jpg" title="" width="150"/>
      </div>
      <p>
       In what her publisher calls a “breathtaking sweep of her half a century of living,”  Turner chronicles her journeys through the canyons of her life and how she sought horizons.
      </p>
      <div style="float: right; padding: 0 0 10px 10px; border: 1px;">
       <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1939447577?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=billthompseyeonb&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=1939447577" target="_window">
        <img alt="Janine  Turner" src="http://images.amazon.com/images/P/1939447577.01.MZZZZZZZ.jpg" title="Turner"/>
       </a>
       <img alt="Turner" border="0" height="1" src="http://www.assoc-amazon.com/e/ir?t=billthompseyeonb&amp;l=as2&amp;o=1&amp;a=1939447577" style="border:none !important; margin:0px !important;" width="1"/>
      </div>
      <p>
       She includes poetry, essays, opinion-editorials, radio interviews and letters, to reveal how she prevailed over heartbreak, alcoholism, and the death of her father.
      </p>

        <img alt="feather" height="16" src="http://www.eyeonbooks.com/wp-content/plugins/social-media-feather/synved-social/image/icon.png" style="display: inline;margin:0;padding:0;width:16px;height:16px;" width="16"/>
       </a>
      </p>
      <p>
       American history books often compress momentous events into capsules, summaries, one-line explanations.
      </p>
      <p>
       Such has often been the case with President Abraham Lincoln’s Emancipation Proclamation, the history-changing document that freed the slaves and changed the course of the Civil War.
      </p>
      <div style="float: left; padding: 0 10px 10px 0; border: none;">
       <img alt="" src="/graphics/brewstertodd.jpg" title="" width="200"/>
      </div>
      <p>
       Now historian and journalist Todd Brewster restores the detail that’s missing, and shows us the six critical months between the time Lincoln first spoke about his intention to free the slaves and the release of the Emancipation Proclamation.
      </p>
      <p>
       As Brewster describes it in his book “Lincoln’s Gamble” it was perhaps the most tumultuous six months of Lincoln’s presidency.  He fought with his generals, disappointed his cabinet, and sank into painful bouts of clinical depression.
      </p>
      <div style="float: right; padding: 0 0 10px 10px; border: 1px;">
       <a href="http://www.amazon.com/gp/product/1451693869?ie=UTF8&amp;tag=billthompseyeonb&amp;linkCode=as2&amp;camp=1789&amp;creative=390957&amp;creativeASIN=1451693869" target="_window">
        <img alt="Todd  Brewster" src="http://images.amazon.com/images/P/1451693869.01.MZZZZZZZ.jpg" title="Brewster"/>
       </a>
       <img alt="Brewster" border="0" height="1" src="http://www.assoc-amazon.com/e/ir?t=billthompseyeonb&amp;l=as2&amp;o=1&amp;a=1451693869" style="border:none !important; margin:0px !important;" width="1"/>
      </div>
      <p>
       And perhaps most surprising, he had to convince himself that emancipation was, indeed, the right course of action.

      <p>
       We know the power of prayer, to heal and comfort.
      </p>
      <p>
       But what if someone wanted to use that power to afflict — and kill?
      </p>
      <div style="float: right; padding: 0 0 10px 10px; border: 1px;">
       <img alt="Philip  Kerr" border="1" src="http://www.eyeonbooks.com/wp-content/gallery/authors/thumbs/thumbs_kerrphilip.jpg"/>
      </div>
      <p>
       <a href="/philip-kerr">
        Philip Kerr
       </a>
       ‘s new psychological thriller “Prayer” poses that uncomfortable question.
      </p>
      <p>
       Gil Martins, an agent with the FBI’s Domestic Terrorism Unit in Houston, confronts the violence generated by extremism every day.  But even he is not fully prepared for what he encounters when confronted with evidence of a serial killer who’s calling on the very power of God to dispatch well-known atheists.
      </p>
