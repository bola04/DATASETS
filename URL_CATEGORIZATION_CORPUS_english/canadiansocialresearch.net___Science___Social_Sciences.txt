         <b>
          <i>
           Ten things to know about Carey Dobersteins 
             book on homelessness governance
           <br/>
          </i>
         </b>
         <a href="http://behindthenumbers.ca/2018/06/06/ten-things-to-know-about-carey-dobersteins-book-on-homelessness-governance/">
          http://behindthenumbers.ca/2018/06/06/ten-things-to-know-about-carey-dobersteins-book-on-homelessness-governance/
         </a>
         <br/>
         By Nick Falvo
         <br/>
         June 6, 2018
         <br/>
         Carey Doberstein, a professor at the University of British Columbias 
             Okanagan campus, has written a book about homelessness governance 
             in Canada. The books focus is the way homelessness funding is 
             targeted and allocated in three cities: Vancouver, Calgary and Toronto. 
             It focuses on the 1995-2015 period.
        </p>
        <p align="left">
         Here are 10 things to know about the book::
         <br/>
         [ Click the link above to read more on each of the ten things to know...]
        </p>
        <p align="left">
         1. There are some major differences in homelessness 
             across Vancouver, Calgary and Toronto.
         <br/>
         2. One such difference pertains to policy and governance.
         <br/>
         3. Better decisions pertaining to homelessness policy and governance 
             are made when multiple stakeholders are involved in decision-making.
         <br/>
         <a href="http://behindthenumbers.ca/2018/06/06/ten-things-to-know-about-carey-dobersteins-book-on-homelessness-governance/">
         </a>
         4. 
             Multiple stakeholders play important roles in such decision-making 
             in both Vancouver and Calgary, more so than in Toronto.
         <br/>
         5. Toronto may be turning a corner.
         <br/>
         6. Government plays a crucial role as a funder of homelessness programming, 
             but government officials have a limited line of sight
         <br/>
         7. Government officials can be risk averse.
         <br/>
         8. The book argues that Vancouver and Torontos municipal governments 
             have been more directly involved in housing and homelessness than 
             Calgarys.
         <br/>
         9. The book could have done a better job of outlining the various 
             funding streams for homelessness programs across the country
         <br/>
         10. The book contains a few factual errors, though none of them undermine 
             the books main arguments.
        </p>
        <p align="left">
         <b>
          Source:
          <br/>
          Behind the Nunmbers
         </b>
         <br/>
         <a href="http://behindthenumbers.ca/">
          http://behindthenumbers.ca/
         </a>
         <br/>
         (Blog of the Canadian Centre for Policy Alternatives)
        </p>
